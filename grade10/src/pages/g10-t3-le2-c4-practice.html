A student's daily streaming time \(X\) (minutes) is modeled by

\[X\sim \text{Uniform}(2,14).\]

↑

  1. Find \(E[X]\).

  2. Find \(\mathrm{Var}(X)\) and the standard deviation.

  3. Write the PDF of \(X\).

  4. Draw the graph of the PDF, indicate \(E[X]\), and draw an interval of one standard deviation around \(E[X]\).

  5. Calculate the probability of the highlighted interval.

  6. Compare the probabilities of intervals of the same length around \(4\) and around \(12\). What is the difference between these probabilities?

↑

For \(X\sim\text{Uniform}(a,b)\):

\[E[X]=\frac{a+b}{2},\qquad \mathrm{Var}(X)=\frac{(b-a)^2}{12}.\]

a)

With \(a=2\) and \(b=14\),

\[E[X]=\frac{2+14}{2}=8.\]

b)

Using the same endpoints,

\[\mathrm{Var}(X)=\frac{(14-2)^2}{12}=\frac{144}{12}=12,\]
so
\[\sigma=\sqrt{12}=2\sqrt{3}\approx 3.46.\]

c)

Since \(X\sim\text{Uniform}(2,14)\), the PDF is constant on the support \([2,14]\). Its height is

\[\frac{1}{14-2}=\frac{1}{12}.\]
Therefore,
\[f(x)= \begin{cases} \frac{1}{12}, & 2\le x\le 14,\\ 0, & \text{otherwise}. \end{cases}\]

d)

From parts a and b, the mean is \(E[X]=8\) and the standard deviation is \(\sigma=2\sqrt{3}\). Therefore, one standard deviation around the mean is

\[\left[E[X]-\sigma,\;E[X]+\sigma\right] = \left[8-2\sqrt{3},\;8+2\sqrt{3}\right] \approx [4.54,\;11.46].\]

Graph referenced from source.

e)

The highlighted interval is the interval found in part d:

\[\left[8-2\sqrt{3},\;8+2\sqrt{3}\right].\]
So the probability of the highlighted interval is
\[P\left(8-2\sqrt{3}\le X\le 8+2\sqrt{3}\right).\]

Using the PDF from part c, the height of the rectangle is \(\frac{1}{12}\). The base of the rectangle is the length of the highlighted interval:

\[\left(8+2\sqrt{3}\right)-\left(8-2\sqrt{3}\right)=4\sqrt{3}.\]

Therefore,

\[P\left(8-2\sqrt{3}\le X\le 8+2\sqrt{3}\right) = (\text{base})(\text{height}) = (4\sqrt{3})\left(\frac{1}{12}\right).\]
Thus,
\[P\left(8-2\sqrt{3}\le X\le 8+2\sqrt{3}\right) = \frac{4\sqrt{3}}{12} = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{3} \approx 0.577.\]

f)

Since this is a continuous model, the probability of any exact single value is \(0\). So we compare intervals of the same length around \(4\) and around \(12\).

Using the PDF from part c, any interval inside \([2,14]\) with length \(L\) has probability

\[P(\text{interval})=L\left(\frac{1}{12}\right)=\frac{L}{12}.\]

The following graph shows the \(8\) intervals. Intervals of length \(1\) are the darkest, and intervals of length \(4\) are the lightest.

Graph referenced from source.

Graph/Table block from source preserved in narrative context.

In each row, the two intervals have the same length and lie completely inside the support \([2,14]\). Since the distribution is uniform, their probabilities are equal.

The difference between the probabilities around \(4\) and around \(12\) is always

\[0.\]

A bus wait time \(W\) (minutes) is modeled by a triangular distribution with minimum \(a=2\), mode \(c=2\), and maximum \(b=14\):

\[W\sim \text{Triangular}(2,2,14).\]

↑

  1. Find \(E[W]\).

  2. Find \(\mathrm{Var}(W)\) and the standard deviation.

  3. Write the PDF of \(W\).

  4. Draw the graph of the PDF, indicate \(E[W]\), and draw an interval of one standard deviation around \(E[W]\).

  5. Calculate the probability of the highlighted interval.

  6. Compare the probabilities of intervals of the same length around \(4\) and around \(12\). What is the difference between these probabilities?

↑

For \(\text{Triangular}(a,c,b)\):

\[E[W]=\frac{a+b+c}{3},\qquad \mathrm{Var}(W)=\frac{a^2+b^2+c^2-ab-ac-bc}{18}.\]

a)

With \(a=2\), \(c=2\), and \(b=14\),

\[E[W]=\frac{2+14+2}{3}=\frac{18}{3}=6.\]

b)

Using the same parameters,

\[\mathrm{Var}(W)=\frac{2^2+14^2+2^2-(2)(14)-(2)(2)-(14)(2)}{18} = \frac{4+196+4-28-4-28}{18} = \frac{144}{18}=8.\]
Therefore,
\[\sigma=\sqrt{8}=2\sqrt{2}\approx 2.83.\]

c)

Since the mode is at the left endpoint, the PDF is a decreasing straight line from \(w=2\) to \(w=14\). The total area under the PDF must be \(1\). Therefore, the maximum height is found from the area of the full triangle:

\[A_{\triangle} = \frac{1}{2}(\text{base})(\text{height})=1 \qquad , \qquad \text{base}=14-2=12.\]
So
\[A_{\triangle}=\frac{1}{2}(12)(\text{height})=1 \qquad \longrightarrow \qquad 6(\text{height})=1 \qquad \longrightarrow \qquad \text{height}=\frac{1}{6}.\]

The PDF has height \(\frac{1}{6}\) at \(w=2\) and height \(0\) at \(w=14\). Since this is one decreasing line segment, use the basic line form

\[f(w)=m(w-c),\]
To find \(c\), use the fact that the line has height \(0\) at \(w=14\):
\[f(14)=0 \qquad \longrightarrow \qquad m(14-c)=0 \qquad \longrightarrow \qquad 14-c=0 \qquad \longrightarrow \qquad c=14.\]
Therefore, the line has the form
\[f(w)=m(w-14), \qquad 2\le w\le 14.\]

To find \(m\), use the fact that the line has height \(\frac{1}{6}\) at the mode \(w=2\):

\[f(2)=\frac{1}{6} \qquad \longrightarrow \qquad m(2-14)=\frac{1}{6} \qquad \longrightarrow \qquad -12m=\frac{1}{6} \qquad \longrightarrow \qquad m=-\frac{1}{72}.\]
Therefore,
\[f(w)=-\frac{1}{72}(w-14) = \frac{14-w}{72}, \qquad 2\le w\le 14.\]

Thus,

\[f(w)= \begin{cases} \frac{14-w}{72}, & 2\le w\le 14,\\ 0, & \text{otherwise}. \end{cases}\]

d)

From parts a and b, the mean is \(E[W]=6\) and the standard deviation is \(\sigma=2\sqrt{2}\). Therefore, one standard deviation around the mean is

\[[E[W]-\sigma,\;E[W]+\sigma] = [6-2\sqrt{2},\;6+2\sqrt{2}] \approx [3.17,\;8.83].\]

Graph referenced from source.

e)

The highlighted interval is the interval found in part d:

\[[6-2\sqrt{2},\;6+2\sqrt{2}].\]
The probability of this interval is represented by the area under the PDF above the highlighted interval:
\[P(6-2\sqrt{2}\le W\le 6+2\sqrt{2}).\]

Since the PDF is a straight line over this interval, the highlighted region is a trapezoid. Therefore,

\[P(6-2\sqrt{2}\le W\le 6+2\sqrt{2}) = \frac{1}{2}(\text{width})(\text{left height}+\text{right height}).\]

Using the PDF from part c, the height at the left endpoint of the highlighted interval is

\[\text{left height}=f(6-2\sqrt{2}) = \frac{14-(6-2\sqrt{2})}{72} = \frac{8+2\sqrt{2}}{72} = \frac{4+\sqrt{2}}{36}.\]
The height at the right endpoint is
\[\text{right height}=f(6+2\sqrt{2}) = \frac{14-(6+2\sqrt{2})}{72} = \frac{8-2\sqrt{2}}{72} = \frac{4-\sqrt{2}}{36}.\]

The width of the trapezoid is

\[\text{width} = (6+2\sqrt{2})-(6-2\sqrt{2}) = 4\sqrt{2}.\]

Substituting the width and the two heights into the trapezoid area formula,

\[P(6-2\sqrt{2}\le W\le 6+2\sqrt{2}) = \frac{1}{2}(4\sqrt{2}) \left( \frac{4+\sqrt{2}}{36} + \frac{4-\sqrt{2}}{36} \right).\]
Since
\[\frac{4+\sqrt{2}}{36} + \frac{4-\sqrt{2}}{36} = \frac{8}{36} = \frac{2}{9},\]
we get
\[P(6-2\sqrt{2}\le W\le 6+2\sqrt{2}) = \frac{1}{2}(4\sqrt{2})\left(\frac{2}{9}\right) = \frac{4\sqrt{2}}{9} \approx 0.629.\]

f)

Since this is a continuous model, the probability of any exact single value is \(0\). So we compare intervals of the same length around \(4\) and around \(12\).

Using the PDF from part c, each interval probability is found as the area of a trapezoid:

\[P(r\le W\le s) = \frac{1}{2}(\text{interval length})(f(r)+f(s)).\]

The following graph shows the \(8\) intervals. Intervals of length \(1\) are the darkest, and intervals of length \(4\) are the lightest.

Graph referenced from source.

Graph/Table block from source preserved in narrative context.

The intervals around \(4\) have larger probability than the intervals around \(12\). This happens because the density is higher near the left side of the distribution and decreases as \(W\) increases.

A bus wait time \(W\) (minutes) is modeled by a triangular distribution with minimum \(a=2\), mode \(c=6\), and maximum \(b=14\):

\[W\sim \text{Triangular}(2,6,14).\]

↑

  1. Find \(E[W]\).

  2. Find \(\mathrm{Var}(W)\) and the standard deviation.

  3. Write the PDF of \(W\).

  4. Draw the graph of the PDF, indicate \(E[W]\), and draw an interval of one standard deviation around \(E[W]\).

  5. Calculate the probability of the highlighted interval.

  6. Compare the probabilities of intervals of the same length around \(4\) and around \(12\). What is the difference between these probabilities?

↑

For \(\text{Triangular}(a,c,b)\):

\[E[W]=\frac{a+b+c}{3},\qquad \mathrm{Var}(W)=\frac{a^2+b^2+c^2-ab-ac-bc}{18}.\]

a)

With \(a=2\), \(c=6\), and \(b=14\),

\[E[W]=\frac{2+14+6}{3}=\frac{22}{3}\approx 7.33.\]

b)

Using the same parameters,

\[\mathrm{Var}(W)=\frac{2^2+14^2+6^2-(2)(14)-(2)(6)-(14)(6)}{18} = \frac{4+196+36-28-12-84}{18} = \frac{112}{18} = \frac{56}{9}.\]
Therefore,
\[\sigma=\sqrt{\frac{56}{9}}=\frac{2\sqrt{14}}{3}\approx 2.49.\]

c)

Since the mode is at \(w=6\), the PDF increases linearly from \(w=2\) to \(w=6\), then decreases linearly from \(w=6\) to \(w=14\). The total area under the PDF must be \(1\). Therefore, the maximum height is found from the area of the full triangle:

\[A_{\triangle} = \frac{1}{2}(\text{base})(\text{height})=1 \qquad , \qquad \text{base}=14-2=12.\]
So
\[A_{\triangle}=\frac{1}{2}(12)(\text{height})=1 \qquad \longrightarrow \qquad 6(\text{height})=1 \qquad \longrightarrow \qquad \text{height}=\frac{1}{6}.\]

The graph has two line segments. For each segment, use the basic line form

\[f(w)=m(w-c).\]

For the left side, the line has height \(0\) at \(w=2\). To find \(c\), use

\[f(2)=0 \qquad \longrightarrow \qquad m_1(2-c)=0 \qquad \longrightarrow \qquad 2-c=0 \qquad \longrightarrow \qquad c=2.\]
Therefore, the left side has the form
\[f(w)=m_1(w-2), \qquad 2\le w\le 6.\]

To find \(m_1\), use the fact that the line has height \(\frac{1}{6}\) at the mode \(w=6\):

\[f(6)=\frac{1}{6} \qquad \longrightarrow \qquad m_1(6-2)=\frac{1}{6} \qquad \longrightarrow \qquad 4m_1=\frac{1}{6} \qquad \longrightarrow \qquad m_1=\frac{1}{24}.\]
Therefore,
\[f(w)=\frac{1}{24}(w-2) = \frac{w-2}{24}, \qquad 2\le w\le 6.\]

For the right side, the line has height \(0\) at \(w=14\). To find \(c\), use

\[f(14)=0 \qquad \longrightarrow \qquad m_2(14-c)=0 \qquad \longrightarrow \qquad 14-c=0 \qquad \longrightarrow \qquad c=14.\]
Therefore, the right side has the form
\[f(w)=m_2(w-14), \qquad 6\le w\le 14.\]

To find \(m_2\), use the fact that the line has height \(\frac{1}{6}\) at the mode \(w=6\):

\[f(6)=\frac{1}{6} \qquad \longrightarrow \qquad m_2(6-14)=\frac{1}{6} \qquad \longrightarrow \qquad -8m_2=\frac{1}{6} \qquad \longrightarrow \qquad m_2=-\frac{1}{48}.\]
Therefore,
\[f(w)=-\frac{1}{48}(w-14) = \frac{14-w}{48}, \qquad 6\le w\le 14.\]

Thus,

\[f(w)= \begin{cases} \frac{w-2}{24}, & 2\le w\le 6,\\ \frac{14-w}{48}, & 6\le w\le 14,\\ 0, & \text{otherwise}. \end{cases}\]

d)

From parts a and b, the mean is \(E[W]=\frac{22}{3}\) and the standard deviation is \(\sigma=\frac{2\sqrt{14}}{3}\). Therefore, one standard deviation around the mean is

\[[E[W]-\sigma,\;E[W]+\sigma] = \left[\frac{22-2\sqrt{14}}{3},\;\frac{22+2\sqrt{14}}{3}\right] \approx [4.84,\;9.83].\]

Graph referenced from source.

e)

The highlighted interval is the interval found in part d:

\[\left[\frac{22-2\sqrt{14}}{3},\;\frac{22+2\sqrt{14}}{3}\right].\]
The probability of this interval is represented by the area under the PDF above the highlighted interval:
\[P\left(\frac{22-2\sqrt{14}}{3}\le W\le \frac{22+2\sqrt{14}}{3}\right).\]

Instead of calculating the highlighted area directly, use the complement rule. The total area under the PDF is \(1\), so the highlighted probability is

\[1-(\text{left extreme triangle})-(\text{right extreme triangle}).\]

Using the PDF from part c, the height at the left endpoint of the highlighted interval is

\[\text{left height} = f\left(\frac{22-2\sqrt{14}}{3}\right) = \frac{\frac{22-2\sqrt{14}}{3}-2}{24} = \frac{8-\sqrt{14}}{36}.\]
The base of the left extreme triangle is
\[\text{left base} = \frac{22-2\sqrt{14}}{3}-2 = \frac{16-2\sqrt{14}}{3}.\]
Therefore, the area of the left extreme triangle is
\[A_{L\triangle} = \frac{1}{2} \left(\frac{16-2\sqrt{14}}{3}\right) \left(\frac{8-\sqrt{14}}{36}\right) = \frac{13}{18}-\frac{4\sqrt{14}}{27}.\]

Using the PDF from part c, the height at the right endpoint of the highlighted interval is

\[\text{right height} = f\left(\frac{22+2\sqrt{14}}{3}\right) = \frac{14-\frac{22+2\sqrt{14}}{3}}{48} = \frac{10-\sqrt{14}}{72}.\]
The base of the right extreme triangle is
\[\text{right base} = 14-\frac{22+2\sqrt{14}}{3} = \frac{20-2\sqrt{14}}{3}.\]
Therefore, the area of the right extreme triangle is
\[A_{R\triangle} = \frac{1}{2} \left(\frac{20-2\sqrt{14}}{3}\right) \left(\frac{10-\sqrt{14}}{72}\right) = \frac{19}{36}-\frac{5\sqrt{14}}{54}.\]

By the complement rule,

\[P\left(\frac{22-2\sqrt{14}}{3}\le W\le \frac{22+2\sqrt{14}}{3}\right) = 1-A_{L\triangle}-A_{R\triangle}\]
Substituting the two triangle areas,
\[P\left(\frac{22-2\sqrt{14}}{3}\le W\le \frac{22+2\sqrt{14}}{3}\right) = 1- \left(\frac{13}{18}-\frac{4\sqrt{14}}{27}\right) - \left(\frac{19}{36}-\frac{5\sqrt{14}}{54}\right).\]
Thus,
\[P\left(\frac{22-2\sqrt{14}}{3}\le W\le \frac{22+2\sqrt{14}}{3}\right) = -\frac{1}{4}+\frac{13\sqrt{14}}{54} \approx 0.651.\]

f)

Since this is a continuous model, the probability of any exact single value is \(0\). So we compare intervals of the same length around \(4\) and around \(12\).

Using the PDF from part c, each interval probability is found as the area of a trapezoid:

\[P(r\le W\le s) = \frac{1}{2}(\text{interval length})(f(r)+f(s)).\]

The following graph shows the \(8\) intervals. Intervals of length \(1\) are the darkest, and intervals of length \(4\) are the lightest.

Graph referenced from source.

Graph/Table block from source preserved in narrative context.

The intervals around \(4\) have larger probability than the intervals around \(12\). This happens because \(4\) is closer to the mode than \(12\), so the density is higher around \(4\).

Quiz scores \(S\) are modeled by

\[S\sim N(6,4^2).\]

↑

  1. State \(E[S]\).

  2. State \(\mathrm{Var}(S)\) and the standard deviation.

  3. Write the PDF of \(S\).

  4. Give the intervals \([\mu-\sigma,\mu+\sigma]\) and \([\mu-2\sigma,\mu+2\sigma]\), draw the graph of the PDF, indicate \(E[S]\), and draw the interval of one standard deviation around \(E[S]\).

  5. Find the probability of the highlighted interval \([\mu-\sigma,\mu+\sigma]\).

  6. Compare the probabilities of intervals of the same length around \(6\) and around \(-2\). What is the difference between these probabilities?

↑

For \(N(\mu,\sigma^2)\),

\[E[S]=\mu, \qquad \mathrm{Var}(S)=\sigma^2.\]

a)

Since

\[S\sim N(6,4^2),\]
the mean is
\[E[S]=\mu=6.\]

b)

Using the same parameters,

\[\mathrm{Var}(S)=\sigma^2=4^2=16.\]
Therefore,
\[\sigma=4.\]

c)

For a normal distribution, the PDF is

\[f(s)=\frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-\frac{(s-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}}.\]

Using \(\mu=6\) and \(\sigma=4\), the PDF is

\[f(s)=\frac{1}{4\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-\frac{(s-6)^2}{2(4)^2}}.\]
Equivalently,
\[f(s)=\frac{1}{4\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-\frac{(s-6)^2}{32}}, \qquad -\infty

d)

From parts a and b, the mean is \(E[S]=6\) and the standard deviation is \(\sigma=4\). Therefore,

\[[\mu-\sigma,\mu+\sigma] = [6-4,\;6+4] = [2,10].\]
Also,
\[[\mu-2\sigma,\mu+2\sigma] = [6-2(4),\;6+2(4)] = [-2,14].\]

Graph referenced from source.

e)

The highlighted interval is the interval found in part d:

\[[2,10].\]
The probability of this interval is represented by the area under the normal curve above the highlighted interval:
\[P(2\le S\le 10).\]

To use the standard normal distribution, standardize with

\[Z=\frac{S-\mu}{\sigma} = \frac{S-6}{4}.\]
Then
\[P(2\le S\le 10) = P\left(\frac{2-6}{4}\le Z\le \frac{10-6}{4}\right).\]
So
\[P(2\le S\le 10) = P(-1\le Z\le 1).\]

Using the standard normal distribution,

\[P(-1\le Z\le 1)\approx 0.6827.\]
Therefore,
\[P(2\le S\le 10)\approx 0.6827.\]

f)

Since this is a continuous model, the probability of any exact single value is \(0\). So we compare intervals of the same length around \(4\) and around \(12\).

Using the standardization formula from part e,

\[Z=\frac{S-6}{4},\]
each interval probability can be found by converting the endpoints to \(z\)-scores and using the standard normal distribution.

The following graph shows the \(8\) intervals. Intervals of length \(1\) are the darkest, and intervals of length \(4\) are the lightest.

Graph referenced from source.

Graph/Table block from source preserved in narrative context.

The intervals around \(4\) have larger probability than the intervals around \(12\). This happens because \(4\) is closer to the mean \(6\), where the normal density is highest. The value \(12\) is farther from the mean, so intervals around \(12\) have less area under the curve.

Compare these uniform models for download times (seconds):

\[X_1\sim \text{Uniform}(4,10),\qquad X_2\sim \text{Uniform}(6,12),\qquad X_3\sim \text{Uniform}(4,14).\]

↑

  1. Find \(E[X_i]\) and \(\mathrm{Var}(X_i)\) for \(i=1,2,3\).

  2. Compare \(X_1\) and \(X_2\): only location changes. How do center and spread change?

  3. Compare \(X_1\) and \(X_3\): only width changes from 6 to 10. How does variance change?

  4. Which model has the widest spread, and why?

↑

Using \(E=\frac{a+b}{2}\), \(\mathrm{Var}=\frac{(b-a)^2}{12}\):

\[E[X_1]=7,\quad \mathrm{Var}(X_1)=\frac{6^2}{12}=3;\]
\[E[X_2]=9,\quad \mathrm{Var}(X_2)=\frac{6^2}{12}=3;\]
\[E[X_3]=9,\quad \mathrm{Var}(X_3)=\frac{10^2}{12}=\frac{25}{3}.\]

Graph/Table block from source preserved in narrative context.

Shifting both endpoints by the same amount changes the mean but not spread. Increasing interval width increases variance.

A packing process time \(T\) (minutes) follows triangular models:

\[T_1\sim \text{Triangular}(2,5,10),\quad T_2\sim \text{Triangular}(2,7,10),\quad T_3\sim \text{Triangular}(4,7,12).\]

↑

  1. Compute \(E[T_i]\) and \(\mathrm{Var}(T_i)\) for each model.

  2. Compare \(T_1\) and \(T_2\): only mode changes. How do center and shape change?

  3. Compare \(T_2\) and \(T_3\): all three parameters shift by +2. What stays the same and what changes?

  4. Which model is most concentrated near larger times? Explain using the peak location.

↑

Using triangular formulas:

\[E[T_1]=\frac{2+5+10}{3}=\frac{17}{3},\quad \mathrm{Var}(T_1)=\frac{2^2+5^2+10^2-2\cdot5-2\cdot10-5\cdot10}{18}=\frac{49}{18}.\]
\[E[T_2]=\frac{2+7+10}{3}=\frac{19}{3},\quad \mathrm{Var}(T_2)=\frac{2^2+7^2+10^2-2\cdot7-2\cdot10-7\cdot10}{18}=\frac{49}{18}.\]
\[E[T_3]=\frac{4+7+12}{3}=\frac{23}{3},\quad \mathrm{Var}(T_3)=\frac{4^2+7^2+12^2-4\cdot7-4\cdot12-7\cdot12}{18}=\frac{49}{18}.\]

Graph/Table block from source preserved in narrative context.

Changing only the mode shifts peak and mean but here keeps variance unchanged; shifting all parameters by +2 shifts location but not spread.

A project completion time \(Y\) (days) is modeled by

\[Y\sim \text{Triangular}(4,4+m,14),\qquad 0\le m\le 10.\]

↑

  1. Write \(E[Y]\) and \(\mathrm{Var}(Y)\) as functions of \(m\).

  2. State the condition on \(m\) that makes the model valid.

A delivery time \(U\) (minutes) for a local route is modeled by

\[U\sim \text{Uniform}(h-2w,\,h+w),\qquad w>0.\]

↑

  1. Derive \(E[U]\), \(\mathrm{Var}(U)\), and \(\sigma_U\) in terms of \(h\) and \(w\).

  2. Identify which parameter controls the center and which controls the interval width.

  3. Compare Case A: \((h,w)=(30,4)\) and Case B: \((h,w)=(35,4)\). Does the distribution shift, widen, or both?

  4. Compare Case C: \((h,w)=(30,4)\) and Case D: \((h,w)=(30,7)\). How do center, width, and spread change?

  5. If both \(h\) and \(w\) increase by 2 at the same time, explain whether the model shifts, widens, or both.

↑

For \(U\sim\text{Uniform}(a,b)\), \(E[U]=\frac{a+b}{2}\), \(\mathrm{Var}(U)=\frac{(b-a)^2}{12}\). Here \(a=h-2w\), \(b=h+w\), so

\[E[U]=\frac{(h-2w)+(h+w)}{2}=h-\frac{w}{2},\]
\[\mathrm{Var}(U)=\frac{\big((h+w)-(h-2w)\big)^2}{12}=\frac{(3w)^2}{12}=\frac{3w^2}{4},\]
\[\sigma_U=\sqrt{\frac{3w^2}{4}}=\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\,w \quad (w>0).\]
The center \(h-\frac{w}{2}\) depends on both \(h\) and \(w\), while the width \(b-a=3w\) depends only on \(w\). Case A \(\to\) B keeps \(w\) fixed, so width and spread stay the same, and the model shifts right by 5. Case C \(\to\) D keeps \(h\) fixed and increases \(w\), so width grows from \(12\) to \(21\), spread increases, and the center moves from \(28\) to \(26.5\) (left). If \(h\) and \(w\) both increase by 2, then width increases by \(3(2)=6\) (widens) and
\[E[U]'-(E[U])=\left(h+2-\frac{w+2}{2}\right)-\left(h-\frac{w}{2}\right)=1,\]
so it also shifts right by 1.

A machine-cycle time \(T\) (seconds) is modeled by

\[T\sim \text{Triangular}(k,\,k+r,\,k+3r),\qquad r>0.\]

↑

  1. Derive \(E[T]\), \(\mathrm{Var}(T)\), and \(\sigma_T\) in terms of \(k\) and \(r\).

  2. State the validity conditions on \(k\) and \(r\), and explain why they are sufficient.

  3. How do \(k\) and \(r\) separately affect center and spread?

  4. Compare shape with the symmetric triangular model having the same endpoints \(k\) and \(k+3r\). Is this model symmetric, left-skewed, or right-skewed?

  5. For fixed \(k\), compare \(r=2\) and \(r=5\): discuss changes in mean, variance, and skewness direction.

↑

For \(\text{Triangular}(a,c,b)\):

\[E[T]=\frac{a+b+c}{3},\qquad \mathrm{Var}(T)=\frac{a^2+b^2+c^2-ab-ac-bc}{18}.\]
With \(a=k\), \(c=k+r\), \(b=k+3r\):
\[E[T]=\frac{k+(k+r)+(k+3r)}{3}=k+\frac{4r}{3}.\]
For variance,
\[\mathrm{Var}(T)=\frac{k^2+(k+3r)^2+(k+r)^2-k(k+3r)-k(k+r)-(k+3r)(k+r)}{18} =\frac{7r^2}{18}.\]
Thus
\[\sigma_T=\sqrt{\frac{7r^2}{18}}=r\sqrt{\frac{7}{18}}.\]
Validity requires \(a\le c\le b\), i.e.
\[k\le k+r\le k+3r,\]
which holds whenever \(r\ge 0\); with \(r>0\), spread is nonzero, so the model is valid and non-degenerate for any real \(k\). Parameter \(k\) shifts location only. Parameter \(r\) increases both mean offset (\(\frac{4r}{3}\)) and spread (\(\propto r^2\)). For endpoints \(k\) and \(k+3r\), symmetry would need mode at midpoint \(k+\frac{3r}{2}\), but here mode is \(k+r\), left of midpoint; therefore the model is right-skewed. Comparing \(r=2\) and \(r=5\) (fixed \(k\)): mean increases by \(\frac{4}{3}(5-2)=4\), variance scales from \(\frac{28}{18}\) to \(\frac{175}{18}\), and skewness direction stays right-skewed.

The daily amount \(N\) (GB) of network data used by a class is modeled by

\[N\sim N\!\left(50+3p,\,(2+q)^2\right),\qquad p\in\mathbb{R},\ q>-2.\]

↑

  1. Derive \(E[N]\), \(\mathrm{Var}(N)\), and \(\sigma_N\) in terms of \(p\) and \(q\).

  2. Explain the role of \(p\) and \(q\): which controls location and which controls spread?

  3. Compare Case A: \((p,q)=(0,1)\) and Case B: \((p,q)=(-2,1)\). What changes in center, concentration around the mean, and tail likelihood?

  4. Compare Case C: \((p,q)=(0,1)\) and Case D: \((p,q)=(0,4)\). What changes in center, spread, concentration near the mean, and likelihood of values far from the mean?

  5. Between models \(M_1:(p,q)=(1,0)\) and \(M_2:(p,q)=(-1,3)\), which model is more likely to produce values at least 15 GB above its own mean? Justify conceptually.

↑

For \(N(\mu,\sigma^2)\), \(E[N]=\mu\), \(\mathrm{Var}(N)=\sigma^2\), \(\sigma>0\). So

\[E[N]=50+3p,\qquad \mathrm{Var}(N)=(2+q)^2,\qquad \sigma_N=2+q \quad (q>-2).\]
Parameter \(p\) changes only location (horizontal shift). Parameter \(q\) changes spread: larger \(q\) gives larger \(\sigma\), wider curve, lower peak, and heavier tails. Case A \(\to\) B keeps \(q=1\), so \(\sigma=3\) is unchanged: concentration and tail behavior relative to each mean stay the same, but mean shifts from \(50\) to \(44\) (left by 6). Case C \(\to\) D keeps \(p=0\), so mean stays 50, but \(\sigma\) increases from \(3\) to \(6\): values are less concentrated near 50 and far-from-mean values become more likely. For \(M_1\), \(\sigma=2\); for \(M_2\), \(\sigma=5\). A value 15 GB above its own mean is \(15/\sigma\) standard deviations above mean: \(7.5\sigma\) for \(M_1\) versus \(3\sigma\) for \(M_2\). The \(3\sigma\) event is much less extreme, so \(M_2\) is more likely.
