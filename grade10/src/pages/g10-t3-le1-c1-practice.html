This first task is a template for all the density-function problems in this activity. Let \(X\) be a continuous random variable with bounded support \([a,b]\), where \(a<b\). Suppose a proposed model is

\[f(x)=\begin{cases}k\,g(x), & a\le x\le b,\\0, & \text{otherwise,}\end{cases}\]

where \(g(x)\) is a known nonnegative profile on \([a,b]\) (it may be constant, linear, triangular, piecewise, symmetric, etc.) and \(k\) is an unknown constant.

Question.

  • C1 Calculations: Determine the value of \(k\) that makes \(f(x)\) a valid PDF on \([a,b]\).
  • C1 Interpretation: Explain what this normalization means for the random variable \(X\) and why the model is valid.
  • Extra analysis: For any subinterval \([c,d]\subseteq[a,b]\) with \(a\le c<d\le b\), express \(P(c\le X\le d)\) and indicate this probability region on a graph.
↑

Structured solution

(C1) Calculations

A valid density must satisfy two general conditions: (1) \(f(x)\ge 0\) on its support (true here when \(k\ge 0\) because \(g(x)\ge 0\)), and (2) total area over \([a,b]\) is \(1\).

Let \(A_{\text{total}}=\) area under \(g(x)\) on \([a,b]\). Then

\[\int_a^b f(x)\,dx=\int_a^b k\,g(x)\,dx=kA_{\text{total}}=1,\]

so the normalizing constant is

\[k=\frac{1}{A_{\text{total}}},\qquad f(x)=\frac{g(x)}{A_{\text{total}}}\text{ on }[a,b].\]

(For each specific problem later, \(A_{\text{total}}\) is found by geometry from that problem's shape.)

(C1) Interpretation

The computation of \(k\) rescales the proposed shape \(g(x)\) so that the entire probability over the support \([a,b]\) is exactly \(1\). In other words, the graph of \(f(x)\) keeps the same relative shape as \(g(x)\), but its total area is normalized to represent a complete probability model for the continuous variable \(X\).

Graph of the obtained PDF

PDF graph for the General Problem template.

Extra analysis

For any event interval \([c,d]\subseteq[a,b]\),

\[P(c\le X\le d)=\int_c^d f(x)\,dx=\frac{1}{A_{\text{total}}}\int_c^d g(x)\,dx=\frac{A_{[c,d]}}{A_{\text{total}}},\]

where \(A_{[c,d]}\) is the geometric area under \(g(x)\) from \(c\) to \(d\). So every later problem follows the same pattern: normalize total area first, then compute subinterval probability as a shaded area fraction.

A sensor records the fill level \(X\) (in meters) of a water tank during a short period. Assume \(X\) is continuous on \([0,5]\) with density

\[f(x)=k,\quad 0\le x\le 5.\]

Question.

  • C1 Calculations: Determine \(k\) so the model is a valid density.
  • C1 Interpretation: Explain what the C1 calculations show about why the density model is valid.
  • Extra analysis: Compute \(P(1\le X\le 4)\) and represent this event on a probability-region graph.
↑

Structured solution

(C1) Calculations

The graph is a rectangle with width 5 and height \(k\), so total area is

\[5k=1\Rightarrow k=\frac{1}{5}.\]

(C1) Interpretation

The value \(k=\frac{1}{5}\) makes the total area over all possible tank fill levels from \(0\) to \(5\) meters equal to \(1\), so this is a valid probability model for \(X\). Because the density is constant on the interval, the model gives the same density to each fill level in that range.

\[P(1\le X\le 4)=3\cdot\frac{1}{5}=\frac{3}{5}=0.6.\]

The travel time \(T\) (in seconds) for a robot to cross a ramp is modeled on \([2,8]\) by

\[f(t)=k(t-2),\quad 2\le t\le 8.\]

Question.

  • C1 Calculations: Determine \(k\) so \(f(t)\) is a valid density.
  • C1 Interpretation: Explain what the C1 calculations show about why the density model is valid.
  • Extra analysis: Compute \(P(T>6)\) and represent this event on a probability-region graph.
↑

Structured solution

(C1) Calculations

The graph from 2 to 8 is a triangle with base 6 and height \(f(8)=6k\). So total area is

\[\frac{1}{2}(6)(6k)=18k=1\Rightarrow k=\frac{1}{18}.\]

(C1) Interpretation

The value \(k=\frac{1}{18}\) makes the full area across robot travel times from \(2\) to \(8\) seconds equal to \(1\), and \(f(t)\ge 0\) on that interval, so this is a valid density model for \(T\). Since the line increases with \(t\), larger times in this range are modeled with greater density than smaller times.

Extra analysis

For \(T>6\), use the trapezoid on \([6,8]\). Its left height is \(f(6)=\frac{1}{18}(4)=\frac{2}{9}\), right height is \(f(8)=\frac{1}{3}\), width is 2:

\[P(T>6)=\frac{\left(\frac{2}{9}+\frac{1}{3}\right)}{2}\cdot 2=\frac{2}{9}+\frac{1}{3}=\frac{5}{9}.\]

An air-quality indicator \(A\) is modeled on \([0,4]\) with piecewise-constant density

\[f(a)=\begin{cases}k, & 0\le a<1,\\2k, & 1\le a<3,\\k, & 3\le a\le 4.\end{cases}\]

Question.

  • C1 Calculations: Find \(k\).
  • C1 Interpretation: Explain what the C1 calculations show about why the density model is valid.
  • Extra analysis: Compute \(P(0.5\le A\le 2.5)\) and represent this event on a probability-region graph.
↑

Structured solution

(C1) Calculations

Add rectangle areas for total area 1:

\[(1\cdot k)+(2\cdot 2k)+(1\cdot k)=6k=1\Rightarrow k=\frac{1}{6}.\]

(C1) Interpretation

With \(k=\frac{1}{6}\), the three block areas over all air-quality index values \(a\in[0,4]\) add to \(1\), and each piece is nonnegative, so this is a valid PDF for \(A\). The taller middle block means the model assigns greater density to index values between \(1\) and \(3\) than to values in \([0,1)\) or \([3,4]\).

Extra analysis

Now split \([0.5,2.5]\): from 0.5 to 1 area is \(0.5\cdot\frac{1}{6}=\frac{1}{12}\), and from 1 to 2.5 area is \(1.5\cdot\frac{1}{3}=\frac{1}{2}\). So

\[P(0.5\le A\le 2.5)=\frac{1}{12}+\frac{1}{2}=\frac{7}{12}.\]

A delivery offset \(Y\) (hours from schedule) is modeled on \([-1,3]\) with a triangular density that rises to a peak at \(y=1\):

\[f(y)=\begin{cases}k(y+1), & -1\le y\le 1,\\k(3-y), & 1< y\le 3.\end{cases}\]

Question.

  • C1 Calculations: Determine \(k\).
  • C1 Interpretation: Explain what the C1 calculations show about why the density model is valid.
  • Extra analysis: Compute \(P(0\le Y\le 2)\) and represent this event on a probability-region graph.
↑

Structured solution

(C1) Calculations

The whole graph is one triangle with base 4 (from -1 to 3) and height \(f(1)=2k\). Thus

\[\frac{1}{2}(4)(2k)=4k=1\Rightarrow k=\frac{1}{4}.\]

(C1) Interpretation

The constant \(k=\frac{1}{4}\) makes the total area across all delivery offsets from \(-1\) to \(3\) hours equal to \(1\), so this is a valid density model for \(Y\). Its triangular shape means offsets near \(y=1\) have greater density, while offsets near the endpoints have lower density, and the model is symmetric around \(y=1\).

Extra analysis

For \(0\le Y\le 2\), use two equal trapezoids: on \([0,1]\) heights are \(f(0)=\frac{1}{4}\) and \(f(1)=\frac{1}{2}\), so area is \(\frac{\left(\frac{1}{4}+\frac{1}{2}\right)}{2}\cdot 1=\frac{3}{8}\). By symmetry, area on \([1,2]\) is also \(\frac{3}{8}\). Hence

\[P(0\le Y\le 2)=\frac{3}{8}+\frac{3}{8}=\frac{3}{4}.\]

\[f(v)=\begin{cases}k, & 0\le v<2,\\2k, & 2\le v\le 6.\end{cases}\]

Structured solution

(C1) Calculations

Total area:

\[(2\cdot k)+(4\cdot 2k)=2k+8k=10k=1\Rightarrow k=\frac{1}{10}.\]

The value \(k=\frac{1}{10}\) makes the total area over all vibration levels from \(0\) to \(6\) equal to \(1\), and both block heights are positive, so this is a valid PDF for \(V\). Because the second block is higher, levels from \(2\) to \(6\) are modeled with greater density than levels from \(0\) to \(2\).

A cooling error variable \(E\) is modeled on \([1,5]\) by

\[f(e)=k(5-e),\quad 1\le e\le 5.\]

Question.

  • C1 Calculations: Determine \(k\).
  • C1 Interpretation: Explain what the C1 calculations show about why the density model is valid.
  • Extra analysis: Compute \(P(2\le E\le 4)\) and represent this event on a probability-region graph.
↑

Structured solution

(C1) Calculations

The full graph is a triangle decreasing from height \(f(1)=4k\) to 0 at \(e=5\). Base is 4, so

\[\frac{1}{2}(4)(4k)=8k=1\Rightarrow k=\frac{1}{8}.\]

(C1) Interpretation

Choosing \(k=\frac{1}{8}\) makes the full area over cooling error values from \(1\) to \(5\) equal to \(1\), so this nonnegative linear rule is a valid density model for \(E\). Since the line decreases as \(e\) increases, smaller errors in this interval are modeled with greater density than larger errors.

Let \(G\) (seconds) be a traffic gap modeled on \([0,6]\) by

\[f(g)=\begin{cases}kg, & 0\le g<2,\\2k, & 2\le g<4,\\k(6-g), & 4\le g\le 6.\end{cases}\]

Question.

  • C1 Calculations: Find \(k\).
  • C1 Interpretation: Explain what the C1 calculations show about why the density model is valid.
  • Extra analysis: Compute \(P(1\le G\le 5)\) and represent this event on a probability-region graph.
↑

Structured solution

(C1) Calculations

Compute total area by pieces: left triangle on \([0,2]\): \(\frac{1}{2}(2)(2k)=2k\), middle rectangle on \([2,4]\): \((2)(2k)=4k\), right triangle on \([4,6]\): \(\frac{1}{2}(2)(2k)=2k\). So

\[2k+4k+2k=8k=1\Rightarrow k=\frac{1}{8}.\]

(C1) Interpretation

The result \(k=\frac{1}{8}\) makes the two end triangles and middle rectangle over all traffic gaps \(g\in[0,6]\) add to total area \(1\), so this nonnegative piecewise graph is a valid PDF for \(G\). The flat middle section shows equal density for gaps from \(2\) to \(4\), while the sloped sides show lower density near very small and very large gaps.

Extra analysis

Now \(P(1\le G\le 5)\) equals total area minus two small end triangles: each end triangle has base 1 and height \(\frac{1}{8}\), so each area is \(\frac{1}{2}(1)\left(\frac{1}{8}\right)=\frac{1}{16}\). Hence

\[P(1\le G\le 5)=1-\frac{1}{16}-\frac{1}{16}=\frac{7}{8}.\]

A quality score \(Q\) is modeled on \([2,8]\) by a line that starts above zero:

\[f(q)=k\left(1+\frac{q-2}{3}\right),\quad 2\le q\le 8.\]

Question.

  • C1 Calculations: Determine \(k\).
  • C1 Interpretation: Explain what the C1 calculations show about why the density model is valid.
  • Extra analysis: Compute \(P(4\le Q\le 7)\) and represent this event on a probability-region graph.
↑

Structured solution

(C1) Calculations

This is linear, with \(f(2)=k\) and \(f(8)=3k\).

The full graph on \([2,8]\) is a trapezoid with parallel sides \(k\) and \(3k\), width 6:

\[\text{Area}=\frac{k+3k}{2}\cdot 6=12k=1\Rightarrow k=\frac{1}{12}.\]

(C1) Interpretation

The value \(k=\frac{1}{12}\) makes the full trapezoid area across quality scores from \(2\) to \(8\) equal to \(1\), so this is a valid density model for \(Q\). Because the line rises from left to right, higher quality scores in this interval are modeled with greater density than lower scores.

A measurement error \(Z\) is modeled on \([-2,2]\) by

\[f(z)=\begin{cases}k(z+2), & -2\le z\le 0,\\k(2-z), & 0< z\le 2.\end{cases}\]

Question.

  • C1 Calculations: Find \(k\).
  • C1 Interpretation: Explain what the C1 calculations show about why the density model is valid.
  • Extra analysis: Compute \(P(-1\le Z\le 1)\) and represent this event on a probability-region graph.
↑

Structured solution

(C1) Calculations

The graph is a symmetric triangle with base 4 and peak height \(f(0)=2k\). So

\[\frac{1}{2}(4)(2k)=4k=1\Rightarrow k=\frac{1}{4}.\]

(C1) Interpretation

With \(k=\frac{1}{4}\), the centered triangle over all measurement errors from \(-2\) to \(2\) has total area \(1\) and stays nonnegative, so it is a valid probability density model for \(Z\). The symmetry indicates errors equally far above and below \(0\) have the same density, with the greatest density near zero error.

Extra analysis

For \(-1\le Z\le 1\), use symmetry: compute area on \([-1,0]\) and double it. On \([-1,0]\), heights are \(f(-1)=\frac{1}{4}\) and \(f(0)=\frac{1}{2}\) with width 1:

\[\text{Area}[-1,0]=\frac{\frac{1}{4}+\frac{1}{2}}{2}\cdot 1=\frac{3}{8}.\]

Therefore

\[P(-1\le Z\le 1)=2\cdot\frac{3}{8}=\frac{3}{4}.\]

A service time variable \(S\) (minutes) is modeled on \([0,10]\) by

\[f(s)=\begin{cases}k, & 0\le s<4,\\\frac{k}{2}, & 4\le s<8,\\\frac{3k}{2}, & 8\le s\le 10.\end{cases}\]

Question.

  • C1 Calculations: Determine \(k\).
  • C1 Interpretation: Explain what the C1 calculations show about why the density model is valid.
  • Extra analysis: Compute \(P(S<7)\) and represent this event on a probability-region graph.
↑

Structured solution

(C1) Calculations

Use rectangle areas for total area 1:

\[(4)(k)+(4)\left(\frac{k}{2}\right)+(2)\left(\frac{3k}{2}\right)=4k+2k+3k=9k=1,\]

so

\[k=\frac{1}{9}.\]

(C1) Interpretation

The constant \(k=\frac{1}{9}\) makes the three rectangular blocks across service times from \(0\) to \(10\) minutes total exactly \(1\), so this nonnegative piecewise-constant rule is a valid PDF for \(S\). The different block heights show that the model gives different density levels to short, midrange, and late service times.
