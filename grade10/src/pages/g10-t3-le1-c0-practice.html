Problem. Let \(X\) be the number of app notifications a student receives in one hour. The probability mass function is

\[\begin{array}{c|cccc}x & 0 & 1 & 2 & 3\\\hline P(X=x) & 0.2 & 0.3 & k & 0.1\end{array}\]

Question.

  • (a) Determine \(k\) so that this is a valid probability distribution.
  • (b) Compute: (i) \(P(X=2)\), (ii) \(P(X\le 2)\), and (iii) \(P(1\le X\le 2)\).
  • (c) Interpret these results in terms of percentage probability.
↑

Structured solution

(a)

For a valid pmf,

\[ 0.2+0.3+k+0.1=1\Rightarrow k=0.4. \]

\[ P(X=x)=\{(0,0.2),(1,0.3),(2,0.4),(3,0.1)\}. \]

PMF graph for Problem 1.

Open this example in the interactive discrete probabilities page

(b)

\[ P(X=2)=0.4,\quad P(X\le 2)=0.2+0.3+0.4=0.9,\quad P(1\le X\le 2)=0.3+0.4=0.7. \]

(c) There is a 40% probability of exactly 2 notifications, a 90% probability of at most 2 notifications, and a 70% probability of between 1 and 2 notifications.

\[ P(Y=y)=\{(0,0.5),(1,0.2),(2,0.2),(3,0.1)\}. \]

Problem. Let \(Z\) be the number of lost pens found in a small box. The probability mass function is

\[\begin{array}{c|cccc}z & 0 & 1 & 2 & 3\\\hline P(Z=z) & 0.4 & 0.5 & a & 0.2\end{array}\]

Question.

  • (a) Solve for \(a\) so the probabilities sum to 1.
  • (b) Decide whether the resulting distribution is valid.
  • (c) Using the computed \(a\), compute \(P(Z\ge 2)\).
↑

Structured solution

(a)

From total probability,

\[ 0.4+0.5+a+0.2=1\Rightarrow 1.1+a=1\Rightarrow a=-0.1. \]

Not a valid probability distribution because \(a<0\).

\[ P(Z=z)=\{(0,0.4),(1,0.5),(2,-0.1),(3,0.2)\}. \]

(c) The computed value at \(z=2\) is negative, so the model is not a valid pmf.

Problem. A café tracks \(T\), the number of customers arriving in a 10-minute window.

\[\begin{array}{c|cccc}t & 1 & 2 & 3 & 4\\\hline P(T=t) & \lambda & 0.7-\lambda & 0.2 & 0.1\end{array}\]

Question.

  • (a) Determine \(\lambda\) so that this is a valid probability distribution.
  • (b) Compute: (i) \(P(T=2)\), (ii) \(P(T\ne 4)\), and (iii) \(P(2\le T\le 3)\).
  • (c) Interpret these results in terms of percentage probability.
↑

Structured solution

(a)

Sum-to-1 gives

\[ \lambda+(0.7-\lambda)+0.2+0.1=1, \]

which is true for all \(\lambda\). For a valid pmf we need nonnegative probabilities:

\[ \lambda\ge 0,\qquad 0.7-\lambda\ge 0 \Rightarrow 0\le \lambda\le 0.7. \]

\[ P(T=t)=\{(1,\lambda),(2,0.7-\lambda),(3,0.2),(4,0.1)\},\quad 0\le\lambda\le 0.7. \]

PMF graph for Problem 5.

Open this example in the interactive discrete probabilities page

(b)

\[ P(T=2)=0.7-\lambda,\quad P(T\ne 4)=1-0.1=0.9,\quad P(2\le T\le 3)=(0.7-\lambda)+0.2=0.9-\lambda. \]

(c) There is a \(100(0.7-\lambda)\%\) probability of exactly 2 customers, a 90% probability of a number of customers different from 4, and a \(100(0.9-\lambda)\%\) probability of between 2 and 3 customers.

\[P(Y=y)=\begin{cases}r, & y=0,\\2r, & y=1,\\3r, & y=2,\\2r, & y=3,\\1-8r, & y=4.\end{cases}\]

↑

Structured solution

(a)

From the total probability condition,

\[ r+2r+3r+2r+(1-8r)=1, \]

which is true for all \(r\).

For the distribution to be valid, all probabilities must be nonnegative:

Hence,

\[ P(Y=y)=\{(0,r),(1,2r),(2,3r),(3,2r),(4,1-8r)\},\quad 0\le r\le \frac{1}{8}. \]

\[ r\ge 0,\qquad 1-8r\ge 0. \]

\[ 0\le r\le \frac{1}{8}. \]

\[ h+0.4+2h+0.3+(0.3-3h)=1, \]

\[ h\ge 0,\qquad 0.3-3h\ge 0. \]

From \(0.3-3h\ge 0\), we get \(0.3\ge 3h\), so \(0.1\ge h\), that is, \(h\le 0.1\). Hence

\[ 0\le h\le 0.1. \]

(c)

\[ P(W=w)=\{(0,h),(1,0.4),(2,2h),(3,0.3),(4,0.3-3h)\}. \]

Problem. Let \(X\) be the number of late buses observed in a morning. The probability mass function is

\[\begin{array}{c|cccc}x & 0 & 1 & 2 & 3\\\hline P(X=x) & u & v & 2u & v\end{array}\]

and \(P(X=0)=P(X=3)\).

Question.

  • (a) Determine \(u\) and \(v\) so that the model is valid.
  • (b) Compute: (i) \(P(X=2)\), (ii) \(P(X>1)\), and (iii) \(P(1\le X\le 3)\).
  • (c) Interpret these results in terms of percentage probability.
↑

Structured solution

(a)

From the extra condition \(P(X=0)=P(X=3)\),

\[ u=v. \]

From total probability,

\[ u+v+2u+v=1\Rightarrow 3u+2v=1. \]

Substitute \(v=u\):

\[ P(X=x)=\{(0,0.2),(1,0.2),(2,0.4),(3,0.2)\}. \]

\[ 5u=1\Rightarrow u=\frac{1}{5},\quad v=\frac{1}{5}. \]

(b)

\[ P(X=2)=2u=\frac{2}{5},\quad P(X>1)=2u+v=\frac{3}{5},\quad P(1\le X\le 3)=v+2u+v=\frac{4}{5}. \]

(c) There is a 40% probability of exactly 2 late buses, a 60% probability of more than 1 late bus, and an 80% probability of between 1 and 3 late buses.

Problem. Let \(Y\) be the number of deliveries delayed in a morning. The probability mass function is

\[\begin{array}{c|cccc}y & 0 & 1 & 2 & 3\\\hline P(Y=y) & 0.3 & \gamma & \gamma & \gamma\end{array}\]

and it is known that

\[P(Y=2)=2P(Y=0).\]

Question.

  • (a) Solve for \(\gamma\) so the probabilities sum to 1.
  • (b) Decide whether the resulting distribution is valid.
  • (c) Using the computed \(\gamma\), compute \(P(Y\ge 1)\).
↑

Structured solution

(a)

From the added condition,

\[ \gamma=2P(Y=0)=2(0.3)=0.6. \]

Using total probability,

\[ 0.3+\gamma+\gamma+\gamma=1. \]

Substituting \(\gamma=0.6\) but this cannot be evaluated as a valid probability from this table.

\[ 0.3+0.6+0.6+0.6=2.1\neq 1. \]

Therefore, the conditions are incompatible with the requirement that probabilities sum to 1, so this does not produce a valid probability mass function.

Equivalently, if we force the sum-to-1 condition first,

\[ 0.3+3\gamma=1\Rightarrow 3\gamma=0.7\Rightarrow \gamma=\frac{7}{30}, \]

but this contradicts the required condition \(\gamma=0.6\).

Not a valid probability distribution because the probabilities cannot simultaneously satisfy both the sum-to-1 condition and the condition \(P(Y=2)=2P(Y=0)\).

If the value from the added condition is used,

\[ P(Y=y)=\{(0,0.3),(1,0.6),(2,0.6),(3,0.6)\}. \]

PMF graph for Problem 12.

Open this example in the interactive discrete probabilities page

(b)

\[ P(Y\ge 1)=P(Y=1)+P(Y=2)+P(Y=3)=\gamma+\gamma+\gamma=3\gamma=3(0.6)=1.8. \]

(c) The extra condition forces values that make the total probability exceed 1, so the pmf is invalid.

Problem. Let \(X\) be the number of yellow cards shown in a match summary. The probability mass function is

\[\begin{array}{c|ccccc}x & 0 & 1 & 2 & 3 & 4\\\hline P(X=x) & b & a & a+b & 2a & a\end{array}\]

and it is known that \(P(X=4)=4P(X=0)\).

Question.

  • (a) Find \(a\) and \(b\) so the model is valid.
  • (b) Compute: (i) \(P(X=4)\), (ii) \(P(X\le 1)\), and (iii) \(P(2\le X\le 4)\).
  • (c) Interpret these results in terms of percentage probability.
↑

Structured solution

(a)

From the condition \(P(X=4)=4P(X=0)\),

\[ a=4b. \]

From total probability,

\[ b+a+(a+b)+2a+a=1\Rightarrow 2b+5a=1. \]

Substitute \(a=4b\):

\[ 2b+5(4b)=1\Rightarrow 22b=1\Rightarrow b=\frac{1}{22}. \]

Then

\[ a=4b=\frac{4}{22}=\frac{2}{11}. \]

\[ P(X=x)=\{(0,0.045),(1,0.182),(2,0.227),(3,0.364),(4,0.182)\}. \]

\[ P(X=4)=a=\frac{2}{11},\quad P(X\le 1)=b+a=\frac{1}{22}+\frac{2}{11}=\frac{5}{22},\quad P(2\le X\le 4)=(a+b)+2a+a=b+4a=\frac{1}{22}+\frac{8}{11}=\frac{17}{22}. \]

(c) There is a \(\frac{2}{11}\) probability of exactly 4 yellow cards, a \(\frac{5}{22}\) probability of at most 1 yellow card, and a \(\frac{17}{22}\) probability of between 2 and 4 yellow cards.

Problem. Let \(Z\) be the number of phone calls to an office in a short period. The probability mass function is

\[\begin{array}{c|cccc}z & 0 & 1 & 2 & 3\\\hline P(Z=z) & \frac{\lambda}{2} & \lambda & \frac{3\lambda}{2} & \lambda\end{array}\]

Question.

  • (a) Determine \(\lambda\).
  • (b) Compute: (i) \(P(Z=2)\), (ii) \(P(Z\le 1)\), and (iii) \(P(1\le Z\le 3)\).
  • (c) Interpret these results in terms of percentage probability.
↑

Structured solution

(a)

Apply total probability 1:

\[ \frac{\lambda}{2}+\lambda+\frac{3\lambda}{2}+\lambda=4\lambda=1\Rightarrow \lambda=\frac{1}{4}. \]

\[ P(Z=z)=\{(0,0.125),(1,0.25),(2,0.375),(3,0.25)\}. \]

\[ P(Z=2)=\frac{3\lambda}{2}=\frac{3}{8},\quad P(Z\le 1)=\frac{\lambda}{2}+\lambda=\frac{3}{8},\quad P(1\le Z\le 3)=\lambda+\frac{3\lambda}{2}+\lambda=\frac{7\lambda}{2}=\frac{7}{8}. \]

(c) There is a 37.5% probability of exactly 2 phone calls, a 37.5% probability of at most 1 phone call, and an 87.5% probability of between 1 and 3 phone calls.

Problem. A teacher records \(W\), the number of missing homework submissions in a class group.

\[\begin{array}{c|ccccc}w & 0 & 1 & 2 & 3 & 4\\\hline P(W=w) & r & 2r & 3r & \frac{1}{2}-2r & \frac{1}{4}\end{array}\]

Question.

  • (a) Determine \(r\) so this is a valid distribution.
  • (b) Compute: (i) \(P(W=4)\), (ii) \(P(W>2)\), and (iii) \(P(1\le W\le 3)\).
  • (c) Interpret these results in terms of percentage probability.
↑

Structured solution

(a)

Check total probability:

\[ P(W=w)=\{(0,0.062),(1,0.125),(2,0.188),(3,0.375),(4,0.25)\}. \]

\[ r+2r+3r+\left(\frac{1}{2}-2r\right)+\frac{1}{4}=1 \Rightarrow 4r+\frac{3}{4}=1 \Rightarrow r=\frac{1}{16}. \]

\[ P(W=4)=\frac{1}{4},\quad P(W>2)=\left(\frac{1}{2}-2r\right)+\frac{1}{4}=\frac{5}{8},\quad P(1\le W\le 3)=2r+3r+\left(\frac{1}{2}-2r\right)=\frac{11}{16}. \]

(c) There is a 25% probability of exactly 4 missing submissions, a 62.5% probability of more than 2 missing submissions, and a 68.75% probability of between 1 and 3 missing submissions.

Problem. Let \(T\) be the number of product returns processed at a counter in a short period. Suppose

\[\begin{array}{c|ccccc}t & 0 & 1 & 2 & 3 & 4\\\hline P(T=t) & g & 2g & 2g & g & g\end{array}\]

Question.

  • (a) Find \(g\).
  • (b) Compute: (i) \(P(T=2)\), (ii) \(P(T\ne 2)\) using a complement, and (iii) \(P(1\le T\le 3)\).
  • (c) Interpret these results in terms of percentage probability.
↑

Structured solution

(a)

Use the total probability rule:

\[ g+2g+2g+g+g=7g=1\Rightarrow g=\frac{1}{7}. \]

\[ P(T=t)=\{(0,0.143),(1,0.286),(2,0.286),(3,0.143),(4,0.143)\}. \]

(c) There is a 28.57% probability of exactly 2 returns, a 71.43% probability of a number of returns different from 2, and a 71.43% probability of between 1 and 3 returns.
