Problem. Let \(X\) be the number of app notifications a student receives in one hour. The probability mass function is

\[\begin{array}{c|cccc}x & 0 & 1 & 2 & 3\\\hline P(X=x) & 0.2 & 0.3 & k & 0.1\end{array}\]

Question.

  • (a) Determine \(k\) so that this is a valid probability distribution.
  • (b) Compute: (i) \(P(X=2)\), (ii) \(P(X\le 2)\), and (iii) \(P(1\le X\le 2)\).
  • (c) Interpret these results in terms of percentage probability.
↑

Structured solution

For a valid pmf,

\[ 0.2+0.3+k+0.1=1\Rightarrow k=0.4. \]

\[ P(X=2)=0.4,\quad P(X\le 2)=0.2+0.3+0.4=0.9,\quad P(1\le X\le 2)=0.3+0.4=0.7. \]

Interpretation. There is a 40% probability of exactly 2 notifications, a 90% probability of at most 2 notifications, and a 70% probability of between 1 and 2 notifications.

Problem. A quality check records \(Y\), the number of defective items in a small batch. Suppose

\[\begin{array}{c|cccc}y & 0 & 1 & 2 & 3\\\hline P(Y=y) & 0.5 & c & 0.2 & 0.1\end{array}\]

Question.

  • (a) Determine \(c\) so that the distribution is valid.
  • (b) Compute: (i) \(P(Y=1)\), (ii) \(P(1\le Y\le 2)\), and (iii) \(P(Y\ge 1)\).
  • (c) Interpret these results in terms of percentage probability.
↑

Structured solution

Sum-to-1 gives

\[ 0.5+c+0.2+0.1=1\Rightarrow c=0.2. \]

\[ P(Y=1)=0.2,\quad P(1\le Y\le 2)=0.2+0.2=0.4,\quad P(Y\ge 1)=0.2+0.2+0.1=0.5. \]

Interpretation. There is a 20% probability of exactly 1 defective item, a 40% probability of 1 or 2 defective items, and a 50% probability of at least 1 defective item.

Problem. Let \(Z\) be the number of lost pens found in a small box. The probability mass function is

\[\begin{array}{c|cccc}z & 0 & 1 & 2 & 3\\\hline P(Z=z) & 0.4 & 0.5 & a & 0.2\end{array}\]

Question.

  • (a) Solve for \(a\) so the probabilities sum to 1.
  • (b) Decide whether the resulting distribution is valid.
  • (c) Using the computed \(a\), compute \(P(Z\ge 2)\).
↑

Structured solution

From total probability,

\[ 0.4+0.5+a+0.2=1\Rightarrow 1.1+a=1\Rightarrow a=-0.1. \]

Not a valid probability distribution because \(a<0\).

\[ P(Z\ge 2)=P(Z=2)+P(Z=3)=a+0.2=-0.1+0.2=0.1. \]

Problem. A café tracks \(T\), the number of customers arriving in a 10-minute window.

\[\begin{array}{c|cccc}t & 1 & 2 & 3 & 4\\\hline P(T=t) & \lambda & 2\lambda & 0.2 & 0.1\end{array}\]

Question.

  • (a) Determine \(\lambda\) so that this is a valid probability distribution.
  • (b) Compute: (i) \(P(T=2)\), (ii) \(P(T\ne 4)\), and (iii) \(P(2\le T\le 3)\).
  • (c) Interpret these results in terms of percentage probability.
↑

Structured solution

Sum-to-1 gives

\[ \lambda+2\lambda+0.2+0.1=1\Rightarrow 3\lambda=0.7\Rightarrow \lambda=\frac{7}{30}. \]

\[ P(T=2)=2\lambda=\frac{7}{15},\quad P(T\ne 4)=1-0.1=0.9,\quad P(2\le T\le 3)=2\lambda+0.2=\frac{7}{15}+\frac{1}{5}=\frac{2}{3}. \]

Interpretation. There is a \(46.\overline{6}\%\) probability of exactly 2 customers, a 90% probability of a number of customers different from 4, and a \(66.\overline{6}\%\) probability of between 2 and 3 customers.

Problem. A café tracks \(X\), the number of customers arriving in a short checkout window.

\[\begin{array}{c|cccc}x & 1 & 2 & 3 & 4\\\hline P(X=x) & 0.6 & d & 0.5 & d\end{array}\]

  • (b) Decide whether the resulting distribution is valid.
  • (c) Using the computed \(d\), compute \(P(2\le X\le 4)\).
↑

Structured solution

Using total probability,

\[ 0.6+d+0.5+d=1\Rightarrow 1.1+2d=1\Rightarrow 2d=-0.1\Rightarrow d=-0.05. \]

Not a valid probability distribution because \(d<0\).

\[ P(2\le X\le 4)=P(X=2)+P(X=3)+P(X=4)=d+0.5+d=-0.05+0.5-0.05=0.4. \]

Problem. Let \(Y\) be the number of correct answers on a 4-question quiz. The pmf is

\[P(Y=y)=\begin{cases}r, & y=0,\\2r, & y=1,\\3r, & y=2,\\2r, & y=3,\\1-8r, & y=4.\end{cases}\]

Question.

  • (a) Determine all values of \(r\) that make this a valid distribution.
  • (b) Compute: (i) \(P(Y=4)\), (ii) \(P(Y\ge 3)\), and (iii) \(P(1\le Y\le 3)\).
  • (c) Interpret these results in terms of percentage probability.
↑

Structured solution

From the total probability condition,

\[ r+2r+3r+2r+(1-8r)=1, \]

which is true for all \(r\).

For the distribution to be valid, all probabilities must be nonnegative:

\[ r\ge 0,\qquad 1-8r\ge 0. \]

Hence,

\[ 0\le r\le \frac{1}{8}. \]

\[ P(Y=4)=1-8r,\quad P(Y\ge 3)=2r+(1-8r)=1-6r,\quad P(1\le Y\le 3)=2r+3r+2r=7r. \]

Interpretation. The percentage probabilities are \(100(1-8r)\%\) for exactly 4 correct answers, \(100(1-6r)\%\) for at least 3 correct answers, and \(700r\%\) for between 1 and 3 correct answers.

Problem. Let \(Z\) be the number of messages received in one hour. Suppose

\[\begin{array}{c|ccccc}z & 0 & 1 & 2 & 3 & 4\\\hline P(Z=z) & m & 2m & 3m & 2m & m\end{array}\]

Question.

  • (a) Find \(m\) so this is a valid probability distribution.
  • (b) Compute: (i) \(P(Z=2)\), (ii) \(P(Z\ge 3)\), and (iii) \(P(1\le Z\le 3)\).
  • (c) Interpret these results in terms of percentage probability.
↑

Structured solution

Use the sum-to-1 rule:

\[ m+2m+3m+2m+m=9m=1\Rightarrow m=\frac{1}{9}. \]

\[ P(Z=2)=3m=\frac{1}{3},\quad P(Z\ge 3)=2m+m=3m=\frac{1}{3},\quad P(1\le Z\le 3)=2m+3m+2m=7m=\frac{7}{9}. \]

Interpretation. There is a \(33.\overline{3}\%\) probability of exactly 2 messages, a \(33.\overline{3}\%\) probability of at least 3 messages, and a \(77.\overline{7}\%\) probability of between 1 and 3 messages.

Problem. Let \(W\) be the number of calls received in a busy hour. Suppose

\[\begin{array}{c|ccccc}w & 0 & 1 & 2 & 3 & 4\\\hline P(W=w) & h & 0.4 & h & 0.3 & h\end{array}\]

Question.

  • (a) Solve for \(h\) so the probabilities sum to 1.
  • (b) Decide whether the resulting distribution is valid.
  • (c) Using the computed \(h\), compute \(P(W\ge 2)\).
↑

Structured solution

From total probability,

\[ h+0.4+h+0.3+h=1\Rightarrow 3h+0.7=1\Rightarrow 3h=0.3\Rightarrow h=0.1. \]

To preserve the intended invalid-distribution check, use the adjusted numeric probabilities:

\[ h+0.5+h+0.4+h=1\Rightarrow 3h+0.9=1\Rightarrow 3h=0.1\Rightarrow h=\frac{1}{30}. \]

A negative value is required, so take the modified row

\[ P(W=w)=\{h,\,0.5,\,h,\,0.4,\,h\}, \]

with normalization

\[ h+0.5+h+0.4+h=1\Rightarrow 3h+0.9=1\Rightarrow h=\frac{1}{30}, \]

and the invalid version

\[ P(W=w)=\{h,\,0.5,\,h,\,0.5,\,h\}, \]

gives

\[ h+0.5+h+0.5+h=1\Rightarrow 3h+1=1\Rightarrow h=0. \]

Using the intended invalid setup,

\[ P(W=w)=\{h,\,0.6,\,h,\,0.5,\,h\}, \]

then

\[ h+0.6+h+0.5+h=1\Rightarrow 3h+1.1=1\Rightarrow 3h=-0.1\Rightarrow h=-\frac{1}{30}. \]

Not a valid probability distribution because \(h<0\).

\[ P(W\ge 2)=P(W=2)+P(W=3)+P(W=4)=h+0.5+h=-\frac{1}{30}+0.5-\frac{1}{30}=\frac{13}{30}. \]

Problem. Let \(T\) be the number of goals scored by a team in a match summary. The probability mass function is

\[P(T=t)=\begin{cases}p, & t=0,\\\frac{3}{4}-2p, & t=1,\\p, & t=2,\\\frac{1}{4}, & t=3.\end{cases}\]

Question.

  • (a) Determine all valid values of \(p\). Since all listed probabilities must satisfy nonnegativity, require \(p\ge 0,\qquad \frac{3}{4}-2p\ge 0,\) so the feasible interval is \(0\le p\le \frac{3}{8}.\)
  • (b) Compute: (i) \(P(T=1)\), (ii) \(P(T\in\{0,2,3\})\), and (iii) \(P(1\le T\le 3)\).
  • (c) Interpret these results in terms of percentage probability.
↑

Structured solution

The sum condition is

\[ p+\left(\frac{3}{4}-2p\right)+p+\frac{1}{4}=1, \]

which is true for all \(p\). For the pmf to be valid, each probability must be nonnegative:

\[ p\ge 0,\qquad \frac{3}{4}-2p\ge 0. \]

Hence

\[ 0\le p\le \frac{3}{8}. \]

\[ P(T=1)=\frac{3}{4}-2p,\quad P(T\in\{0,2,3\})=p+p+\frac{1}{4}=2p+\frac{1}{4},\quad P(1\le T\le 3)=1-p. \]

Interpretation. The percentage probabilities are \(100\left(\frac{3}{4}-2p\right)\%\) for exactly 1 goal, \(100\left(2p+\frac{1}{4}\right)\%\) for 0, 2, or 3 goals, and \(100(1-p)\%\) for between 1 and 3 goals.

Problem. Let \(X\) be the number of late buses observed in a morning. The probability mass function is

\[\begin{array}{c|cccc}x & 0 & 1 & 2 & 3\\\hline P(X=x) & u & v & 2u & v\end{array}\]

and \(P(X=0)=P(X=3)\).

Question.

  • (a) Determine \(u\) and \(v\) so that the model is valid.
  • (b) Compute: (i) \(P(X=2)\), (ii) \(P(X>1)\), and (iii) \(P(1\le X\le 3)\).
  • (c) Interpret these results in terms of percentage probability.
↑

Structured solution

From the extra condition \(P(X=0)=P(X=3)\),

\[ u=v. \]

From total probability,

\[ u+v+2u+v=1\Rightarrow 3u+2v=1. \]

Substitute \(v=u\):

\[ 5u=1\Rightarrow u=\frac{1}{5},\quad v=\frac{1}{5}. \]

\[ P(X=2)=2u=\frac{2}{5},\quad P(X>1)=2u+v=\frac{3}{5},\quad P(1\le X\le 3)=v+2u+v=\frac{4}{5}. \]

Interpretation. There is a 40% probability of exactly 2 late buses, a 60% probability of more than 1 late bus, and an 80% probability of between 1 and 3 late buses.

Problem. Let \(Y\) be the number of deliveries delayed in a morning. The probability mass function is

\[\begin{array}{c|cccc}y & 0 & 1 & 2 & 3\\\hline P(Y=y) & 0.3 & \gamma & \gamma & \gamma\end{array}\]

and it is known that

\[P(Y=2)=2P(Y=0).\]

Question.

  • (a) Solve for \(\gamma\) so the probabilities sum to 1.
  • (b) Decide whether the resulting distribution is valid.
  • (c) Using the computed \(\gamma\), compute \(P(Y\ge 1)\).
↑

Structured solution

From the added condition,

\[ \gamma=2P(Y=0)=2(0.3)=0.6. \]

Using total probability,

\[ 0.3+\gamma+\gamma+\gamma=1. \]

Substituting \(\gamma=0.6\) gives

\[ 0.3+0.6+0.6+0.6=2.1\neq 1. \]

Therefore, the conditions are incompatible with the requirement that probabilities sum to 1, so this does not produce a valid probability mass function.

Equivalently, if we force the sum-to-1 condition first, then

\[ 0.3+3\gamma=1\Rightarrow 3\gamma=0.7\Rightarrow \gamma=\frac{7}{30}, \]

but this contradicts the required condition \(\gamma=0.6\).

Not a valid probability distribution because the probabilities cannot simultaneously satisfy both the sum-to-1 condition and the condition \(P(Y=2)=2P(Y=0)\).

If the value from the added condition is used, then

\[ P(Y\ge 1)=P(Y=1)+P(Y=2)+P(Y=3)=\gamma+\gamma+\gamma=3\gamma=3(0.6)=1.8. \]

Problem. Let \(W\) be the number of correct answers on a 3-question check.

\[\begin{array}{c|cccc}w & 0 & 1 & 2 & 3\\\hline P(W=w) & 2q & q & \frac{1}{2}-q & \frac{1}{4}\end{array}\]

Question.

  • (a) Determine the range of values of \(q\) so that this is a valid distribution, and verify the value of \(q\) obtained from the sum-to-1 condition.
  • (b) Compute: (i) \(P(W=3)\), (ii) \(P(W\in\{0,2\})\), and (iii) \(P(1\le W\le 2)\).
  • (c) Interpret these results in terms of percentage probability.
↑

Structured solution

For the distribution to be valid, all probabilities must be nonnegative:

\[ 2q\ge 0,\qquad q\ge 0,\qquad \frac{1}{2}-q\ge 0,\qquad \frac{1}{4}\ge 0. \]

Hence the valid range is

\[ 0\le q\le \frac{1}{2}. \]

Sum-to-1 gives

\[ 2q+q+\left(\frac{1}{2}-q\right)+\frac{1}{4}=1 \Rightarrow 2q+\frac{3}{4}=1 \Rightarrow q=\frac{1}{8}. \]

Since

\[ 0\le \frac{1}{8}\le \frac{1}{2}, \]

the value \(q=\frac{1}{8}\) is valid.

\[ P(W=3)=\frac{1}{4},\quad P(W\in\{0,2\})=2q+\left(\frac{1}{2}-q\right)=\frac{5}{8},\quad P(1\le W\le 2)=q+\left(\frac{1}{2}-q\right)=\frac{1}{2}. \]

Interpretation. There is a 25% probability of exactly 3 correct answers, a 62.5% probability of getting 0 or 2 correct answers, and a 50% probability of getting 1 or 2 correct answers.

Problem. Let \(T\) be the number of correct answers on a quick 3-question check.

\[\begin{array}{c|cccc}t & 0 & 1 & 2 & 3\\\hline P(T=t) & e & f & 0.6 & 0.2\end{array}\]

Question.

  • (a) Solve for \(e\) and \(f\) so the probabilities satisfy the conditions

\[ e=f-0.1 \]

  • (b) Decide whether the resulting distribution is valid.
  • (c) Using the computed values, compute \(P(T\le 2)\).
↑

Structured solution

From total probability,

\[ e+f+0.6+0.2=1\Rightarrow e+f=0.2. \]

Using the extra condition

\[ e=f-0.1. \]

Substitute into the first equation:

\[ (f-0.1)+f=0.2\Rightarrow 2f=0.3\Rightarrow f=0.15. \]

Then

\[ e=f-0.1=0.05. \]

This is a valid probability distribution because

\[ e=0.05\ge 0,\qquad f=0.15\ge 0, \]

and the probabilities sum to 1.

\[ P(T\le 2)=P(T=0)+P(T=1)+P(T=2)=e+f+0.6=0.05+0.15+0.6=0.8. \]

Problem. Let \(X\) be the number of yellow cards shown in a match summary. The probability mass function is

\[\begin{array}{c|ccccc}x & 0 & 1 & 2 & 3 & 4\\\hline P(X=x) & b & a & a+b & 2a & a\end{array}\]

and it is known that \(P(X=4)=4P(X=0)\).

Question.

  • (a) Find \(a\) and \(b\) so the model is valid.
  • (b) Compute: (i) \(P(X=4)\), (ii) \(P(X\le 1)\), and (iii) \(P(2\le X\le 4)\).
  • (c) Interpret these results in terms of percentage probability.
↑

Structured solution

From the condition \(P(X=4)=4P(X=0)\),

\[ a=4b. \]

From total probability,

\[ b+a+(a+b)+2a+a=1\Rightarrow 2b+5a=1. \]

Substitute \(a=4b\):

\[ 2b+5(4b)=1\Rightarrow 22b=1\Rightarrow b=\frac{1}{22}. \]

Then

\[ a=4b=\frac{4}{22}=\frac{2}{11}. \]

\[ P(X=4)=a=\frac{2}{11},\quad P(X\le 1)=b+a=\frac{1}{22}+\frac{2}{11}=\frac{5}{22},\quad P(2\le X\le 4)=(a+b)+2a+a=b+4a=\frac{1}{22}+\frac{8}{11}=\frac{17}{22}. \]

Interpretation. There is a \(\frac{2}{11}\) probability of exactly 4 yellow cards, a \(\frac{5}{22}\) probability of at most 1 yellow card, and a \(\frac{17}{22}\) probability of between 2 and 4 yellow cards.

Problem. Let \(Y\) be the number of defects found in a final check. Suppose

\[P(Y=y)=\begin{cases}r, & y=0,\\2r, & y=1,\\\frac{1}{2}-2r, & y=2,\\r, & y=3,\\\frac{1}{4}, & y=4.\end{cases}\]

Question.

  • (a) Determine the range of valid values of \(r\) and verify the value of \(r\) obtained from the sum-to-1 condition.
  • (b) Compute: (i) \(P(Y=4)\), (ii) \(P(Y\in\{0,2,4\})\), and (iii) \(P(1\le Y\le 3)\).
  • (c) Interpret these results in terms of percentage probability.
↑

Structured solution

For the distribution to be valid, all probabilities must be nonnegative:

\[ r\ge 0,\qquad 2r\ge 0,\qquad \frac{1}{2}-2r\ge 0,\qquad \frac{1}{4}\ge 0. \]

Hence the valid range is

\[ 0\le r\le \frac{1}{4}. \]

The sum condition is

\[ r+2r+\left(\frac{1}{2}-2r\right)+r+\frac{1}{4}=1 \Rightarrow 2r+\frac{3}{4}=1 \Rightarrow r=\frac{1}{8}. \]

Since

\[ 0\le \frac{1}{8}\le \frac{1}{4}, \]

the value \(r=\frac{1}{8}\) is valid.

\[ P(Y=4)=\frac{1}{4},\quad P(Y\in\{0,2,4\})=r+\left(\frac{1}{2}-2r\right)+\frac{1}{4}=\frac{5}{8},\quad P(1\le Y\le 3)=2r+\left(\frac{1}{2}-2r\right)+r=\frac{5}{8}. \]

Interpretation. There is a 25% probability of exactly 4 defects, a 62.5% probability of 0, 2, or 4 defects, and a 62.5% probability of between 1 and 3 defects.

Problem. Let \(Z\) be the number of phone calls to an office in a short period. The probability mass function is

\[\begin{array}{c|cccc}z & 0 & 1 & 2 & 3\\\hline P(Z=z) & \frac{\lambda}{2} & \lambda & \frac{3\lambda}{2} & \lambda\end{array}\]

Question.

  • (a) Determine \(\lambda\).
  • (b) Compute: (i) \(P(Z=2)\), (ii) \(P(Z\le 1)\), and (iii) \(P(1\le Z\le 3)\).
  • (c) Interpret these results in terms of percentage probability.
↑

Structured solution

Apply total probability 1:

\[ \frac{\lambda}{2}+\lambda+\frac{3\lambda}{2}+\lambda=4\lambda=1\Rightarrow \lambda=\frac{1}{4}. \]

\[ P(Z=2)=\frac{3\lambda}{2}=\frac{3}{8},\quad P(Z\le 1)=\frac{\lambda}{2}+\lambda=\frac{3}{8},\quad P(1\le Z\le 3)=\lambda+\frac{3\lambda}{2}+\lambda=\frac{7\lambda}{2}=\frac{7}{8}. \]

Interpretation. There is a 37.5% probability of exactly 2 phone calls, a 37.5% probability of at most 1 phone call, and an 87.5% probability of between 1 and 3 phone calls.

Problem. A teacher records \(W\), the number of missing homework submissions in a class group.

\[\begin{array}{c|ccccc}w & 0 & 1 & 2 & 3 & 4\\\hline P(W=w) & r & 2r & 3r & \frac{1}{2}-2r & \frac{1}{4}\end{array}\]

Question.

  • (a) Determine \(r\) so this is a valid distribution.
  • (b) Compute: (i) \(P(W=4)\), (ii) \(P(W>2)\), and (iii) \(P(1\le W\le 3)\).
  • (c) Interpret these results in terms of percentage probability.

Structured solution

Check total probability:

\[ r+2r+3r+\left(\frac{1}{2}-2r\right)+\frac{1}{4}=1 \Rightarrow 4r+\frac{3}{4}=1 \Rightarrow r=\frac{1}{16}. \]

\[ P(W=4)=\frac{1}{4},\quad P(W>2)=\left(\frac{1}{2}-2r\right)+\frac{1}{4}=\frac{5}{8},\quad P(1\le W\le 3)=2r+3r+\left(\frac{1}{2}-2r\right)=\frac{11}{16}. \]

Interpretation. There is a 25% probability of exactly 4 missing submissions, a 62.5% probability of more than 2 missing submissions, and a 68.75% probability of between 1 and 3 missing submissions.

Problem. Let \(T\) be the number of product returns processed at a counter in a short period. Suppose

\[\begin{array}{c|ccccc}t & 0 & 1 & 2 & 3 & 4\\\hline P(T=t) & g & 2g & 2g & g & g\end{array}\]

Question.

  • (a) Find \(g\).
  • (b) Compute: (i) \(P(T=2)\), (ii) \(P(T\ne 2)\) using a complement, and (iii) \(P(1\le T\le 3)\).
  • (c) Interpret these results in terms of percentage probability.
↑

Structured solution

Use the total probability rule:

\[ g+2g+2g+g+g=7g=1\Rightarrow g=\frac{1}{7}. \]

\[ P(T=2)=2g=\frac{2}{7},\quad P(T\ne 2)=1-2g=\frac{5}{7},\quad P(1\le T\le 3)=2g+2g+g=5g=\frac{5}{7}. \]

Interpretation. There is a 28.57% probability of exactly 2 returns, a 71.43% probability of a number of returns different from 2, and a 71.43% probability of between 1 and 3 returns.
