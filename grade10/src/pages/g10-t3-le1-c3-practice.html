Problem.

Let $X$ be a continuous random variable with density
\[ f(x)=\frac{x}{18},\quad 0\le x\le 6, \] and $f(x)=0$ otherwise.

Questions/Tasks.

(a) State the support of $X$. (b) Verify geometrically that $f$ is a valid PDF. (c) Compute $P(0\le X\le 3)$.

↑

Prompt (a). State the support of $X$.

The support is $[0,6]$.

↑

Prompt (b). Verify geometrically that $f$ is a valid PDF.

The graph is a single straight line from $(0,0)$ to $(6,\tfrac13)$.
So total area is triangle area:
\[ \frac12(6)\left(\frac13\right)=1. \] Hence $f$ is valid.

↑

Prompt (c). Compute $P(0\le X\le 3)$.

On $[0,3]$, the area is a smaller triangle with height $f(3)=\frac16$:
\[ P(0\le X\le 3)=\frac12(3)\left(\frac16\right)=\frac14. \]

↑

The model increases linearly, so larger values in the support are relatively more likely than very small values.

Problem.

A random variable $Y$ has density
\[ f(y)=\begin{cases} \dfrac{y}{16}, & 0\le y\le 4,\\ \dfrac{8-y}{16}, & 4\le y\le 8,\\ 0, & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases} \]

Questions/Tasks.

(a) Verify this is a valid PDF. (b) Compute $P(2\le Y\le 6)$. (c) Identify the most likely region and explain why.

↑

Prompt (a). Verify this is a valid PDF.

The graph is a triangle with base $8$ and peak height $f(4)=\frac14$.
\[ \text{Area}=\frac12(8)\left(\frac14\right)=1. \] So it is valid.

↑

Prompt (b). Compute $P(2\le Y\le 6)$.

Use complement:
\[ P(2\le Y\le 6)=1-P(0\le Y\le 2)-P(6\le Y\le 8). \] Each side area is a triangle with base $2$ and height $\frac18$:
\[ \frac12(2)\left(\frac18\right)=\frac18. \] Hence
\[ P(2\le Y\le 6)=1-\frac18-\frac18=\frac34. \]

↑

Prompt (c). Identify the most likely region and explain why.

Values near $y=4$ are most likely because the density reaches its highest point there.

↑

Values near $y=4$ are most likely because the density reaches its highest point there.

Problem.

A random variable $Z$ has density
\[ f(z)=\begin{cases} \dfrac{z}{20}, & 0\le z\le 4,\\ \dfrac{10-z}{30}, & 4\le z\le 10,\\ 0, & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases} \]

Questions/Tasks.

(a) Verify the model is a valid PDF. (b) Compute $P(1\le Z\le 7)$. (c) Where is $Z$ most likely to be found?

↑

Prompt (a). Verify the model is a valid PDF.

Peak at $z=4$ has height $\frac15$.
Left triangle area: $\frac12(4)(\frac15)=\frac25$.
Right triangle area: $\frac12(6)(\frac15)=\frac35$.
Total $=1$.

↑

Prompt (b). Compute $P(1\le Z\le 7)$.

Compute by complement:
\[ P(1\le Z\le 7)=1-P(0\le Z\le 1)-P(7\le Z\le 10). \] \[ P(0\le Z\le 1)=\frac12(1)\left(\frac1{20}\right)=\frac1{40}. \] At $z=7$, $f(7)=\frac{1}{10}$, so
\[ P(7\le Z\le 10)=\frac12(3)\left(\frac{1}{10}\right)=\frac{3}{20}. \] Therefore
\[ P(1\le Z\le 7)=1-\frac1{40}-\frac3{20}=\frac{33}{40}=0.825. \]

↑

Prompt (c). Where is $Z$ most likely to be found?

The peak at $z=4$ marks the most likely region, but the longer right side spreads mass across larger values.

↑

The peak at $z=4$ marks the most likely region, but the longer right side spreads mass across larger values.

Problem.

Let $T$ have density
\[ f(t)=\begin{cases} \dfrac{t}{9}, & 0\le t\le 3,\\ \dfrac{6-t}{9}, & 3\le t\le 6,\\ 0, & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases} \]

Questions/Tasks.

(a) Verify validity. (b) Compute $P(2\le T\le 5)$ by splitting at the peak. (c) Compare $P(1\le T\le 2)$ and $P(4\le T\le 5)$.

↑

Prompt (a). Verify validity.

Triangle base $6$, height $\frac13$ gives area
\[ \frac12(6)\left(\frac13\right)=1. \]

↑

Prompt (b). Compute $P(2\le T\le 5)$ by splitting at the peak.

Split $[2,5]$ into $[2,3]$ and $[3,5]$.
\[ A_{[2,3]}=\frac{\left(\frac29+\frac13\right)}{2}\cdot1=\frac{5}{18}, \quad A_{[3,5]}=\frac{\left(\frac13+\frac19\right)}{2}\cdot2=\frac{4}{9}. \] So
\[ P(2\le T\le 5)=\frac{5}{18}+\frac{4}{9}=\frac{13}{18}. \]

↑

Prompt (c). Compare $P(1\le T\le 2)$ and $P(4\le T\le 5)$.

By symmetry around $t=3$, these two one-unit intervals have equal probability.

↑

The central region around $t=3$ is most probable because the graph is highest there.

Problem.

Let $W$ have density
\[ f(w)=\begin{cases} \dfrac{w}{20}, & 0\le w\le 5,\\ \dfrac{8-w}{12}, & 5\le w\le 8,\\ 0, & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases} \]

Questions/Tasks.

(a) Verify this is a valid PDF. (b) Compute $P(3\le W\le 7)$ by splitting at $w=5$. (c) Which is larger: $P(2\le W\le 4)$ or $P(6\le W\le 8)$? Explain from the graph.

↑

Prompt (a). Verify this is a valid PDF.

Peak height is $f(5)=\frac14$.
\[ \text{Area}=\frac12(5)\left(\frac14\right)+\frac12(3)\left(\frac14\right)=\frac58+\frac38=1. \]

↑

Prompt (b). Compute $P(3\le W\le 7)$ by splitting at $w=5$.

\[ A_{[3,5]}=\frac{\left(\frac3{20}+\frac14\right)}{2}\cdot2=\frac25, \quad A_{[5,7]}=\frac{\left(\frac14+\frac1{12}\right)}{2}\cdot2=\frac13. \] Hence
\[ P(3\le W\le 7)=\frac25+\frac13=\frac{11}{15}. \]

↑

Prompt (c). Which is larger: $P(2\le W\le 4)$ or $P(6\le W\le 8)$? Explain from the graph.

\[ P(2\le W\le 4)=\frac{\left(\frac1{10}+\frac15\right)}{2}\cdot2=\frac3{10}, \quad P(6\le W\le 8)=\frac12(2)\left(\frac16\right)=\frac16. \] So $P(2\le W\le 4)>P(6\le W\le 8)$.

↑

Because the peak is closer to 5 and the left side is wider, mid-left intervals can hold more area than far-right intervals.

↑

\[ P(2\le X\le 4)=\frac{5}{9}. \]

↑

Prompt (c). Explain why the peak corresponds to the most likely region.

The density is highest near $x=3$, so intervals around $3$ capture the greatest probability mass.

↑

Peak is $f(4)=4k$ (continuous at $4$).

\[ P(3\le Y\le 8)=\frac7{40}+\frac{8}{15}=\frac{17}{24}. \]

↑

Prompt (c). Identify the most likely region from the shape.

The mode is still at $y=4$, but the longer right tail means probability extends farther to the right.

↑

Problem.

A variable $B$ on $[0,12]$ has density
\[ f(b)=\begin{cases} \dfrac{k}{5}b, & 0\le b\le 5,\\ k-\dfrac{k}{12}(b-5), & 5\le b\le 9,\\ \dfrac{2k}{9}(12-b), & 9\le b\le 12,\\ 0, & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases} \]

Questions/Tasks.

(a) Determine $k$. (b) Compute $P(4\le B\le 10)$. (c) Explain how the peak location affects where most mass sits.

↑

Prompt (a). Determine $k$.

Areas:
\[ A_{[0,5]}=\frac12(5)(k)=\frac{5k}{2}, \quad A_{[5,9]}=\frac{(k+\frac{2k}{3})}{2}\cdot4=\frac{10k}{3}, \quad A_{[9,12]}=\frac12(3)\left(\frac{2k}{3}\right)=k. \] So
\[ \frac{5k}{2}+\frac{10k}{3}+k=\frac{41k}{6}=1 \Rightarrow k=\frac{6}{41}. \]

↑

Prompt (b). Compute $P(4\le B\le 10)$.

Split into $[4,5]$, $[5,9]$, and $[9,10]$.
\[ A_{[4,5]}=\frac{\left(\frac{4k}{5}+k\right)}{2}\cdot1=\frac{9k}{10}, \quad A_{[5,9]}=\frac{10k}{3}, \] \[ A_{[9,10]}=\frac{\left(\frac{2k}{3}+\frac{4k}{9}\right)}{2}\cdot1=\frac{5k}{9}. \] Hence
\[ P(4\le B\le 10)=\frac{9k}{10}+\frac{10k}{3}+\frac{5k}{9}=\frac{431}{615}\approx0.701. \]

↑

Prompt (c). Explain how the peak location affects where most mass sits.

The high central part from about $5$ to $9$ concentrates much of the probability mass.

↑

The high central part from about $5$ to $9$ concentrates much of the probability mass.

Problem.

Two models for waiting time on $[0,8]$ are:
\[ f_A(x)=\begin{cases} \dfrac{x}{16}, & 0\le x\le 4,\\ \dfrac{8-x}{16}, & 4\le x\le 8, \end{cases} \qquad f_B(x)=\begin{cases} \dfrac{x}{24}, & 0\le x\le 6,\\ \dfrac{8-x}{8}, & 6\le x\le 8. \end{cases} \]

Questions/Tasks.

(a) Show each is a valid PDF. (b) Compare $P_A(3\le X\le 5)$ and $P_B(3\le X\le 5)$. (c) Explain how moving the peak changes the most likely region.

↑

Prompt (a). Show each is a valid PDF.

Each has base $8$ and height $\frac14$, so each total area is
\[ \frac12(8)\left(\frac14\right)=1. \]

↑

Prompt (b). Compare $P_A(3\le X\le 5)$ and $P_B(3\le X\le 5)$.

For model $A$:
\[ P_A(3\le X\le 5)=2\cdot\frac{\left(\frac{3}{16}+\frac14\right)}{2}\cdot1=\frac{7}{16}. \] For model $B$, on $[3,5]$ we are on the rising side only:
\[ P_B(3\le X\le 5)=\frac{\left(\frac18+\frac{5}{24}\right)}{2}\cdot2=\frac13. \] So $P_A(3\le X\le 5)>P_B(3\le X\le 5)$.

↑

Prompt (c). Explain how moving the peak changes the most likely region.

Peak $4$ (model $A$) centers mass near the middle, while peak $6$ (model $B$) shifts more mass to larger values.

↑

Shifting the peak right moves the most likely region right and reduces central-middle probabilities.

Problem.

For $0<p<10$, define
\[ f_p(x)=\begin{cases} \dfrac{x}{5p}, & 0\le x\le p,\\ \dfrac{10-x}{5(10-p)}, & p\le x\le 10,\\ 0, & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases} \]

Questions/Tasks.

(a) Show $f_p$ is a valid PDF for any $0<p<10$. (b) Express $P(X\le p)$ in terms of $p$. (c) Explain how changing $p$ affects symmetry and which side carries more probability.

↑

Prompt (a). Show $f_p$ is a valid PDF for any $0<p<10$.

Peak value is always $f_p(p)=\frac15$.
Total area is triangle area with base $10$ and height $\frac15$:
\[ \frac12(10)\left(\frac15\right)=1. \]

↑

Prompt (b). Express $P(X\le p)$ in terms of $p$.

Left side is a triangle with base $p$ and height $\frac15$:
\[ P(X\le p)=\frac12 p\left(\frac15\right)=\frac{p}{10}. \]

Prompt (c). Explain how changing $p$ affects symmetry and which side carries more probability.

If $p=5$, the density is symmetric.
If $p<5$, the peak is left-shifted and more mass lies on the right.
If $p>5$, the peak is right-shifted and more mass lies on the left.

↑

The peak location controls which side of the support receives more total area, even though peak height is fixed.

Problem.

A model on $[0,8]$ is
\[ f(x)=\begin{cases} \dfrac{h}{3}x, & 0\le x\le 3,\\ h-\dfrac{h}{5}(x-3), & 3\le x\le 8,\\ 0, & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases} \] where $h>0$.

Questions/Tasks.

(a) Determine $h$ so $f$ is a valid PDF. (b) Find $P(2\le X\le 6)$. (c) Compare $P(0\le X\le 2)$ with $P(6\le X\le 8)$ and interpret asymmetry.

↑

Prompt (a). Determine $h$ so $f$ is a valid PDF.

The graph is triangular with base $8$ and height $h$, so
\[ \frac12(8)h=1\Rightarrow h=\frac14. \]

↑

Prompt (b). Find $P(2\le X\le 6)$.

With $h=\frac14$:
\[ A_{[2,3]}=\frac{\left(\frac16+\frac14\right)}{2}\cdot1=\frac{5}{24}, \quad A_{[3,6]}=\frac{\left(\frac14+\frac1{10}\right)}{2}\cdot3=\frac{21}{40}. \] Hence
\[ P(2\le X\le 6)=\frac{5}{24}+\frac{21}{40}=\frac{11}{15}. \]

↑

Prompt (c). Compare $P(0\le X\le 2)$ with $P(6\le X\le 8)$ and interpret asymmetry.

\[ P(0\le X\le 2)=\frac12(2)\left(\frac16\right)=\frac16, \quad P(6\le X\le 8)=\frac12(2)\left(\frac1{10}\right)=\frac1{10}. \] So $P(0\le X\le 2)>P(6\le X\le 8)$.

↑

Even with one peak, unequal left/right widths allocate probability mass differently.

Problem.

For $0<p<12$, define
\[ f(x)=\begin{cases} kx, & 0\le x\le p,\\ \dfrac{kp}{12-p}(12-x), & p\le x\le 12,\\ 0, & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases} \]

Questions/Tasks.

(a) Determine $k$ in terms of $p$. (b) Show that the peak height is independent of $p$. (c) Find $P(X\le 6)$ for the case $p=4$. (d) Compare $P(X\le 6)$ for $p=4$ and $p=8$. (e) Explain clearly how moving the peak changes the most likely region and concentration of probability mass.

↑

Prompt (a). Determine $k$ in terms of $p$.

The peak value is $f(p)=kp$.
Total area is sum of two triangles:
\[ \frac12 p(kp)+\frac12(12-p)(kp)=6kp=1 \Rightarrow k=\frac{1}{6p}. \]

↑

Prompt (b). Show that the peak height is independent of $p$.

Peak height is
\[ f(p)=kp=\frac{1}{6}, \] so it does not depend on $p$.

↑

Prompt (c). Find $P(X\le 6)$ for the case $p=4$.

For $p=4$, $k=\frac{1}{24}$ and
\[ f(x)=\begin{cases} \dfrac{x}{24}, & 0\le x\le 4,\\ \dfrac{12-x}{48}, & 4\le x\le 12. \end{cases} \] Then
\[ P(X\le 6)=\underbrace{\frac12(4)\left(\frac16\right)}_{[0,4]}+\underbrace{\frac{\left(\frac16+\frac18\right)}{2}\cdot2}_{[4,6]}=\frac13+\frac{7}{24}=\frac58. \]

↑

Prompt (d). Compare $P(X\le 6)$ for $p=4$ and $p=8$.

For $p=8$, by symmetry of the setup around $x=6$, we get
\[ P(X\le 6)=\frac38. \] So shifting the peak right from $4$ to $8$ lowers left-half probability.

↑

Prompt (e). Explain clearly how moving the peak changes the most likely region and concentration of probability mass.

Moving the peak changes which region is most likely: left-shifted peaks concentrate area earlier, right-shifted peaks concentrate area later.

↑

The highest density marks the most likely region, but interval probability is always determined by area under the piecewise linear graph.

Problem.

Let $0<q<p$. Define
\[ f(x)=\begin{cases} \dfrac{h}{q}x, & 0\le x\le q,\\ \dfrac{h}{p-q}(p-x), & q\le x\le p,\\ 0, & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases} \]

Questions/Tasks.

(a) Determine $h$ so that $f$ is a valid PDF. (b) Find $P(X\le q)$ in terms of $p$ and $q$. (c) Find $P\!\left(\frac q2\le X\le q\right)$ and $P\!\left(q\le X\le\frac{p+q}{2}\right)$. (d) For fixed $p$, explain how increasing $q$ changes the shape and concentration of mass.

↑

Prompt (a). Determine $h$ so that $f$ is a valid PDF.

The graph is triangular with base $p$ and peak height $h$, so
\[ \frac12(p)(h)=1\Rightarrow h=\frac{2}{p}. \]

↑

Prompt (b). Find $P(X\le q)$ in terms of $p$ and $q$.

Left area up to the peak is a triangle:
\[ P(X\le q)=\frac12(q)(h)=\frac12(q)\left(\frac{2}{p}\right)=\frac{q}{p}. \]

↑

Prompt (c). Find $P\!\left(\frac q2\le X\le q\right)$ and $P\!\left(q\le X\le\frac{p+q}{2}\right)$.

On $\left[\frac q2,q\right]$, heights are $\frac h2$ and $h$:
\[ P\!\left(\frac q2\le X\le q\right)=\frac{\left(\frac h2+h\right)}{2}\cdot\frac q2=\frac{3hq}{8}=\frac{3q}{4p}. \] On $\left[q,\frac{p+q}{2}\right]$, heights are $h$ and $\frac h2$:
\[ P\!\left(q\le X\le\frac{p+q}{2}\right)=\frac{\left(h+\frac h2\right)}{2}\cdot\frac{p-q}{2}=\frac{3h(p-q)}{8}=\frac{3(p-q)}{4p}. \]

↑

Prompt (d). For fixed $p$, explain how increasing $q$ changes the shape and concentration of mass.

With $p$ fixed, increasing $q$ moves the peak right. The left side widens and the right side narrows, so a larger fraction of total area lies on the left of the peak ($\frac{q}{p}$ increases).

↑

The peak marks the most likely region, but interval probabilities are still determined by area under the piecewise linear graph.

Problem.

For $0<q<p$, use the family
\[ f_{p,q}(x)=\begin{cases} \dfrac{2x}{pq}, & 0\le x\le q,\\ \dfrac{2(p-x)}{p(p-q)}, & q\le x\le p,\\ 0, & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases} \] Consider two parameter settings:

Prompt (a). Verify that this is a valid PDF for all $0<q<p$.

Peak height is
\[ f_{p,q}(q)=\frac{2}{p}. \] Total area is triangular area:

↑

Prompt (b). For $0<r\le q$, show that $P(X\le r)=\dfrac{r^2}{pq}$.

If $0<r\le q$, the area from $0$ to $r$ is a triangle with height $f(r)=\frac{2r}{pq}$:

From A to B, support gets wider ($p$ increases) and peak moves farther right ($q$ increases). So the distribution becomes more right-skewed in where mass is concentrated, and early-left intervals (like $[0,4]$) capture much less area.

Problem.

Let $a<p<b$ and define
\[ f(x)=\begin{cases} C(x-a), & a\le x\le p,\\ \dfrac{C(p-a)}{b-p}(b-x), & p\le x\le b,\\ 0, & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases} \]

Questions/Tasks.

(a) Determine $C$ in terms of $a,p,b$. (b) Find the peak height $f(p)$ and show how it depends on the support width. (c) Find $P(a\le X\le p)$. (d) Compute \[ P\!\left(\frac{a+p}{2}\le X\le\frac{p+b}{2}\right). \] (e) For $0<d\le\min\{p-a,b-p\}$, compare \[ P(p-d\le X\le p)\quad\text{and}\quad P(p\le X\le p+d), \] and explain how $p-a$ and $b-p$ jointly control which side near the peak carries more mass.

↑

Prompt (a). Determine $C$ in terms of $a,p,b$.

Peak height is
\[ h=f(p)=C(p-a). \] Total area is one triangle with base $b-a$ and height $h$:
\[ \frac12(b-a)h=1 \Rightarrow h=\frac{2}{b-a}. \] Thus
\[ C=\frac{h}{p-a}=\frac{2}{(b-a)(p-a)}. \]

↑

Prompt (b). Find the peak height $f(p)$ and show how it depends on the support width.

From above,
\[ f(p)=h=\frac{2}{b-a}, \] so the peak height depends on total support width $b-a$ (wider support gives lower peak).

↑

Prompt (c). Find $P(a\le X\le p)$.

Left-of-peak probability is left triangle area:
\[ P(a\le X\le p)=\frac12(p-a)h=\frac{p-a}{b-a}. \]

↑

Prompt (d). Compute \[ P\!\left(\frac{a+p}{2}\le X\le\frac{p+b}{2}\right). \]

Split at $p$.
On $\left[\frac{a+p}{2},p\right]$, heights are $\frac h2$ and $h$:
\[ A_L=\frac{\left(\frac h2+h\right)}{2}\cdot\frac{p-a}{2}=\frac{3h(p-a)}{8}. \] On $\left[p,\frac{p+b}{2}\right]$, heights are $h$ and $\frac h2$:
\[ A_R=\frac{\left(h+\frac h2\right)}{2}\cdot\frac{b-p}{2}=\frac{3h(b-p)}{8}. \] So
\[ P\!\left(\frac{a+p}{2}\le X\le\frac{p+b}{2}\right)=A_L+A_R=\frac{3h(b-a)}{8}=\frac34. \]

↑

Prompt (e). For $0<d\le\min\{p-a,b-p\}$, compare \[ P(p-d\le X\le p)\quad\text{and}\quad P(p\le X\le p+d), \] and explain how $p-a$ and $b-p$ jointly control which side near the peak carries more mass.

Left near-peak strip:
\[ P(p-d\le X\le p)=\frac{\left(h\left(1-\frac{d}{p-a}\right)+h\right)}{2}\cdot d =hd\left(1-\frac{d}{2(p-a)}\right). \] Right near-peak strip:
\[ P(p\le X\le p+d)=\frac{\left(h+h\left(1-\frac{d}{b-p}\right)\right)}{2}\cdot d =hd\left(1-\frac{d}{2(b-p)}\right). \] If $p-a<b-p$, then $\frac{1}{p-a}>\frac{1}{b-p}$ so the left expression is smaller; more near-peak mass is on the right. If $p-a>b-p$, the opposite occurs. If equal, the two probabilities are equal.

↑

This model shows that two shape parameters ($p-a$ and $b-p$) jointly control asymmetry: the most likely region is around $p$, but nearby left/right interval probabilities differ according to how steeply each side rises or falls.
