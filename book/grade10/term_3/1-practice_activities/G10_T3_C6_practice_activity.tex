\section{Problem 1: Normal Distribution Left Tail with Positive and Negative z-values}
\textbf{Problem.} In a regional exam linked to trade-and-economics literacy, analysts used school records from multiple districts to estimate that learner scores are approximately normal for classroom modelling, with \(X\) = score, mean 70, and standard deviation 8, so \(X\sim N(70,8^2)\). Find \(P(X<82)\) and \(P(X<60.4)\).\\
\StdLeft{1.50}{P(Z<1.50)}
\StdSym{1.20}{P(Z<-1.20)}{P(Z>1.20)}
\[Z=\frac{X-70}{8}.\]
\[P(X<82)=P\!\left(Z<\frac{82-70}{8}\right)=P(Z<1.50)=0.9332.\]
\[P(X<60.4)=P\!\left(Z<\frac{60.4-70}{8}\right)=P(Z<-1.20)=1-0.8849=0.1151.\]
\textbf{Interpretation.} Economically, about 93.32\% score below 82 and 11.51\% score below 60.4; with mean 70 as the typical score and standard deviation 8 as the usual spread in performance observed across schools.

\section{Problem 2: Normal Distribution Right Tail with Positive and Negative z-values}
\textbf{Problem.} A logistics study of import deliveries at a port used shipment-tracking data to estimate that delivery times are approximately normal for classroom use, with \(X\) = minutes per delivery, mean 56, and standard deviation 5, so \(X\sim N(56,5^2)\). Find \(P(X>63.5)\) and \(P(X>52)\).\\
\StdRight{1.50}{P(Z>1.50)}
\StdComp{1.50}{P(Z<1.50)=0.9332}{P(Z>1.50)=0.0668}
\[Z=\frac{X-56}{5}.\]
\[P(X>63.5)=P\!\left(Z>\frac{63.5-56}{5}\right)=P(Z>1.50)=1-0.9332=0.0668.\]
\[P(X>52)=P\!\left(Z>\frac{52-56}{5}\right)=P(Z>-0.80)=\Phi(0.80)=0.7881.\]
\textbf{Interpretation.} In this trade-logistics setting, 6.68\% of deliveries take more than 63.5 min and 78.81\% take more than 52 min; 56 min is the typical time and 5 min measures normal variation around that average.

\section{Problem 7: Normal Distribution Two Regions with Positive z-bounds}
\textbf{Problem.} An energy regulator analysing electricity affordability used household meter data to estimate an approximately normal model for classroom use, with \(X\) = monthly electricity use (kWh), mean 400, and standard deviation 50, so \(X\sim N(400,50^2)\). \StdTwo{1.00}{1.90}{P(Z<1.00\ \text{or}\ Z>1.90)}

\textbf{Interpretation.} This indicates 87.00\% of households are below 450 kWh or above 495 kWh; the result separates moderate-use households from higher-demand households, using mean 400 and spread 50 estimated from observed meter readings.
