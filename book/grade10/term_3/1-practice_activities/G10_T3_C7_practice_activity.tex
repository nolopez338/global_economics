\section{Problem 1: Left-tail inverse normal (positive and negative z)}
Using school assessment records from districts studying labour-market readiness, analysts estimated that exam scores are approximately normal for classroom modelling; let $X$ be score, with mean 68 and standard deviation 8, so $X\sim N(68,8^2)$.
\subsection*{C7}
\[P(X<x_1)=0.8023,\qquad P(X<x_2)=0.1151.\]
\XNormalLeft{0.85}{x_1}{P(X<x_1)=0.8023}{}
\XNormalLeft{-1.20}{x_2}{P(X<x_2)=0.1151}{}
\[\Phi(z)=0.8023\Rightarrow z_1=0.85,\quad \Phi(z)=0.1151\Rightarrow z_2=-1.20.\]
\[Z=\frac{X-68}{8},\qquad x=68+z(8).\]
\[x_1=74.8,\qquad x_2=58.4.\]
\ZNormalLeft{0.85}{P(Z<0.85)=0.8023}{}
\textbf{Interpretation:} The cutoff values mean about 80.23\% score below 74.8 and 11.51\% below 58.4; 68 is the typical score and 8 shows the usual spread in observed performance.

\section{Problem 2: Right-tail inverse normal (positive and negative z)}
A logistics tracking study of imported shipments estimated that delivery times are approximately normal for classroom use; let $X$ be delivery time in minutes, with mean 40 and standard deviation 6, so $X\sim N(40,6^2)$.
\subsection*{C7}
\[P(X>x_1)=0.0885,\qquad P(X>x_2)=0.7580.\]
\XNormalRight{1.35}{x_1}{P(X>x_1)=0.0885}{}
\[P(X\le x_1)=0.9115\Rightarrow z_1=\Phi^{-1}(0.9115)=1.35.\]
\XNormalRight{-0.70}{x_2}{P(X>x_2)=0.7580}{}
\[P(X\le x_2)=0.2420\Rightarrow z_2=\Phi^{-1}(0.2420)=-0.70.\]
\[Z=\frac{X-40}{6},\quad x=40+z(6).\]
\[x_1=48.1,\qquad x_2=35.8.\]
\ZNormalRight{1.35}{P(Z>1.35)=0.0885}{}
\textbf{Interpretation:} Economically, these cutoffs imply 8.85\% of deliveries exceed 48.1 min and 75.80\% exceed 35.8 min; 40 min is average time and 6 min is typical variation.

\section{Problem 3: Mixed left-tail and right-tail cutoff}
A firm exporting processed foods used monthly sales reports to estimate an approximately normal model for classroom purposes; let $X$ be monthly sales in $\$000, with mean 52 and standard deviation 10, so $X\sim N(52,10^2)$.
\subsection*{C7}
\[P(X<x_1)=0.7088,\qquad P(X>x_2)=0.1841.\]
\[z_1=\Phi^{-1}(0.7088)=0.55.\]
\[P(X\le x_2)=0.8159\Rightarrow z_2=\Phi^{-1}(0.8159)=0.90.\]
\[Z=\frac{X-52}{10},\quad x=52+z(10).\]
\[x_1=57.5,\qquad x_2=61.0.\]
\textbf{Interpretation:} The sales thresholds indicate 70.88\% of months are below $\$57.5k and 18.41\% exceed $\$61.0k; mean 52 is typical demand and 10 reflects common monthly movement.

\section{Problem 4: Central band with both positive z-bounds}
From platform records in a global-skills learning programme, weekly app usage was estimated as approximately normal for classroom modelling; let $X$ be weekly minutes, with mean 95 and standard deviation 15, so $X\sim N(95,15^2)$.
\subsection*{C7}
\[P(X<x_1)=0.6368,\qquad P(X<x_2)=0.9192.\]
\[z_1=0.35,\qquad z_2=1.40.\]
\[Z=\frac{X-95}{15},\quad x=95+z(15).\]
\[x_1=100.25,\qquad x_2=116.0.\]
\ZNormalBand{0.35}{1.40}{P(0.35<Z<1.40)=0.2824}{}
\textbf{Interpretation:} So $P(100.25<X<116.0)=0.2824$, meaning 28.24\% of learners fall in that usage band; 95 minutes is average engagement and 15 minutes is the usual spread.

\section{Problem 5: Central band with negative and positive z-bounds}
In an export manufacturing line, package-weight checks from company data estimated an approximately normal distribution for classroom use; let $X$ be weight in grams, with mean 500 and standard deviation 20, so $X\sim N(500,20^2)$.
\subsection*{C7}
\[P(X<x_1)=0.1711,\qquad P(X<x_2)=0.8749.\]
\[z_1=-0.95,\qquad z_2=1.15.\]
\[Z=\frac{X-500}{20},\quad x=500+z(20).\]
\[x_1=481.0,\qquad x_2=523.0.\]
\ZNormalBand{-0.95}{1.15}{P(-0.95<Z<1.15)=0.7038}{}
\textbf{Interpretation:} This means 70.38\% of export items weigh from 481 g to 523 g; 500 g is the central target and 20 g is the routine production variation.
