\section{Streaming Time (Uniform)}

\textbf{Problem.}
A student's daily streaming time \(X\) (minutes) is modeled by
\[
X\sim \text{Uniform}(8,16).
\]

\textbf{Questions/Tasks.}
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{\alph*)}]
	\item Find \(E[X]\).
	\item Find \(\mathrm{Var}(X)\) and the standard deviation.
	\item Draw the graph of the PDF, indicate \(E[X]\), and draw an interval of one standard deviation around \(E[X]\).
	\item Is a value of 22 minutes more likely than 14 minutes? Explain.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{C4}
For \(X\sim\text{Uniform}(a,b)\):
\[
E[X]=\frac{a+b}{2},\qquad \mathrm{Var}(X)=\frac{(b-a)^2}{12}.
\]

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{\alph*)}]
	\item With \(a=8\) and \(b=16\),
	\[
	E[X]=\frac{8+16}{2}=12.
	\]
	
	\item
	\[
	\mathrm{Var}(X)=\frac{(16-8)^2}{12}=\frac{64}{12}=\frac{16}{3},
	\]
	\[
	\sigma=\sqrt{\frac{16}{3}}=\frac{4}{\sqrt{3}}\approx 2.31.
	\]
	
	\item The PDF is constant on the interval \([8,16]\), with height
	\[
	f(x)=\frac{1}{16-8}=\frac{1}{8}.
	\]
	One standard deviation around the mean is
	\[
	\left[E[X]-\sigma,\;E[X]+\sigma\right]
	=
	\left[12-\frac{4}{\sqrt{3}},\;12+\frac{4}{\sqrt{3}}\right]
	\approx [9.69,\;14.31].
	\]
	
	\begin{center}
		\begin{tikzpicture}
			\begin{axis}[
				width=10cm,
				height=5.5cm,
				axis lines=left,
				xlabel={$x$},
				ylabel={$f(x)$},
				xmin=6,
				xmax=18,
				ymin=0,
				ymax=0.18,
				grid=both,
				xtick={8,9.69,12,14.31,16},
				xticklabels={$8$,$12-\sigma$,$E[X]=12$,$12+\sigma$,$16$},
				ytick=\empty
				]
				\addplot[very thick, blue] coordinates {(8,0) (8,0.125) (16,0.125) (16,0)};
				\addplot[ultra thick, orange] coordinates {(12,0) (12,0.125)};
				\addplot[densely dotted] coordinates {(9.69,0) (9.69,0.125)};
				\addplot[densely dotted] coordinates {(14.31,0) (14.31,0.125)};
				\addplot[thick, red] coordinates {(9.69,0.015) (14.31,0.015)};
			\end{axis}
		\end{tikzpicture}
	\end{center}
	
	\item Since this is a continuous model, the probability of any exact single value is \(0\). So we compare small intervals of the same length around \(9\) and \(13\).
	
	For example, take intervals of length \(1\):
	\[
	[8.5,9.5]
	\qquad \text{and} \qquad
	[12.5,13.5].
	\]
	
	Both intervals lie completely inside the support \([8,16]\). Since the distribution is uniform, the density is constant:
	\[
	f(x)=\frac{1}{16-8}=\frac{1}{8}.
	\]
	
	Therefore,
	\[
	P(8.5\le X\le 9.5)=1\cdot \frac{1}{8}=\frac{1}{8},
	\]
	and
	\[
	P(12.5\le X\le 13.5)=1\cdot \frac{1}{8}=\frac{1}{8}.
	\]
	
	So the probabilities are equal. This shows that in a uniform distribution, any two intervals of the same length inside the support have the same probability.
	
\end{enumerate}

\section{Bus Wait Time (Triangular 1)}

\textbf{Problem.}
A bus wait time \(W\) (minutes) is modeled by a triangular distribution with minimum \(a=2\), mode \(c=2\), and maximum \(b=14\):
\[
W\sim \text{Triangular}(2,2,14).
\]

\textbf{Questions/Tasks.}
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{\alph*)}]
	\item Find \(E[W]\), \(\mathrm{Var}(W)\), and the standard deviation.
	\item Describe the shape (left-skewed, right-skewed, or symmetric) and justify from parameters.
	\item Explain why values near 2 are more likely than values near 14.
	\item Interpret the spread in the bus context.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{C4}
For \(\text{Triangular}(a,c,b)\):
\[
E[W]=\frac{a+b+c}{3},\qquad
\mathrm{Var}(W)=\frac{a^2+b^2+c^2-ab-ac-bc}{18}.
\]
So
\[
E[W]=\frac{2+14+2}{3}=\frac{18}{3}=6,
\]
\[
\mathrm{Var}(W)=\frac{2^2+14^2+2^2-(2)(14)-(2)(2)-(14)(2)}{18}
=\frac{4+196+4-28-4-28}{18}
=\frac{144}{18}=8.
\]
\[
\sigma=\sqrt{8}=2\sqrt{2}\approx 2.83.
\]
\TriangularDistGraph{2}{2}{14}{0}{16}{2,6,10,14}
Since the mode is at the left endpoint \(2\), the density is greatest there and then decreases linearly toward \(14\). Therefore the distribution is right-skewed, with a long tail to the right.

Values near \(2\) are more likely than values near \(14\) because the graph has its maximum height at \(2\) and slopes downward as \(W\) increases.

The standard deviation of about \(2.83\) minutes indicates that wait times are spread around the mean of \(6\) minutes, but shorter waits are much more common than longer waits.

\section{Bus Wait Time (Triangular 2)}

\textbf{Problem.}
A bus wait time \(W\) (minutes) is modeled by a triangular distribution with minimum \(a=2\), mode \(c=6\), and maximum \(b=14\):
\[
W\sim \text{Triangular}(2,6,14).
\]

\textbf{Questions/Tasks.}
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{\alph*)}]
  \item Find \(E[W]\), \(\mathrm{Var}(W)\), and the standard deviation.
  \item Describe the shape (left-skewed, right-skewed, or symmetric) and justify from parameters.
  \item Explain why values near 6 are more likely than values near 2 or 14.
  \item Interpret the spread in the bus context.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{C4}
For \(\text{Triangular}(a,c,b)\):
\[
E[W]=\frac{a+b+c}{3},\qquad
\mathrm{Var}(W)=\frac{a^2+b^2+c^2-ab-ac-bc}{18}.
\]
So
\[
E[W]=\frac{2+14+6}{3}=\frac{22}{3}\approx 7.33,
\]
\[
\mathrm{Var}(W)=\frac{2^2+14^2+6^2-(2)(14)-(2)(6)-(14)(6)}{18}
=\frac{4+196+36-28-12-84}{18}=\frac{112}{18}=\frac{56}{9}.
\]
\[
\sigma=\sqrt{\frac{56}{9}}=\frac{\sqrt{56}}{3}\approx 2.49.
\]
\TriangularDistGraph{2}{6}{14}{0}{16}{2,6,10,14}
Since mode 6 is closer to 2 than to 14, the distribution is right-skewed (longer right side).

\section{Comparing Uniform Distributions}

\textbf{Problem.}
Compare these uniform models for download times (seconds):
\[
X_1\sim \text{Uniform}(4,10),\qquad X_2\sim \text{Uniform}(6,12),\qquad X_3\sim \text{Uniform}(4,14).
\]

\textbf{Questions/Tasks.}
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{\alph*)}]
  \item Find \(E[X_i]\) and \(\mathrm{Var}(X_i)\) for \(i=1,2,3\).
  \item Compare \(X_1\) and \(X_2\): only location changes. How do center and spread change?
  \item Compare \(X_1\) and \(X_3\): only width changes from 6 to 10. How does variance change?
  \item Which model has the widest spread, and why?
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{C4}
Using \(E=\frac{a+b}{2}\), \(\mathrm{Var}=\frac{(b-a)^2}{12}\):
\[
E[X_1]=7,\quad \mathrm{Var}(X_1)=\frac{6^2}{12}=3;
\]
\[
E[X_2]=9,\quad \mathrm{Var}(X_2)=\frac{6^2}{12}=3;

\[
E[X_3]=9,\quad \mathrm{Var}(X_3)=\frac{10^2}{12}=\frac{25}{3}.
\]

\section{Uniform with Parameters}

\textbf{Problem.}
A random time \(X\) (minutes) is
\[
X\sim \text{Uniform}(p,p+12),\qquad p\in\mathbb{R}.
\]

\textbf{Questions/Tasks.}
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{\alph*)}]
  \item Express \(E[X]\) in terms of \(p\).
  \item Express \(\mathrm{Var}(X)\) in terms of \(p\).

  \item If \(p\) increases by 5, how do mean and variance change?
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{C4}
\[
E[X]=\frac{p+(p+12)}{2}=p+6,
\qquad
\mathrm{Var}(X)=\frac{((p+12)-p)^2}{12}=\frac{12^2}{12}=12.
\]
\begin{center}

So \(p\) controls location; spread is fixed by width 12 and does not depend on \(p\).

\section{Triangular with Parameters}

\textbf{Problem.}
A project completion time \(Y\) (days) is modeled by
\[
Y\sim \text{Triangular}(4,4+m,14),\qquad 0\le m\le 10.
\]

\textbf{Questions/Tasks.}
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{\alph*)}]
  \item Write \(E[Y]\) and \(\mathrm{Var}(Y)\) as functions of \(m\).
  \item State the condition on \(m\) that makes the model valid.
  \item How does the mean change as \(m\) increases?
  \item How does moving the mode affect the shape (left/right skewness)?
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{C4}
Here \(a=4\), \(c=4+m\), \(b=14\).
\[
E[Y]=\frac{a+b+c}{3}=\frac{4+14+(4+m)}{3}=\frac{22+m}{3}.
\]
\[
\mathrm{Var}(Y)=\frac{a^2+b^2+c^2-ab-ac-bc}{18}
=\frac{16+196+(4+m)^2-56-4(4+m)-14(4+m)}{18}
=\frac{m^2-10m+100}{18}.
\]
\begin{center}

\end{center}
Validity needs \(a\le c\le b\Rightarrow 4\le 4+m\le 14\Rightarrow 0\le m\le 10\).
Mean increases linearly with \(m\). As mode moves right, the distribution becomes less right-skewed and can become left-skewed when the mode passes midpoint 9.

\section{Uniform with Parameters}

\textbf{Problem.}
Commute time \(C\) (minutes) is modeled by
\[
C\sim \text{Uniform}(10-r,10+r),\qquad r>0.
\]

\textbf{Questions/Tasks.}
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{\alph*)}]
  \item Find \(E[C]\) and \(\mathrm{Var}(C)\) in terms of \(r\).
  \item What condition on \(r\) is needed for a valid interval?
  \item As \(r\) increases, what happens to interval width and spread?
  \item For what \(r\) is the distribution narrower, and why?
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{C4}
\[
E[C]=\frac{(10-r)+(10+r)}{2}=10,
\]
\[
\mathrm{Var}(C)=\frac{((10+r)-(10-r))^2}{12}=\frac{(2r)^2}{12}=\frac{r^2}{3}.
\]
\begin{center}
\begin{tikzpicture}

\section{Uniform with Parameters}

\textbf{Problem.}
A delivery time \(U\) (minutes) for a local route is modeled by
\[
U\sim \text{Uniform}(h-2w,\,h+w),\qquad w>0.
\]

\textbf{Questions/Tasks.}
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{\alph*)}]
  \item Derive \(E[U]\), \(\mathrm{Var}(U)\), and \(\sigma_U\) in terms of \(h\) and \(w\).
  \item Identify which parameter controls the center and which controls the interval width.
  \item Compare Case A: \((h,w)=(30,4)\) and Case B: \((h,w)=(35,4)\). Does the distribution shift, widen, or both?
  \item Compare Case C: \((h,w)=(30,4)\) and Case D: \((h,w)=(30,7)\). How do center, width, and spread change?
  \item If both \(h\) and \(w\) increase by 2 at the same time, explain whether the model shifts, widens, or both.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{C4}
For \(U\sim\text{Uniform}(a,b)\), \(E[U]=\frac{a+b}{2}\), \(\mathrm{Var}(U)=\frac{(b-a)^2}{12}\).
Here \(a=h-2w\), \(b=h+w\), so
\[
E[U]=\frac{(h-2w)+(h+w)}{2}=h-\frac{w}{2},
\]
\[
\mathrm{Var}(U)=\frac{\big((h+w)-(h-2w)\big)^2}{12}=\frac{(3w)^2}{12}=\frac{3w^2}{4},
\]
\[
\sigma_U=\sqrt{\frac{3w^2}{4}}=\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\,w \quad (w>0).
\]
The center \(h-\frac{w}{2}\) depends on both \(h\) and \(w\), while the width \(b-a=3w\) depends only on \(w\).
Case A \(\to\) B keeps \(w\) fixed, so width and spread stay the same, and the model shifts right by 5.
Case C \(\to\) D keeps \(h\) fixed and increases \(w\), so width grows from \(12\) to \(21\), spread increases, and the center moves from \(28\) to \(26.5\) (left).
If \(h\) and \(w\) both increase by 2, then width increases by \(3(2)=6\) (widens) and
\[
E[U]'-(E[U])=\left(h+2-\frac{w+2}{2}\right)-\left(h-\frac{w}{2}\right)=1,
\]
so it also shifts right by 1.

\section{Triangular with Parameters}

\textbf{Problem.}
A machine-cycle time \(T\) (seconds) is modeled by
\[
T\sim \text{Triangular}(k,\,k+r,\,k+3r),\qquad r>0.
\]

\textbf{Questions/Tasks.}
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{\alph*)}]
  \item Derive \(E[T]\), \(\mathrm{Var}(T)\), and \(\sigma_T\) in terms of \(k\) and \(r\).
  \item State the validity conditions on \(k\) and \(r\), and explain why they are sufficient.
  \item How do \(k\) and \(r\) separately affect center and spread?
  \item Compare shape with the symmetric triangular model having the same endpoints \(k\) and \(k+3r\). Is this model symmetric, left-skewed, or right-skewed?
  \item For fixed \(k\), compare \(r=2\) and \(r=5\): discuss changes in mean, variance, and skewness direction.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{C4}
For \(\text{Triangular}(a,c,b)\):
\[
E[T]=\frac{a+b+c}{3},\qquad
\mathrm{Var}(T)=\frac{a^2+b^2+c^2-ab-ac-bc}{18}.
\]
With \(a=k\), \(c=k+r\), \(b=k+3r\):
\[
E[T]=\frac{k+(k+r)+(k+3r)}{3}=k+\frac{4r}{3}.
\]
For variance,
\[
\mathrm{Var}(T)=\frac{k^2+(k+3r)^2+(k+r)^2-k(k+3r)-k(k+r)-(k+3r)(k+r)}{18}
=\frac{7r^2}{18}.
\]
Thus
\[
\sigma_T=\sqrt{\frac{7r^2}{18}}=r\sqrt{\frac{7}{18}}.
\]
Validity requires \(a\le c\le b\), i.e.
\[
k\le k+r\le k+3r,
\]
which holds whenever \(r\ge 0\); with \(r>0\), spread is nonzero, so the model is valid and non-degenerate for any real \(k\).
Parameter \(k\) shifts location only. Parameter \(r\) increases both mean offset (\(\frac{4r}{3}\)) and spread (\(\propto r^2\)).
For endpoints \(k\) and \(k+3r\), symmetry would need mode at midpoint \(k+\frac{3r}{2}\), but here mode is \(k+r\), left of midpoint; therefore the model is right-skewed.
Comparing \(r=2\) and \(r=5\) (fixed \(k\)): mean increases by \(\frac{4}{3}(5-2)=4\), variance scales from \(\frac{28}{18}\) to \(\frac{175}{18}\), and skewness direction stays right-skewed.

\section{Normal with Parameters}

\textbf{Problem.}
The daily amount \(N\) (GB) of network data used by a class is modeled by
\[
N\sim N\!\left(50+3p,\,(2+q)^2\right),\qquad p\in\mathbb{R},\ q>-2.
\]

\textbf{Questions/Tasks.}
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{\alph*)}]
  \item Derive \(E[N]\), \(\mathrm{Var}(N)\), and \(\sigma_N\) in terms of \(p\) and \(q\).
  \item Explain the role of \(p\) and \(q\): which controls location and which controls spread?
  \item Compare Case A: \((p,q)=(0,1)\) and Case B: \((p,q)=(-2,1)\). What changes in center, concentration around the mean, and tail likelihood?
  \item Compare Case C: \((p,q)=(0,1)\) and Case D: \((p,q)=(0,4)\). What changes in center, spread, concentration near the mean, and likelihood of values far from the mean?
  \item Between models \(M_1:(p,q)=(1,0)\) and \(M_2:(p,q)=(-1,3)\), which model is more likely to produce values at least 15 GB above its own mean? Justify conceptually.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{C4}
For \(N(\mu,\sigma^2)\), \(E[N]=\mu\), \(\mathrm{Var}(N)=\sigma^2\), \(\sigma>0\).
So
\[
E[N]=50+3p,\qquad
\mathrm{Var}(N)=(2+q)^2,\qquad
\sigma_N=2+q \quad (q>-2).
\]
Parameter \(p\) changes only location (horizontal shift). Parameter \(q\) changes spread: larger \(q\) gives larger \(\sigma\), wider curve, lower peak, and heavier tails.
Case A \(\to\) B keeps \(q=1\), so \(\sigma=3\) is unchanged: concentration and tail behavior relative to each mean stay the same, but mean shifts from \(50\) to \(44\) (left by 6).
Case C \(\to\) D keeps \(p=0\), so mean stays 50, but \(\sigma\) increases from \(3\) to \(6\): values are less concentrated near 50 and far-from-mean values become more likely.
For \(M_1\), \(\sigma=2\); for \(M_2\), \(\sigma=5\). A value 15 GB above its own mean is \(15/\sigma\) standard deviations above mean: \(7.5\sigma\) for \(M_1\) versus \(3\sigma\) for \(M_2\). The \(3\sigma\) event is much less extreme, so \(M_2\) is more likely.
