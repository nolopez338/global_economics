    \section*{General Problem}
    
    \textbf{Problem.}
    This first task is a \emph{template} for all the density-function problems in this activity.
    Let $X$ be a continuous random variable with bounded support $[a,b]$, where $a<m<b$.
    Suppose a proposed piecewise linear model is
    \[
    f(x)=\begin{cases}
    \dfrac{k}{m-a}(x-a), & a\le x\le m,\\[4pt]
    \dfrac{k}{b-m}(b-x), & m<x\le b,\\
    0, & \text{otherwise,}
    \end{cases}
    \]
    where $k>0$ is unknown.
    
    \textbf{Question.}
    \begin{itemize}
      \item \textbf{C1 Calculations:} Determine the value of $k$ that makes $f(x)$ a valid PDF on $[a,b]$.
      \item \textbf{C1 Interpretation:} Explain what this normalization means for the random variable $X$ and why the model is valid.
      \item \textbf{Extra analysis:} For any interval $[c,d]$ with $a\le c<m<d\le b$, express $P(c\le X\le d)$ using geometric areas and indicate this probability region on a graph.
    \end{itemize}
    
    \textbf{Solution.}
    \textbf{C1 Calculations.}
    A valid density must satisfy two conditions:
    \begin{enumerate}
      \item $f(x)\ge 0$ on its support,
      \item total area under the graph on $[a,b]$ is $1$.
    \end{enumerate}
    Here the graph is a single triangle with base $b-a$ and peak height $k$ at $x=m$, so
    \[
    A_{\text{total}}=\frac{1}{2}(b-a)k.
    \]
    Set this total area equal to $1$:
    \[
    \frac{1}{2}(b-a)k=1
    \quad\Rightarrow\quad
    k=\frac{2}{b-a}.
    \]
    So the normalized piecewise linear PDF is
    \[
    f(x)=\begin{cases}
    \dfrac{2(x-a)}{(b-a)(m-a)}, & a\le x\le m,\\[4pt]
    \dfrac{2(b-x)}{(b-a)(b-m)}, & m<x\le b,\\
    0, & \text{otherwise.}
    \end{cases}
    \]
    
    \begin{center}
    \begin{tikzpicture}
    \begin{axis}[
      width=11cm,
      height=6cm,
      axis lines=left,
      xlabel={$x$},
      ylabel={$f(x)$},
      xmin=-0.5,
      xmax=6.5,
      ymin=-0.02,
      ymax=0.55,
      grid=both,
      xtick={0,3,6},
      xticklabels={$a$,$m$,$b$}
    ]
    \addplot[draw=none, fill=blue!20, fill opacity=0.35] coordinates {(0,0) (3,0.45) (6,0) (6,0)} \closedcycle;
    \addplot[thick] coordinates {(0,0) (3,0.45) (6,0)};
    \addplot[only marks, mark=*, mark size=2pt] coordinates {(3,0.45)};
    \node[font=\scriptsize, anchor=south] at (axis cs:3,0.45) {$\left(m,k\right)$};
    \end{axis}
    \end{tikzpicture}
    \end{center}
    
    \textbf{C1 Interpretation.}
    The value of $k$ sets the peak height so that the full triangular area is exactly $1$. That means all probabilities over the support $[a,b]$ add to a complete whole, so this piecewise linear graph is a valid PDF for $X$. The left and right line segments describe how density increases up to $m$ and then decreases after $m$. In context, an interval of width $1$ centered near $m$ (for example, $[m-0.5,m+0.5]$ when it stays inside $[a,b]$) would typically carry more probability than an interval of width $1$ near an endpoint such as $[a,a+1]$. Likewise, matching intervals equally far from $m$ on opposite sides have the same probability by symmetry. The Extra analysis interval $[c,d]$ is one concrete example of this broader pattern because it combines area from both sides of the peak.
    
    \textbf{Extra analysis.}
    For $a\le c<m<d\le b$, split $[c,d]$ at $m$.
    Let
    \[
    f(c)=\frac{k}{m-a}(c-a),
    \qquad
    f(d)=\frac{k}{b-m}(b-d).
    \]
    Then the event area is the sum of two trapezoids:
    \[
    P(c\le X\le d)
    =\frac{f(c)+k}{2}(m-c)+\frac{k+f(d)}{2}(d-m).
    \]
    Substituting $k=\frac{2}{b-a}$ gives a fully normalized expression.

		\section{Water Tank Fill Level}
		
		\textbf{Problem.}
		A sensor records the fill level $X$ (in meters) of a water tank during a short period.
		The operations team wants to know how often the tank is in a \emph{usable storage band} (not too close to empty and not too close to full), because that helps them plan pumping and daily water release.
		Assume $X$ is continuous on $[0,5]$ with density
		\[
		f(x)=k,\quad 0\le x\le 5.
		\]
		
		\textbf{Question.}
		\begin{itemize}
			\item \textbf{C1 Calculations:} Determine $k$ so the model is a valid density.
			\item \textbf{C1 Interpretation:} Explain what the C1 calculations show about why the density model is valid.
			\item \textbf{Extra analysis:} Compute $P(1\le X\le 4)$ and represent this event on a probability-region graph.
		\end{itemize}
		
		\textbf{Solution.}
		\textbf{C1 Calculations.}
		The graph is a rectangle with width $5$ and height $k$, so total area is
		\[
		5k=1\Rightarrow k=\frac{1}{5}.
		\]
		\[
		f(x)=\frac{1}{5},\quad 0\le x\le 5.
		\]
		\begin{center}
		\begin{tikzpicture}
		\begin{axis}[
		  width=11cm,
		  height=6cm,
		  axis lines=left,
		  xlabel={$x$},
		  ylabel={$f(x)$},
		  xmin=-1,
		  xmax=6,
		  ymin=0.15,
		  ymax=0.25,
		  grid=both
		]
		\addplot[domain=0:5, samples=200, draw=none, fill=blue!20, fill opacity=0.35] {1/5} \closedcycle;
		\addplot[smooth, domain=0:5, samples=200] {1/5};
		\end{axis}
		\end{tikzpicture}
		\end{center}
		
		\textbf{C1 Interpretation.}
		The value $k=\frac{1}{5}$ makes the total area over all possible tank fill levels from $0$ to $5$ meters equal to $1$, so this is a valid probability model for $X$. Because the density is constant on the interval, the model gives the same density to each fill level in that range. So equal-length fill-level intervals have equal probability: for example, $[0,1]$, $[2,3]$, and $[4,5]$ each represent the same share of probability. A 2-meter band such as $[1,3]$ also has the same probability as $[3,5]$. The Extra analysis interval $[1,4]$ is one larger concrete example of this equal-length-interval rule.
		
		\textbf{Extra analysis.}
		Now $P(1\le X\le 4)$ is a rectangle of width $3$ and height $\frac{1}{5}$:
		\[
		P(1\le X\le 4)=3\cdot\frac{1}{5}=\frac{3}{5}=0.6.
		\]
		This means the model places $60\%$ of the total fill-level probability in the practical operating range from $1$ m to $4$ m, so the tank is more often in that usable band than in the edge zones near empty or near full.

		\section{Ramp Travel Time}
		
		\textbf{Problem.}
		The travel time $T$ (in seconds) for a robot to cross a ramp is modeled on $[2,8]$ by
		This matters for throughput planning: supervisors want to estimate how frequently trips fall in the \emph{slow-time region} where the robot may delay later tasks.
		\[
		f(t)=k(t-2),\quad 2\le t\le 8.
		\]
		
		\textbf{Question.}
		\begin{itemize}
			\item \textbf{C1 Calculations:} Determine $k$ so $f(t)$ is a valid density.
			\item \textbf{C1 Interpretation:} Explain what the C1 calculations show about why the density model is valid.
			\item \textbf{Extra analysis:} Compute $P(T>6)$ and represent this event on a probability-region graph.
		\end{itemize}
		
		\textbf{Solution.}
		\textbf{C1 Calculations.}
		The graph from $2$ to $8$ is a triangle with base $6$ and height $f(8)=6k$.
		So total area is
		\[
		\frac{1}{2}(6)(6k)=18k=1\Rightarrow k=\frac{1}{18}.
		\]
		\[
		f(t)=\frac{1}{18}(t-2),\quad 2\le t\le 8.
		\]
		\begin{center}
		\begin{tikzpicture}
		\begin{axis}[
		  width=11cm,
		  height=6cm,
		  axis lines=left,
		  xlabel={$x$},
		  ylabel={$f(x)$},
		  xmin=1,
		  xmax=9,
		  ymin=-0.05,
		  ymax=0.383,
		  grid=both
		]
		\addplot[domain=2:8, samples=200, draw=none, fill=blue!20, fill opacity=0.35] {(x-2)/18} \closedcycle;
		\addplot[smooth, domain=2:8, samples=200] {(x-2)/18};
		\addplot[only marks, mark=*, mark size=2pt] coordinates {(2,0) (8,1/3)};
		\node[font=\scriptsize, anchor=south east] at (axis cs:2,0) {$(2,0)$};
		\node[font=\scriptsize, anchor=south west] at (axis cs:8,1/3) {$\left(8,\tfrac{1}{3}\right)$};
		\end{axis}
		\end{tikzpicture}
		\end{center}
		
		\textbf{C1 Interpretation.}
		The value $k=\frac{1}{18}$ makes the full area across robot travel times from $2$ to $8$ seconds equal to $1$, and $f(t)\ge 0$ on that interval, so this is a valid density model for $T$. Since the line increases with $t$, larger times in this range are modeled with greater density than smaller times. For equal-width intervals, a right-shifted interval carries more probability than a left-shifted one; for instance, a 1-second band near $7$--$8$ seconds is more likely than a 1-second band near $2$--$3$ seconds. Similarly, the interval $[5,7]$ would be expected to have more probability than $[3,5]$ even though both have width $2$. The Extra analysis event $T>6$ is one concrete example of this right-heavy pattern.
		
		\textbf{Extra analysis.}
		For $T>6$, use the trapezoid on $[6,8]$.
		Its left height is $f(6)=\frac{1}{18}(4)=\frac{2}{9}$, right height is $f(8)=\frac{1}{3}$, width is $2$:
		\[
		P(T>6)=\frac{\left(\frac{2}{9}+\frac{1}{3}\right)}{2}\cdot 2
		=\frac{2}{9}+\frac{1}{3}=\frac{5}{9}.
		\]
		So, under this model, more than half of crossings fall above $6$ seconds; this suggests slow crossings are common enough to be relevant when setting cycle-time expectations.

		\section{Air Quality Index Block Model}
		
		\textbf{Problem.}
		An air-quality indicator $A$ is modeled on $[0,4]$ with piecewise-constant density
		Health staff track the interval $[0.5,2.5]$ as a \emph{routine monitoring zone} where normal school activities are usually kept, so its probability helps judge how often typical conditions occur.
		\[
		f(a)=\begin{cases}
		k, & 0\le a<1,\\
		2k, & 1\le a<3,\\
		k, & 3\le a\le 4.
		\end{cases}
		\]
		
		\textbf{Question.}
		\begin{itemize}
			\item \textbf{C1 Calculations:} Find $k$.
			\item \textbf{C1 Interpretation:} Explain what the C1 calculations show about why the density model is valid.
			\item \textbf{Extra analysis:} Compute $P(0.5\le A\le 2.5)$ and represent this event on a probability-region graph.
		\end{itemize}
		
		\textbf{Solution.}
		\textbf{C1 Calculations.}
		Add rectangle areas for total area 1:
		\[
		(1\cdot k)+(2\cdot 2k)+(1\cdot k)=6k=1\Rightarrow k=\frac{1}{6}.
		\]
		\[
		f(a)=\begin{cases}
		\frac{1}{6}, & 0\le a<1,\\
		\frac{1}{3}, & 1\le a<3,\\
		\frac{1}{6}, & 3\le a\le 4.
		\end{cases}
		\]

The taller middle block means the model assigns greater density to index values between $1$ and $3$ than to values in $[0,1)$ or $[3,4]$. In practice, a 1-unit interval inside the middle block (such as $[1,2]$) has twice the probability of a 1-unit interval in an end block (such as $[0,1]$ or $[3,4]$). Also, intervals that mix regions, like $[0.5,1.5]$, combine two different density levels. The Extra analysis interval $[0.5,2.5]$ is one concrete example of such a mixed-region interval.

		\section{Symmetric Delivery Window}
		
		\textbf{Problem.}
		A delivery offset $Y$ (hours from schedule) is modeled on $[-1,3]$ with a triangular density
		that rises to a peak at $y=1$:
		The dispatcher is interested in the central window $[0,2]$ because deliveries in that band are still manageable for staffing and customer notifications.
		\[
		f(y)=\begin{cases}
		k(y+1), & -1\le y\le 1,\\
		k(3-y), & 1< y\le 3.
		\end{cases}
		\]
		
		\textbf{Question.}
		\begin{itemize}
			\item \textbf{C1 Calculations:} Determine $k$.
			\item \textbf{C1 Interpretation:} Explain what the C1 calculations show about why the density model is valid.
			\item \textbf{Extra analysis:} Compute $P(0\le Y\le 2)$ and represent this event on a probability-region graph.
		\end{itemize}
		
		\textbf{Solution.}
		\textbf{C1 Calculations.}
		The whole graph is one triangle with base $4$ (from $-1$ to $3$) and height $f(1)=2k$.
		Thus
		\[
		\frac{1}{2}(4)(2k)=4k=1\Rightarrow k=\frac{1}{4}.
		\]
		\[
		f(y)=\begin{cases}
		\frac{1}{4}(y+1), & -1\le y\le 1,\\
		\frac{1}{4}(3-y), & 1< y\le 3.
		\end{cases}
		\]

		The constant $k=\frac{1}{4}$ makes the total area across all delivery offsets from $-1$ to $3$ hours equal to $1$, so this is a valid density model for $Y$. Its triangular shape means offsets near $y=1$ have greater density, while offsets near the endpoints have lower density, and the model is symmetric around $y=1$. So two offsets equally far from $1$ (for example, $0.5$ and $1.5$) have the same density, and matching intervals like $[0,1]$ and $[1,2]$ carry equal probability. Intervals near the peak, such as $[0.5,1.5]$, are more likely than equal-width intervals near the ends, such as $[-1,0]$. The Extra analysis window $[0,2]$ is one concrete example centered around the peak.
		
		\textbf{Extra analysis.}
		For $0\le Y\le 2$, use two equal trapezoids:
		on $[0,1]$ heights are $f(0)=\frac{1}{4}$ and $f(1)=\frac{1}{2}$,
		so area is $\frac{\left(\frac{1}{4}+\frac{1}{2}\right)}{2}\cdot 1=\frac{3}{8}$.
		By symmetry, area on $[1,2]$ is also $\frac{3}{8}$.
		Hence
		\[
		P(0\le Y\le 2)=\frac{3}{8}+\frac{3}{8}=\frac{3}{4}.
		\]
		So the model indicates a $75\%$ chance of being in the manageable offset window, meaning most deliveries are expected to remain within the operationally preferred timing band.

		\section{Three-Level Service Time}
		
		\textbf{Problem.}
		A service time variable $S$ (minutes) is modeled on $[0,10]$ by
		A team leader wants the probability of $S<7$ because calls finished before $7$ minutes are counted as \emph{on-pace completions} for staffing and queue management.
		\[
		f(s)=\begin{cases}
		k, & 0\le s<4,\\
		\frac{k}{2}, & 4\le s<8,\\
		\frac{3k}{2}, & 8\le s\le 10.
		\end{cases}
		\]
		
		\textbf{Question.}
		\begin{itemize}
			\item \textbf{C1 Calculations:} Determine $k$.
			\item \textbf{C1 Interpretation:} Explain what the C1 calculations show about why the density model is valid.
			\item \textbf{Extra analysis:} Compute $P(S<7)$ and represent this event on a probability-region graph.
		\end{itemize}
		
		\textbf{Solution.}
		\textbf{C1 Calculations.}
		Use rectangle areas for total area 1:
		\[
		(4)(k)+(4)\left(\frac{k}{2}\right)+(2)\left(\frac{3k}{2}\right)
		=4k+2k+3k=9k=1,
		\]
		so
		\[
		k=\frac{1}{9}.
		\]
		\[
		f(s)=\begin{cases}
		\frac{1}{9}, & 0\le s<4,\\
		\frac{1}{18}, & 4\le s<8,\\
		\frac{1}{6}, & 8\le s\le 10.
		\end{cases}
		\]

		\textbf{C1 Interpretation.}
		The constant $k=\frac{1}{9}$ makes the three rectangular blocks across service times from $0$ to $10$ minutes total exactly $1$, so this nonnegative piecewise-constant rule is a valid PDF for $S$. The different block heights show that the model gives different density levels to short, midrange, and late service times. For equal-length intervals, a segment in $[8,10]$ has the most probability, a segment in $[0,4]$ is next, and a segment in $[4,8]$ has the least because their densities are $\frac{1}{6}$, $\frac{1}{9}$, and $\frac{1}{18}$ respectively. Intervals that cross a boundary, like $[3,5]$, combine two density levels. The Extra analysis event $S<7$ is one concrete example that combines the first block and part of the second.
		
		\textbf{Extra analysis.}
		For $S<7$, include $[0,4]$ and $[4,7]$:
		\[
		P(S<7)=4\left(\frac{1}{9}\right)+3\left(\frac{1}{18}\right)
		=\frac{4}{9}+\frac{1}{6}=\frac{11}{18}.
		\]
		In context, $\frac{11}{18}$ of modeled service times are on-pace completions, so the majority of requests are expected to finish before the 7-minute planning threshold.
