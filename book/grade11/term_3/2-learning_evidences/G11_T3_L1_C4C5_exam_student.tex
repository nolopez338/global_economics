\SubsectionBox{Criteria assessment}\vspace{-0.25cm}
Each criterion is assessed with two scenarios.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{C4} is assessed with 2 proportion scenarios.
    \item \textbf{C5} is assessed with 2 mean scenarios.
\end{itemize}

For each criterion, one scenario is designed so that the data should lead to rejecting \(H_0\), and the other is designed so that the data should lead to not rejecting \(H_0\).

Use a significance level of \(\alpha=0.05\) in all four scenarios unless your teacher indicates otherwise.

You may use class notes and approved formula sheets. Show every relevant step, including hypotheses, test direction, calculations, decision rule, and contextual conclusion.

\vspace{0.25cm}
\SubsectionBox{Student Problems}\vspace{-0.25cm}

In \textbf{each} problem, complete all requested parts:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Propose a realistic specific scenario consistent with the context.
\item Identify the parameter and symbol (\(p\) or \(\mu\)).
\item State \(H_0\) and \(H_a\) with correct notation.
\item Identify the tail type (left, right, or two-tailed).
\item Use the provided sample data to compute the test statistic and p-value (or critical-value decision).
\item State the statistical decision (reject \(H_0\) / do not reject \(H_0\)).
\item Write a conclusion in context.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Criterion C4 --- Proportions}
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Problem 1 --- School context (designed to reject \(H_0\))}\\
At your school, administrators claim that \textbf{at least 70\%} of students arrive on time on Mondays. In a random sample of \(n=200\) students, \(x=122\) arrived on time.

Create a realistic school scenario for this claim and test whether the evidence is strong enough to contradict the claim.

\item \textbf{Problem 2 --- Home context (designed to not reject \(H_0\))}\\
In your home area, a community report claims that \textbf{40\%} of households separate recyclable waste correctly. In a random sample of \(n=150\) households, \(x=66\) do this correctly.

Create a realistic home/neighborhood scenario and test whether the sample gives enough evidence that the true proportion is different from 40\%.
\end{enumerate}

\columnbreak

\textbf{Criterion C5 --- Means}
\begin{enumerate}
\setcounter{enumi}{2}
\item \textbf{Problem 3 --- Grade 11 group context (designed to reject \(H_0\))}\\
A teacher states that the \textbf{mean number of minutes} Grade 11 students spend nightly on economics homework is \(\mu=50\) minutes. For a random sample of \(n=36\) students, the sample mean is \(\bar{x}=56\) minutes. Assume the population standard deviation is known: \(\sigma=12\) minutes.

Create a realistic Grade 11 scenario and test whether students spend \textbf{more} than 50 minutes on average.

\item \textbf{Problem 4 --- Bogotá context (designed to not reject \(H_0\))}\\
A city program claims that the \textbf{mean daily travel time} for students in Bogotá is \(\mu=42\) minutes. For a random sample of \(n=49\) students, the sample mean is \(\bar{x}=43\) minutes. Assume \(\sigma=7\) minutes.

Create a realistic Bogotá mobility scenario and test whether the true mean travel time is different from 42 minutes.
\end{enumerate}

\vspace{0.2cm}
\textbf{Reminder:} Even when a problem is marked as ``designed to reject'' or ``designed to not reject,'' your score depends on your statistical justification, not only on the final sentence.
