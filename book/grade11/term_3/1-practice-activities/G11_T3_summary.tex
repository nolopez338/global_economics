\section{Summary of \texttt{G11\_T3\_L1\_C1C2C3\_practice\_activity1.tex}}
This activity develops foundational work for Criteria C1--C3 by helping students identify whether a context uses a population mean or proportion, assign correct symbols, and write hypotheses in all three possible directions (lower, higher, different). Its purpose is introductory: students first build a stable template for parameter notation and then connect each alternative-hypothesis direction to left-, right-, or two-tailed testing. The reasoning focus is classification and structure recognition before full procedural testing.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Daily app check-ins for students and recent graduates:} Introductory proportion problem where students map context values to \(p, p_0, n, x, \alpha\), then write all three directional hypothesis pairs and classify the corresponding tail types.
    \item \textbf{Evening study time during a demanding term:} Parallel mean-based setup (with known \(\sigma\)) that reinforces the same three-direction framework, now transferring symbolic reasoning from proportions to means.
    \item \textbf{Defect rate in portable chargers:} Proportion-focused quality-control context that strengthens directional hypothesis writing under a stricter significance level, emphasizing precision in claim wording.
    \item \textbf{Homework completion time in an evening certification program:} Mean-context consolidation problem in which students repeat the full C1--C3 cycle and verify that tail classification is determined by the alternative-hypothesis direction, not by topic context.
\end{itemize}

\section{Summary of \texttt{G11\_T3\_L1\_C1C2C3\_practice\_activity2.tex}}
This redesigned follow-up activity structures C1--C3 work as a clear progression from contextual direction selection to evidence-based conclusion making. Students first identify the correct one-direction formulation from context and complete full hypothesis-test decisions using reject \(H_0\) or fail to reject \(H_0\). They then examine how conclusions can change when significance levels change while hypotheses and tail direction remain fixed. The activity ends with paired comparisons on identical data to show explicitly how choosing a one-sided versus two-sided alternative can change p-values and decisions.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Problem 1 --- Math-support program and average algebra score:} Lower-tailed mean context where students identify \(\mu\), write \(H_0: \mu=\mu_0\) and \(H_a: \mu<\mu_0\), compute test evidence, and reach a reject-\(H_0\) decision.
    \item \textbf{Problem 2 --- Delivery app on-time rate after route training:} Higher-tailed proportion context where students map \(p\)-notation, test \(H_a: p>p_0\), and obtain a reject-\(H_0\) decision from the sample evidence.
    \item \textbf{Problem 3 --- Clinic waiting time consistency check:} Two-tailed mean context for a ``different'' claim, producing a fail-to-reject-\(H_0\) outcome so students see contrasting decisions across Stage 1.
    \item \textbf{Problem 4 --- Fuel efficiency review under different policy strictness:} Lower-tailed mean test where hypotheses and tail stay fixed, but conclusions are compared across multiple \(\alpha\) levels motivated by routine monitoring versus stricter accountability.
    \item \textbf{Problem 5 --- Scholarship completion-rate target:} Higher-tailed proportion test where the same evidence is interpreted under internal-planning and public-reporting significance standards, showing threshold-sensitive decisions.
    \item \textbf{Problem 6 --- Factory packaging-weight stability check:} Two-tailed mean quality-control test that keeps the ``different'' formulation fixed while contrasting conclusions across looser and stricter quality thresholds.
    \item \textbf{Problem 7 --- App response time (lower vs different):} Same context, same data, and same \(\alpha\) comparison of left-tailed and two-tailed formulations to highlight how alternative-hypothesis framing changes p-values and can affect rejection strength.
    \item \textbf{Problem 8 --- Daily study-time claim (higher vs different):} Same-data comparison of right-tailed and two-tailed formulations at one significance level where both tests fail to reject \(H_0\), reinforcing meaningful non-significance interpretation under different alternatives.
\end{itemize}

\section{Summary of \texttt{G11\_T3\_L1\_C4\_practice\_activity.tex}}
This activity is now the dedicated C4 ladder for one-sample proportion hypothesis testing. It develops a full proportion-testing workflow from foundational setup to direction and significance-level sensitivity, then to same-evidence comparative framing, and finally to synthesis cases that classify robust, threshold-sensitive, and near-boundary outcomes using reject-\(H_0\)/fail-to-reject-\(H_0\) language in context.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{General Problem (Stage 1 --- proportion-only full procedure):} Establishes parameter identification \((p)\), benchmark setup \((p_0)\), sample proportion \((\hat p)\), condition checks, one-sample proportion \(z\)-statistic, p-value by tail type, decision rule, and contextual conclusion template.
    \item \textbf{On-time assignment completion rate (Stage 1 --- introductory lower-tail proportion test):} First direct lower-tail proportion application with full step-by-step computation and conclusion.
    \item \textbf{Platform adoption rate after training (Stage 1 --- introductory higher-tail proportion test):} First direct higher-tail proportion application reinforcing the same full workflow.
    \item \textbf{Compliance with safety checklist (Stage 1 --- introductory two-tailed proportion test):} First direct two-tailed proportion application for ``different'' claims.
    \item \textbf{Completion rate sensitivity to significance level (Stage 2 --- lower-tail \(\alpha\)-sensitivity):} Holds sample evidence fixed and compares decisions across 5\% and 1\% thresholds.
    \item \textbf{Defect rate sensitivity to significance level (Stage 2 --- higher-tail \(\alpha\)-sensitivity):} Demonstrates threshold-sensitive decisions from the same higher-tail proportion evidence.
    \item \textbf{Participation rate sensitivity to significance level (Stage 2 --- two-tailed \(\alpha\)-sensitivity):} Two-tailed proportion case where decision changes with significance level.
    \item \textbf{Attendance benchmark: lower vs higher (Stage 3 --- directional comparison):} Same data compared under opposite one-tailed alternatives to show tail-dependent p-values and contrasting decisions.
    \item \textbf{On-time delivery benchmark: lower vs different (Stage 3 --- directional comparison):} Same proportion evidence compared between lower-tail and two-tailed framing.
    \item \textbf{Customer approval benchmark: higher vs different (Stage 3 --- directional comparison):} Same evidence comparison where higher-tail and two-tailed tests both reject but with different p-values.
    \item \textbf{Compliance benchmark: lower vs higher vs different (Stage 3 --- three-way directional comparison):} Same evidence tested under all three alternatives, yielding consistent fail-to-reject outcomes.
    \item \textbf{Synthesis case A: near-boundary triple comparison (Stage 4 --- boundary reasoning):} Advanced proportion case emphasizing careful interpretation when evidence is near decision boundaries.
    \item \textbf{Synthesis case B: one-direction-only rejection (Stage 4 --- threshold-sensitive synthesis):} Shows a pattern where only one directional test rejects while opposite-direction and two-tailed tests do not.
    \item \textbf{Synthesis case C: directional and two-tailed rejection with opposite-direction non-rejection (Stage 4 --- strongest synthesis):} Strong-evidence proportion case where one direction and the two-tailed test reject, but the opposite direction fails to reject.
\end{itemize}

\section{Summary of \texttt{G11\_T3\_L1\_C5\_practice\_activity.tex}}
This activity is now the dedicated C5 ladder for one-sample mean inference with known population standard deviation. It develops the mean \(z\)-test procedure from foundational setup to direction and significance-level sensitivity, then to same-evidence directional comparisons, and finally to synthesis structures that distinguish robust, near-boundary, and threshold-sensitive decisions using precise hypothesis-testing language.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{General Problem (Stage 1 --- mean-only full procedure):} Establishes parameter identification \((\mu)\), benchmark setup \((\mu_0)\), sample mean \((\bar x)\), known \(\sigma\), one-sample mean \(z\)-statistic, p-value logic, decision rule, and contextual conclusion template.
    \item \textbf{Average commute time after schedule redesign (Stage 1 --- introductory lower-tail mean test):} First direct lower-tail mean \(z\)-test with full worked inference sequence.
    \item \textbf{Average monthly spending on phone plans (Stage 1 --- introductory higher-tail mean test):} First direct higher-tail mean \(z\)-test reinforcing benchmark-above reasoning.
    \item \textbf{Average monthly commuting cost (Stage 1 --- introductory two-tailed mean test):} First direct two-tailed mean \(z\)-test for ``different'' claims.
    \item \textbf{Average processing time sensitivity (Stage 2 --- lower-tail \(\alpha\)-sensitivity):} Same mean evidence evaluated at 5\% and 1\% to show threshold-dependent decisions.
    \item \textbf{Average monthly savings sensitivity (Stage 2 --- higher-tail \(\alpha\)-sensitivity):} Same higher-tail mean evidence produces different conclusions under different \(\alpha\) levels.
    \item \textbf{Average mathematics score sensitivity (Stage 2 --- two-tailed \(\alpha\)-sensitivity):} Two-tailed mean case that highlights how fixed evidence can cross one threshold but not a stricter one.
    \item \textbf{Average monthly gym visits: lower vs higher (Stage 3 --- directional comparison):} Same data under opposite one-tailed alternatives to illustrate tail and decision reversal.
    \item \textbf{Average sleep hours: lower vs different (Stage 3 --- directional comparison):} Same evidence compared between lower-tail and two-tailed framing.
    \item \textbf{Average call duration: higher vs different (Stage 3 --- directional comparison):} Same evidence compared between higher-tail and two-tailed tests with both rejecting.
    \item \textbf{Average monthly subscriptions cost: lower vs higher vs different (Stage 3 --- three-way directional comparison):} Three-way same-evidence mean comparison with coherent fail-to-reject outcomes.
    \item \textbf{Synthesis case A: strict-threshold non-rejection pattern (Stage 4 --- robust non-rejection synthesis):} Advanced mean case where all alternatives fail to reject under strict significance control.
    \item \textbf{Synthesis case B: one-direction-only rejection (Stage 4 --- near-boundary synthesis):} Near-boundary mean evidence used to interpret directional framing and non-rejection coherence.
    \item \textbf{Synthesis case C: directional and two-tailed rejection with opposite-direction non-rejection (Stage 4 --- strongest synthesis):} Strong-signal mean case where one directional test and two-tailed test reject, while the opposite direction fails to reject.
\end{itemize}

\section{Summary of \texttt{G11\_T3\_L1\_C6\_practice\_activity.tex}}
This redesigned C6 activity is organized as a deliberate five-stage learning ladder that develops interpretation from foundational decision language to advanced policy synthesis. The progression keeps sample-size and margin-of-error awareness, p-value versus threshold reading, and decision-theoretic communication in context, while making Type I and Type II error consequences the central thread connecting every problem.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Problem 1 --- General framework for decision interpretation (Stage 1):} Establishes reject \(H_0\) versus fail to reject \(H_0\), clarifies that decisions are probabilistic rather than certain, defines Type I/Type II errors, and sets the rule that the immediate error concern depends on the reported decision.
    \item \textbf{Problem 2 --- Fuel-price monitoring report (Stage 2):} Introductory single-test case with a reported reject-\(H_0\) outcome, requiring contextual interpretation of the supported claim and explicit Type I error meaning for fare planning.
    \item \textbf{Problem 3 --- Public employment office review (Stage 2):} Introductory single-test case with fail-to-reject-\(H_0\), focusing on insufficient evidence language and contextual Type II risk in labor-policy evaluation.
    \item \textbf{Problem 4 --- Export quality audit under strict and lenient standards (Stage 3):} Same p-value evaluated at \(\alpha=0.05\) and \(\alpha=0.01\), showing rejection at both thresholds and interpreting robust conclusions with persistent Type I caution.
    \item \textbf{Problem 5 --- Digital payment adoption with threshold-sensitive evidence (Stage 3):} Same p-value interpreted at strict and planning thresholds, producing reject at 5\% but fail to reject at 1\%, and highlighting the shift between Type I protection and Type II exposure.
    \item \textbf{Problem 6 --- Transport reliability monitoring with stable non-rejection (Stage 3):} Same p-value interpreted at two thresholds with fail-to-reject outcomes at both, emphasizing stable non-rejection and continued Type II concern.
    \item \textbf{Problem 7 --- Public health screening decision design (Stage 3 to Stage 4 bridge):} Contextual comparison of Type I versus Type II consequences to justify why stricter or looser \(\alpha\) may be appropriate in screening policy.
    \item \textbf{Problem 8 --- Labor policy evaluation and retraining expansion (Stage 4):} Context-driven error-cost analysis requiring interpretation of what Type I and Type II errors imply for employment-program budgeting and missed improvements.
    \item \textbf{Problem 9 --- Educational intervention review with precision notes (Stage 4):} Interprets decisions while linking sample size and margin of error to evidence precision, then compares practical severity of false positive versus false negative conclusions.
    \item \textbf{Problem 10 --- Service regulation dashboard with two independent tests (Stage 4):} Multi-test reading task requiring separation of hypotheses, decisions, and immediate error concerns for each metric in one policy report.
    \item \textbf{Problem 11 --- Economic monitoring dashboard with three independent tests (Stage 4):} Extends dashboard reasoning across robust and threshold-sensitive findings, asking students to assign distinct error-priority cautions by indicator.
    \item \textbf{Problem 12 --- Consumer protection and quality assurance synthesis (Stage 5):} Advanced cross-threshold task classifying results as robust, moderate, or tentative and linking each category to Type I/Type II consequence communication.
    \item \textbf{Problem 13 --- Integrated policy planning under alternative error tolerances (Stage 5):} Strongest synthesis problem comparing policy choices under stricter versus more lenient false-positive tolerance, with explicit justification of which error is more costly in each domain.
\end{itemize}

\section{Summary of \texttt{G11\_T3\_L1\_C7\_practice\_activity.tex}}
This file focuses on confidence intervals for population proportions through repeated p-value-versus-\(\alpha\) comparisons and decision justification in context. The activity builds fluency in deciding reject/fail-to-reject outcomes, interpreting practical implications, and stating limitations of statistical evidence. The reasoning progression moves from single-test comparisons to multi-test and multi-threshold synthesis.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Quality control: defective export units:} Introductory right-tailed proportion case emphasizing direct p-value comparison and practical action limits.
    \item \textbf{Logistics: on-time delivery target:} Left-tailed proportion decision task linking rejection to operational intervention.
    \item \textbf{Port operations: mean customs processing time:} Right-tailed mean example with strong rejection and policy interpretation.
    \item \textbf{Macroeconomics: mean inflation monitoring:} Non-rejection mean case highlighting why moderate p-values cannot justify directional claims.
    \item \textbf{Banking: loan approval fairness check:} Near-cutoff left-tailed proportion case reinforcing strict comparison rules when p-values are close to \(\alpha\).
    \item \textbf{Digital payments: fraud checks in two regions:} Comparative two-region design showing different decisions under a shared evidence threshold.
    \item \textbf{Telecom: churn result under two reference probabilities:} Same p-value interpreted at 5\% and 1\% to illustrate threshold-dependent decisions.
    \item \textbf{Public services: waiting-time tests across departments:} Multi-department dashboard requiring separate p-value/\(\alpha\) comparisons with mixed thresholds.
    \item \textbf{Labor market policy: stability and change across thresholds:} Two-claim robustness classification across 5\% and 1\% standards.
    \item \textbf{Economic monitoring dashboard: mixed evidence across indicators:} Advanced synthesis of several indicators across multiple significance levels to separate robust from tentative evidence.
\end{itemize}
