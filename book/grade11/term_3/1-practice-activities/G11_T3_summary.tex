\section{Summary of \texttt{G11\_T3\_L1\_C1C2C3\_practice\_activity1.tex}}
This activity develops foundational work for Criteria C1--C3 by helping students identify whether a context uses a population mean or proportion, assign correct symbols, and write hypotheses in all three possible directions (lower, higher, different). Its purpose is introductory: students first build a stable template for parameter notation and then connect each alternative-hypothesis direction to left-, right-, or two-tailed testing. The reasoning focus is classification and structure recognition before full procedural testing.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Daily app check-ins for students and recent graduates:} Introductory proportion problem where students map context values to \(p, p_0, n, x, \alpha\), then write all three directional hypothesis pairs and classify the corresponding tail types.
    \item \textbf{Evening study time during a demanding term:} Parallel mean-based setup (with known \(\sigma\)) that reinforces the same three-direction framework, now transferring symbolic reasoning from proportions to means.
    \item \textbf{Defect rate in portable chargers:} Proportion-focused quality-control context that strengthens directional hypothesis writing under a stricter significance level, emphasizing precision in claim wording.
    \item \textbf{Homework completion time in an evening certification program:} Mean-context consolidation problem in which students repeat the full C1--C3 cycle and verify that tail classification is determined by the alternative-hypothesis direction, not by topic context.
\end{itemize}

\section{Summary of \texttt{G11\_T3\_L1\_C1C2C3\_practice\_activity2.tex}}
This redesigned follow-up activity structures C1--C3 work as a clear progression from contextual direction selection to evidence-based conclusion making. Students first identify the correct one-direction formulation from context and complete full hypothesis-test decisions using reject \(H_0\) or fail to reject \(H_0\). They then examine how conclusions can change when significance levels change while hypotheses and tail direction remain fixed. The activity ends with paired comparisons on identical data to show explicitly how choosing a one-sided versus two-sided alternative can change p-values and decisions.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Problem 1 --- Math-support program and average algebra score:} Lower-tailed mean context where students identify \(\mu\), write \(H_0: \mu=\mu_0\) and \(H_a: \mu<\mu_0\), compute test evidence, and reach a reject-\(H_0\) decision.
    \item \textbf{Problem 2 --- Delivery app on-time rate after route training:} Higher-tailed proportion context where students map \(p\)-notation, test \(H_a: p>p_0\), and obtain a reject-\(H_0\) decision from the sample evidence.
    \item \textbf{Problem 3 --- Clinic waiting time consistency check:} Two-tailed mean context for a ``different'' claim, producing a fail-to-reject-\(H_0\) outcome so students see contrasting decisions across Stage 1.
    \item \textbf{Problem 4 --- Fuel efficiency review under different policy strictness:} Lower-tailed mean test where hypotheses and tail stay fixed, but conclusions are compared across multiple \(\alpha\) levels motivated by routine monitoring versus stricter accountability.
    \item \textbf{Problem 5 --- Scholarship completion-rate target:} Higher-tailed proportion test where the same evidence is interpreted under internal-planning and public-reporting significance standards, showing threshold-sensitive decisions.
    \item \textbf{Problem 6 --- Factory packaging-weight stability check:} Two-tailed mean quality-control test that keeps the ``different'' formulation fixed while contrasting conclusions across looser and stricter quality thresholds.
    \item \textbf{Problem 7 --- App response time (lower vs different):} Same context, same data, and same \(\alpha\) comparison of left-tailed and two-tailed formulations to highlight how alternative-hypothesis framing changes p-values and can affect rejection strength.
    \item \textbf{Problem 8 --- Daily study-time claim (higher vs different):} Same-data comparison of right-tailed and two-tailed formulations at one significance level where both tests fail to reject \(H_0\), reinforcing meaningful non-significance interpretation under different alternatives.
\end{itemize}

\section{Summary of \texttt{G11\_T3\_L1\_C4\_practice\_activity.tex}}
This file centers on Criterion C4 through the full contextual hypothesis-testing procedure: define hypotheses, verify conditions where required, compute test statistics and p-values, compare with significance levels, and interpret decisions in words. The activity progresses from a general template to mixed applied cases in proportions and means, including one-tailed/two-tailed contrasts and near-boundary decision cases.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{General Problem:} Procedural overview problem introducing the complete hypothesis-testing workflow and directional variants in a template format.
    \item \textbf{Food delivery completion rate:} Introductory proportion test (lower-tail) emphasizing condition checks and p-value-based rejection.
    \item \textbf{Average one-way commute time for office staff:} Mean \(z\)-test (right-tail) focused on a fail-to-reject decision and contextual interpretation.
    \item \textbf{Weekly side-income amount from freelance tasks:} Two-tailed mean test across two significance levels to compare consistency of conclusions.
    \item \textbf{Monthly public transport spending benchmark:} Comparative two-tailed mean case where the decision changes with stricter \(\alpha\), reinforcing threshold sensitivity.
    \item \textbf{Use of automatic savings transfers:} Split one-tailed proportion exercise (lower vs higher) showing how direction alone changes p-values and conclusions with the same sample data.
    \item \textbf{Average daily sleep before workdays:} Mean context with paired opposite one-tailed tests that produce contrasting decisions from one statistic.
    \item \textbf{Average monthly spending on mobile data plans:} Mean context mirroring directional contrast (higher vs lower) to reinforce tail-dependent interpretation.
    \item \textbf{Use of monthly budgeting reminders:} Three-direction proportion comparison (higher, lower, different) where all tests fail to reject, highlighting coherent non-rejection interpretation.
    \item \textbf{Use of monthly budgeting reminders with a near-boundary lower-tail result:} Boundary case emphasizing careful p-value comparison when evidence is close to \(\alpha\).
    \item \textbf{Use of monthly budgeting reminders with lower-tail and two-tailed rejection:} Advanced comparative case showing when lower-tail and two-tailed conclusions both reject while higher-tail does not.
    \item \textbf{Use of monthly budgeting reminders with higher-tail and two-tailed rejection:} Symmetric advanced case where higher-tail and two-tailed tests reject, reinforcing directional logic.
\end{itemize}

\section{Summary of \texttt{G11\_T3\_L1\_C5\_practice\_activity.tex}}
This file focuses on confidence-interval and inference-linked work for a population mean with known population standard deviation, operationalized here through one-sample \(z\)-test structures and directional decision interpretation. Pedagogically, students strengthen formal symbolic setup and statistical computation while comparing outcomes under different tail choices and significance levels. The reasoning emphasis is quantitative precision and comparative inference.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{General Problem:} General mean-inference template that formalizes hypotheses, \(z\)-statistic computation, p-value rules, and contextual conclusions.
    \item \textbf{Average monthly spending on phone plans:} Introductory right-tailed mean test with a fail-to-reject conclusion.
    \item \textbf{Average weekly grocery budget:} Left-tailed mean test with very strong evidence and dual-\(\alpha\) confirmation.
    \item \textbf{Average monthly commuting cost:} Two-tailed mean case showing rejection at 5\% but not at 1\%.
    \item \textbf{Average daily screen-free time after work:} Paired directional tests on two samples, reinforcing how direction and sample context change decisions.
    \item \textbf{Average monthly gym visits:} Same-sample directional contrast where lower-tail rejects but higher-tail does not.
    \item \textbf{Average focus hours in deep-work sessions:} Complementary directional contrast where higher-tail rejects but lower-tail does not.
    \item \textbf{Average monthly streaming subscriptions cost:} Three-direction mean comparison yielding consistent non-rejection across higher, lower, and different claims.
    \item \textbf{Average monthly commuting distance:} Strong-signal example where higher and two-tailed tests reject, but lower-tail does not.
    \item \textbf{Average number of sick days used per year:} Mirror strong-signal case where lower and two-tailed tests reject, while higher-tail does not.
\end{itemize}

\section{Summary of \texttt{G11\_T3\_L1\_C6\_practice\_activity.tex}}
This activity targets sample-size, margin-of-error, and interpretation-oriented reasoning through contextual decisions from reported test outcomes, especially when p-values are compared to different significance thresholds. Its pedagogical purpose is to train students to read statistical decisions carefully in policy and planning contexts, distinguish evidence from certainty, and articulate Type I/Type II implications. The reasoning is interpretive, comparative, and decision-theoretic.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{General Problem:} Framework problem on interpreting reject/fail-to-reject outcomes, threshold comparisons, and contextual error meanings without recalculating statistics.
    \item \textbf{Fuel-price monitoring report:} Contextual interpretation of a rejection in a right-tailed mean test, including explicit Type I error translation.
    \item \textbf{Public employment office review:} Fail-to-reject interpretation in a lower-tailed mean setting, with emphasis on what remains unproven and Type II risk.
    \item \textbf{Export quality audit with two evidence thresholds:} Single p-value interpreted at 5\% and 1\% to highlight robust rejection across stricter standards.
    \item \textbf{Digital payment adoption check under strict and lenient policy standards:} Threshold-sensitive case where conclusions differ at 1\% and 5\%.
    \item \textbf{Border operations dashboard: two independent service tests:} Multi-metric interpretation task separating conclusions for a two-tailed mean test and a one-tailed proportion test.
    \item \textbf{Education and labor program review: two different directional checks:} Dual-context interpretation with directional differences and concrete policy-error narrative.
    \item \textbf{Economic development briefing with mixed thresholds:} Comparative synthesis identifying one threshold-sensitive and one robust conclusion across independent tests.
    \item \textbf{Telecom service regulation: three independent compliance checks:} Multi-test dashboard interpretation balancing reject and fail-to-reject outcomes across domains.
    \item \textbf{Integrated transport and labor assessment with robustness checks:} Three-test robustness analysis at 10\% and 5\%, classifying stable vs sensitive findings.
    \item \textbf{National performance synthesis: three tests, multiple confidence standards:} Advanced cross-threshold reporting exercise that categorizes strong, moderate, and tentative evidence.
\end{itemize}

\section{Summary of \texttt{G11\_T3\_L1\_C7\_practice\_activity.tex}}
This file focuses on confidence intervals for population proportions through repeated p-value-versus-\(\alpha\) comparisons and decision justification in context. The activity builds fluency in deciding reject/fail-to-reject outcomes, interpreting practical implications, and stating limitations of statistical evidence. The reasoning progression moves from single-test comparisons to multi-test and multi-threshold synthesis.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Quality control: defective export units:} Introductory right-tailed proportion case emphasizing direct p-value comparison and practical action limits.
    \item \textbf{Logistics: on-time delivery target:} Left-tailed proportion decision task linking rejection to operational intervention.
    \item \textbf{Port operations: mean customs processing time:} Right-tailed mean example with strong rejection and policy interpretation.
    \item \textbf{Macroeconomics: mean inflation monitoring:} Non-rejection mean case highlighting why moderate p-values cannot justify directional claims.
    \item \textbf{Banking: loan approval fairness check:} Near-cutoff left-tailed proportion case reinforcing strict comparison rules when p-values are close to \(\alpha\).
    \item \textbf{Digital payments: fraud checks in two regions:} Comparative two-region design showing different decisions under a shared evidence threshold.
    \item \textbf{Telecom: churn result under two reference probabilities:} Same p-value interpreted at 5\% and 1\% to illustrate threshold-dependent decisions.
    \item \textbf{Public services: waiting-time tests across departments:} Multi-department dashboard requiring separate p-value/\(\alpha\) comparisons with mixed thresholds.
    \item \textbf{Labor market policy: stability and change across thresholds:} Two-claim robustness classification across 5\% and 1\% standards.
    \item \textbf{Economic monitoring dashboard: mixed evidence across indicators:} Advanced synthesis of several indicators across multiple significance levels to separate robust from tentative evidence.
\end{itemize}
