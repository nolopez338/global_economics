\ExamSection{Problems}
\begin{ExamProblems}

\item
\section{Fuel-price monitoring report}
A city transport authority uses monthly fuel-price data to update bus-fare planning. The 4.0\% benchmark comes from last year's official inflation forecast used in the municipal budget. This month, the analytics team drew a random sample of retail stations from across the city and ran a one-sided test of whether mean monthly fuel inflation is above that 4.0\% planning value. They used \(H_0\colon \mu=4.0\), \(H_1\colon \mu>4.0\), at \(\alpha=0.05\), and the reported decision was \textit{reject \(H_0\)}. In context, explain what decision makers are justified in saying, what they are \emph{not} justified in claiming, and why this is still not absolute proof.

\subsection*{C6}
\begin{itemize}
    \item The decision supports the claim that the population mean inflation is greater than 4.0\%.
    \item It does not prove with certainty that \(\mu>4.0\); there is still Type I error risk, meaning officials could incorrectly announce inflation is above 4.0\% when the true citywide mean is actually 4.0\%.
    \item It does not say every product category has inflation above 4.0\%; the result is about the population mean.
\end{itemize}

\section{Public employment office review}
A labor ministry is reviewing whether a new job-matching platform is shortening unemployment spells. The historical reference used in annual planning is 11 weeks, based on the ministry's previous three-year average. From recent case files, analysts sampled claimants who completed the program this quarter and tested whether the average duration is now lower than 11 weeks. They used \(H_0\colon \mu=11\), \(H_1\colon \mu<11\), with \(\alpha=0.10\), and obtained \textit{fail to reject \(H_0\)}. State the proper contextual conclusion, explain what cannot be concluded, and describe what a Type II error would look like in this exact policy setting.

\subsection*{C6}
\begin{itemize}
    \item The data do not provide sufficient evidence to support the claim that mean duration is below 11 weeks.
    \item This does not prove \(\mu=11\); it only means evidence was not strong enough at the 10\% level.
    \item Some individuals may still be unemployed for fewer than 11 weeks; the test concerns the population mean. A Type II error here would mean the platform really did reduce the true mean below 11 weeks, but the ministry failed to detect that improvement.
\end{itemize}

\section{Export quality audit with two evidence thresholds}
A manufacturer exports precision parts under contracts that reward a defect rate below 5\%. The 5\% reference is written in buyer agreements and used in quarterly compliance audits. From this month's export batches, quality staff sampled items and tested the claim that the true defect proportion is below 5\% using \(H_0\colon p=0.05\) versus \(H_1\colon p<0.05\). The p-value is 0.032. The same evidence is reviewed at two standards: \(\alpha=0.05\) and \(\alpha=0.01\). For each level, state the decision, compare whether the practical conclusion changes, and explain what remains uncertain in both cases.

\subsection*{C6}
\begin{itemize}
    \item At 5\%, reject \(H_0\): there is sufficient evidence to conclude the defect proportion is below 5\%.
    \item At 1\%, fail to reject \(H_0\): there is insufficient evidence at this stricter level to conclude the defect proportion is below 5\%.
    \item The conclusion is threshold-sensitive: the same sample supports the claim at 5\% but not at 1\%. Even where the claim is supported, it is not absolute proof of a defect rate below 5\%.
\end{itemize}

\item
\section{Digital payment adoption check under strict and lenient policy standards}
A central bank tracks household use of digital payments to decide whether to expand cashless-service infrastructure. The benchmark is 70\%, taken from last year's national inclusion target. A national sample of households is analyzed with \(H_0\colon p=0.70\), \(H_1\colon p>0.70\), producing a p-value of 0.041. Officials review this at two standards: a stricter research threshold \(\alpha=0.01\) and a more permissive planning threshold \(\alpha=0.05\). Explain which conclusion is supported at each level, what can be said for infrastructure decisions, and what cannot be overclaimed.

\subsection*{C6}
\begin{itemize}
    \item At 1\%, fail to reject \(H_0\): evidence is not strong enough to claim the population proportion exceeds 70\%.
    \item At 5\%, reject \(H_0\): evidence is sufficient to support that the population proportion exceeds 70\%.
    \item Even at 5\%, this does not guarantee every household uses digital payments weekly; the conclusion is probabilistic, not absolute certainty.
\end{itemize}

\section{Border operations dashboard: two independent service tests}
A logistics agency reviews two independent service metrics from the same quarter because staffing decisions depend on both speed and participation.

\textbf{Test A (customs wait time).} The service contract states an expected mean wait of 2.5 hours. Analysts test whether the true mean is different using \(H_0\colon \mu=2.5\), \(H_1\colon \mu\ne2.5\), at \(\alpha=0.05\), and report \textit{reject \(H_0\)}.

\textbf{Test B (school meal participation in border districts).} A parallel social-support program has a policy floor of 62\% participation. Program data are tested with \(H_0\colon p=0.62\), \(H_1\colon p<0.62\), at \(\alpha=0.05\), and the reported decision is \textit{fail to reject \(H_0\)}.

Interpret Test A and Test B separately. For each one, state what the agency may conclude in context and what it still may not claim.

\subsection*{C6}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Test A:} Evidence supports that the population mean customs wait time is not 2.5 hours.
    \item \textbf{Test A limitation:} The result does not identify direction unless sample information is checked separately, and it does not imply all shipments wait longer or shorter than 2.5 hours.
    \item \textbf{Test B:} At 5\%, evidence is insufficient to conclude participation is below 62\%; this does not prove participation is exactly 62\%.
\end{itemize}

\section{Education and labor program review: two different directional checks}
A regional policy board evaluates two unrelated outcomes before renewing program budgets.

\textbf{Test A (school meal participation).} The board's minimum acceptable rate is 62\% based on nutrition-policy commitments. A survey test uses \(H_0\colon p=0.62\), \(H_1\colon p<0.62\), \(\alpha=0.10\), and reports \textit{reject \(H_0\)}.

\textbf{Test B (wage growth stability).} For a labor support package, economists check whether annual wage growth differs from the 3.5\% planning assumption using \(H_0\colon \mu=3.5\), \(H_1\colon \mu\ne3.5\), \(\alpha=0.10\), with decision \textit{fail to reject \(H_0\)}.

Give two independent interpretations. Also explain one concrete error risk: describe what a Type I error would mean for Test A in this policy context.

\subsection*{C6}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Test A:} The data provide sufficient evidence that participation is below 62\%.
    \item \textbf{Test A error meaning:} A Type I error would mean the board wrongly concludes participation fell below 62\% and reallocates funds, even though the true participation rate is actually at the 62\% benchmark.
    \item \textbf{Test B:} There is not enough evidence to conclude wage growth differs from 3.5\%; this does not prove the mean is exactly 3.5\%.
\end{itemize}

\section{Economic development briefing with mixed thresholds}
A national development office combines training and credit evidence before drafting next year's strategy.

\textbf{Test A (training certification success).} The office wants to know whether pass rates exceed the 80\% target tied to donor funding. Using \(H_0\colon p=0.80\), \(H_1\colon p>0.80\), the p-value is 0.074. Interpret this test at both \(\alpha=0.10\) and \(\alpha=0.05\).

\textbf{Test B (average farm loan size).} A separate lending program tests whether mean approved loans are below the 18{,}000-dollar ceiling for smallholder support, using \(H_0\colon \mu=18000\), \(H_1\colon \mu<18000\), at \(\alpha=0.05\), with decision \textit{reject \(H_0\)}.

Compare outcomes across levels and across tests: identify which finding is sensitive to the confidence threshold and which remains clear.

\subsection*{C6}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Test A at 10\%:} Reject \(H_0\), so evidence supports a pass proportion above 80\%.
    \item \textbf{Test A at 5\%:} Fail to reject \(H_0\), so evidence is insufficient at this stricter level; this finding is threshold-sensitive.
    \item \textbf{Test B at 5\%:} There is sufficient evidence that the population mean approved loan is below 18{,}000 dollars; this result does not imply every loan is below that value.
\end{itemize}

\section{Telecom service regulation: three independent compliance checks}
A communications regulator conducts three independent checks before publishing a quarterly compliance bulletin. All tests use \(\alpha=0.05\).

\textbf{Test A (broadband access).} The benchmark from last year's census is 76\%. Using \(H_0\colon p=0.76\), \(H_1\colon p\ne0.76\), the outcome is \textit{fail to reject \(H_0\)}.

\textbf{Test B (customer response speed).} Operators are expected to keep average complaint response time at 24 hours. Testing \(H_0\colon \mu=24\), \(H_1\colon \mu>24\), the outcome is \textit{reject \(H_0\)}.

\textbf{Test C (network outage rate).} The technical standard is 3.0\% monthly outages. Testing \(H_0\colon p=0.03\), \(H_1\colon p<0.03\), the outcome is \textit{fail to reject \(H_0\)}.

Interpret each decision one by one and state clearly what the regulator can conclude and what remains uncertain for each metric.

\subsection*{C6}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Test A:} At the 5\% level, evidence is insufficient to conclude the broadband proportion differs from 76\%; this does not prove the true proportion equals 76\%.
    \item \textbf{Test B:} Evidence supports that average complaint response time is above 24 hours.
    \item \textbf{Test C:} Evidence is insufficient to conclude outages are below 3.0\%; a meaningful improvement may exist but remain undetected.
\end{itemize}

\section{Integrated transport and labor assessment with robustness checks}
A policy task force reviews three independent indicators and asks which conclusions are robust across evidence thresholds.

\textbf{Test A (fuel inflation).} For fare planning, they test whether mean monthly fuel inflation is above 4.0\%. The p-value is 0.004.

\textbf{Test B (unemployment duration).} For labor policy, they test whether average unemployment duration is below 11 weeks. The p-value is 0.027.

\textbf{Test C (broadband access).} For digital inclusion, they test whether broadband access differs from 76\%. The p-value is 0.081.

All three tests are interpreted at \(\alpha=0.10\) and \(\alpha=0.05\). Identify which conclusions stay the same at both levels and which depend on the selected threshold. Then explain what that means for how confidently the task force should communicate each claim.

\subsection*{C6}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Test A:} Reject \(H_0\) at both 10\% and 5\%; the conclusion that mean fuel inflation is above 4.0\% is robust.
    \item \textbf{Test B:} Reject \(H_0\) at both 10\% and 5\%; the conclusion that mean unemployment duration is below 11 weeks is also robust.
    \item \textbf{Test C:} Reject \(H_0\) at 10\% but fail to reject at 5\%; the conclusion that broadband access differs from 76\% is threshold-sensitive and should be communicated more cautiously.
\end{itemize}

\item
\section{National performance synthesis: three tests, multiple confidence standards}
A national monitoring committee prepares an end-of-year report and must synthesize evidence from three independent programs while avoiding overstatement.

\textbf{Test A (digital payment adoption).} Relative to the 70\% target, the test for whether adoption is higher yields p-value 0.012.

\textbf{Test B (export defect control).} Relative to the 5\% contract limit, the test for whether defects are lower yields p-value 0.043.

\textbf{Test C (school meal participation).} Relative to the 62\% policy floor, the test for whether participation is lower yields p-value 0.097.

The committee reviews each result at three levels: \(\alpha=0.10\), \(\alpha=0.05\), and \(\alpha=0.01\). For each test, state exactly where the claim is supported and where it is not. Distinguish findings that remain strong under stricter standards from those that only appear under more permissive standards, and explain the practical communication message for each case.

\subsection*{C6}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Test A (p = 0.012):} Supported at 10\% and 5\%, but not at 1\%; the evidence is strong but not at the strictest threshold.
    \item \textbf{Test B (p = 0.043):} Supported at 10\% and 5\%, but not at 1\%; this is moderate evidence and should not be presented as certainty.
    \item \textbf{Test C (p = 0.097):} Supported only at 10\%, not at 5\% or 1\%; this is the most threshold-sensitive finding and should be reported as tentative.
\end{itemize}
