\section{General Problem (One-Sample Mean \texorpdfstring{\(z\)}{z}-Test Template)}
A benchmark claim concerns a population mean \(\mu\). A random sample of size \(n\) has sample mean \(\bar x\), and the population standard deviation \(\sigma\) is known. At significance level \(\alpha\), test whether the true mean is lower than, higher than, or different from the benchmark \(\mu_0\).

\textbf{Questions}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item[(a)] Identify the population parameter and write hypotheses for lower-tail, higher-tail, and two-tailed alternatives.
    \item[(b)] Write the one-sample mean \(z\)-test statistic.
    \item[(c)] State how p-values are computed for each tail type.
    \item[(d)] State the decision rule using p-value and \(\alpha\).
    \item[(e)] Write a contextual conclusion template for reject/fail-to-reject outcomes.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{C5}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Parameter:} \(\mu\) = true population mean in context.
    \item \textbf{Hypotheses:}
    \[
    H_0\colon \mu=\mu_0,\qquad
    H_a\colon \mu<\mu_0\ \text{or}\ \mu>\mu_0\ \text{or}\ \mu\ne\mu_0.
    \]
    \item \textbf{Test statistic (known \(\sigma\)):}
    \[
    z=\frac{\bar x-\mu_0}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}.
    \]
    \item \textbf{p-value by tail type:}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Lower-tail: \(P(Z\le z_{obs})\)
        \item Higher-tail: \(P(Z\ge z_{obs})\)
        \item Two-tailed: \(2P(Z\ge |z_{obs}|)\)
    \end{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Decision rule:} Reject \(H_0\) if p-value \(\le\alpha\); otherwise fail to reject \(H_0\).
    \item \textbf{Conclusion template:} ``There is sufficient evidence'' (reject \(H_0\)) or ``There is not enough evidence'' (fail to reject \(H_0\)) for the contextual claim in \(H_a\).
\end{itemize}

\section{Average commute time after schedule redesign (introductory lower-tail)}
A company benchmark for average commute time is 42 minutes. After a schedule redesign, a random sample of 100 employees has mean commute time 41.0 minutes. The population standard deviation is known to be 5 minutes. At \(\alpha=5\%\), test whether the true mean commute time is lower than 42 minutes.

\subsection*{C5}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Hypotheses:} \(H_0\colon \mu=42\), \(H_a\colon \mu<42\).
    \item \textbf{Test statistic:}
    \[
    z=\frac{41.0-42}{5/\sqrt{100}}=\frac{-1.0}{0.5}=-2.00.
    \]
    \item \textbf{p-value:} \(P(Z\le -2.00)\approx 0.0228\).
    \item \textbf{Decision:} Reject \(H_0\) since \(0.0228<0.05\).
    \item \textbf{Conclusion:} There is sufficient evidence that the true mean commute time is below 42 minutes.
\end{itemize}

\section{Average monthly spending on phone plans (introductory higher-tail)}
A provider benchmark is 45 dollars per month for average phone-plan spending. A random sample of 64 customers has mean spending 46.2 dollars. The known population standard deviation is 8 dollars. At \(\alpha=5\%\), test whether the true mean spending is higher than 45 dollars.

\subsection*{C5}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Hypotheses:} \(H_0\colon \mu=45\), \(H_a\colon \mu>45\).
    \item \textbf{Test statistic:}
    \[
    z=\frac{46.2-45}{8/\sqrt{64}}=1.20.

\section{Average monthly commuting cost (introductory two-tailed)}
A benchmark for average monthly commuting cost is 220 dollars. A random sample of 64 workers has mean cost 224 dollars. The known population standard deviation is 15 dollars. At \(\alpha=5\%\), test whether the true mean is different from 220 dollars.

\subsection*{C5}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Hypotheses:} \(H_0\colon \mu=220\), \(H_a\colon \mu\ne220\).
    \item \textbf{Test statistic:}
    \[
    z=\frac{224-220}{15/\sqrt{64}}\approx 2.13.

\end{itemize}

\section{Average processing time sensitivity (lower-tail)}
A service desk benchmark is 18.0 minutes for average request processing time. A random sample of 144 requests has mean 17.65 minutes. The known population standard deviation is 2.1 minutes. Test whether the true mean is lower than 18.0 minutes at \(\alpha=5\%\) and \(\alpha=1\%\).

\subsection*{C5}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Hypotheses:} \(H_0\colon \mu=18.0\), \(H_a\colon \mu<18.0\).
    \item \textbf{Test statistic:}
    \[
    z=\frac{17.65-18.0}{2.1/\sqrt{144}}=\frac{-0.35}{0.175}=-2.00.
    \]
    \item \textbf{p-value:} \(P(Z\le -2.00)\approx 0.0228\).

    \item \textbf{Sensitivity note:} Data and statistic stay fixed; only \(\alpha\) changes the decision.
\end{itemize}

\section{Synthesis case B: one-direction-only rejection}
A benchmark for average waiting time is 18.0 minutes. A random sample of 81 customers has mean waiting time 17.29 minutes. The known population standard deviation is 3.3 minutes. Use \(\alpha=5\%\).

\textbf{Questions}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item[(a)] Test \(H_a\colon \mu<18.0\).
    \item[(b)] Test \(H_a\colon \mu>18.0\).
    \item[(c)] Test \(H_a\colon \mu\ne18.0\).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{C5}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \(z=\dfrac{17.29-18.0}{3.3/\sqrt{81}}=\dfrac{-0.71}{0.3667}\approx-1.94\).
    \item Lower-tail p-value \(\approx0.0262\), reject \(H_0\).
    \item Higher-tail p-value \(\approx0.9332\), fail to reject \(H_0\).
    \item Two-tailed p-value \(\approx0.0524\), fail to reject \(H_0\).
    \item \textbf{Conclusion:} Only the lower-direction test rejects; the opposite direction and two-tailed test fail to reject.
\end{itemize}
