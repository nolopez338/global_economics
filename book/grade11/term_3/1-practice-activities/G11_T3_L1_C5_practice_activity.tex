\ExamSection{Evaluated criteria}
\begin{ExamCriteria}
    \item C5: Calculates hypothesis tests on the mean of a population.
\end{ExamCriteria}


\section{General Problem}
A benchmark claim may refer to a population mean. A hypothesis test is conducted at a given significance level \(\alpha\) to evaluate whether the sample evidence supports or contradicts the benchmark value.

\textbf{Mean template:} A benchmark mean value (for example, 75) is given for a population mean. A sample of size \(n\) (for example, 36) is collected with sample mean \(\bar x\) (for example, 79). If provided, the population standard deviation \(\sigma\) is known (for example, 12). Using a significance level (for example, 1\%), test whether the true population mean is lower than, higher than, or different from the benchmark mean.

\textbf{Questions}
\begin{enumerate}
	\item[(a)] Write the hypotheses for the case where the true parameter is lower than the benchmark.
	\item[(b)] Write the hypotheses for the case where the true parameter is higher than the benchmark.
	\item[(c)] Write the hypotheses for the case where the true parameter is different from the benchmark.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{C5}
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{General setup:} Identify the parameter of interest, the benchmark value, the sample information, and the significance level \(\alpha\).
	\item \textbf{Mean test with known \(\sigma\) (one-sample \(z\)-test):}
	\begin{itemize}
		\item Define \(\mu\) as the true population mean and \(\mu_0\) as the benchmark mean.
		\item State hypotheses according to direction:
		\[
		H_0\colon \mu=\mu_0,\qquad H_a\colon \mu<\mu_0\ \text{or}\ \mu>\mu_0\ \text{or}\ \mu\ne \mu_0.
		\]
		\item Compute the test statistic:
		\[
		z=\frac{\bar x-\mu_0}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}.
		\]
		\item Compute the \(p\)-value from the standard normal distribution using the tail type (left-tailed, right-tailed, or two-tailed).
		\item Decision rule: reject \(H_0\) if \(p\)-value \(\le \alpha\); otherwise fail to reject \(H_0\).
		\item State the conclusion in words about the population mean relative to the benchmark.
	\end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\section{Average monthly spending on phone plans}
A mobile provider says customers spend an average of 45 dollars per month on phone plans. A random sample of 64 customers has an average of 46.2 dollars per month. The known long-run standard deviation is 8 dollars. Use a 5\% significance level to test whether the true mean spending is higher than 45 dollars.

\subsection*{C5}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Let \(\mu\) be the true mean monthly spending on phone plans.
    \item \textbf{Hypotheses:} \(H_0\colon \mu=45\), \(H_1\colon \mu>45\).
    \item \textbf{Test statistic:}
    \[
    z=\frac{46.2-45}{8/\sqrt{64}}=\frac{1.2}{1}=1.20.
    \]
    \item \textbf{\(p\)-value:} \(P(Z\ge 1.20)\approx 0.1151\).
    \item \textbf{Decision:} Fail to reject \(H_0\) since \(0.1151\ge 0.05\).
    \item \textbf{Conclusion:} There is insufficient evidence that the true average monthly phone-plan spending is above 45 dollars.
\end{itemize}

\section{Average weekly grocery budget}
A budgeting app reports that users spend 180 dollars per week on groceries on average. A random sample of 100 users shows an average of 171 dollars per week. The known long-run standard deviation is 18 dollars. Test whether the true mean is lower than 180 dollars using two significance levels: 5\% and 1\%.

\subsection*{C5}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Let \(\mu\) be the true mean weekly grocery spending.
    \item \textbf{Hypotheses:} \(H_0\colon \mu=180\), \(H_1\colon \mu<180\).
    \item \textbf{Test statistic:}
    \[
    z=\frac{171-180}{18/\sqrt{100}}=\frac{-9}{1.8}=-5.00.
    \]
    \item \textbf{\(p\)-value:} \(P(Z\le -5.00)\approx 0.0000\).
    \item \textbf{Decision at 5\%:} Reject \(H_0\) since \(0.0000<0.05\).
    \item \textbf{Decision at 1\%:} Reject \(H_0\) since \(0.0000<0.01\).
    \item \textbf{Conclusion:} At both significance levels, there is strong evidence that the true average weekly grocery spending is below 180 dollars.
\end{itemize}

\section{Average monthly commuting cost}
A workplace wellness program says employees spend 220 dollars per month on commuting on average. A random sample of 64 employees has an average of 224 dollars per month. The known long-run standard deviation is 15 dollars. Test whether the true mean is different from 220 dollars using two significance levels: 5\% and 1\%.

\subsection*{C5}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Let \(\mu\) be the true mean monthly commuting cost.
    \item \textbf{Hypotheses:} \(H_0\colon \mu=220\), \(H_1\colon \mu\ne 220\).
    \item \textbf{Test statistic:}
    \[
    z=\frac{224-220}{15/\sqrt{64}}=\frac{4}{1.875}\approx 2.13.
    \]
    \item \textbf{\(p\)-value:} \(2P(Z\ge 2.13)\approx 0.0332\).
    \item \textbf{Decision at 5\%:} Reject \(H_0\) since \(0.0332<0.05\).
    \item \textbf{Decision at 1\%:} Fail to reject \(H_0\) since \(0.0332\ge 0.01\).
    \item \textbf{Conclusion:} The data suggest a difference from 220 dollars at 5\%, but not at 1\%.
\end{itemize}

\section{Average daily screen-free time after work}
A productivity coach says people average 90 minutes of screen-free time after work each day. The known long-run standard deviation is 20 minutes. Use a 5\% significance level.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item[(a)] Sample A: A random sample of 64 people has an average of 95 minutes. Test whether the true mean is higher than 90 minutes.
    \item[(b)] Sample B: A different random sample of 100 people has an average of 84 minutes. Test whether the true mean is lower than 90 minutes.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{C5}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Let \(\mu\) be the true mean daily screen-free time after work.
    \item \textbf{(a) Higher-than test}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Hypotheses: \(H_0\colon \mu=90\), \(H_1\colon \mu>90\).
        \item Test statistic:
        \[
        z=\frac{95-90}{20/\sqrt{64}}=\frac{5}{2.5}=2.00.
        \]
        \item \(p\)-value: \(P(Z\ge 2.00)\approx 0.0228\).
        \item Decision: Reject \(H_0\) since \(0.0228<0.05\).
        \item Conclusion: There is evidence that the mean screen-free time is higher than 90 minutes.
    \end{itemize}
    \item \textbf{(b) Lower-than test}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Hypotheses: \(H_0\colon \mu=90\), \(H_1\colon \mu<90\).
        \item Test statistic:
        \[
        z=\frac{84-90}{20/\sqrt{100}}=\frac{-6}{2}=-3.00.
        \]
        \item \(p\)-value: \(P(Z\le -3.00)\approx 0.0013\).
        \item Decision: Reject \(H_0\) since \(0.0013<0.05\).
        \item Conclusion: There is evidence that the mean screen-free time is lower than 90 minutes in Sample B.
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}
