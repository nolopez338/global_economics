\item
\section{Quality control: defective export units}
A factory tests whether the defective rate is above 3\%. Let \(p\) be the true defective proportion.
Use \(H_0: p\le 0.03\), \(H_1: p>0.03\).
From a random shipment sample, analysts counted defective units, compared the sample rate with the 3\% benchmark under \(H_0\), and computed the probability of getting a result at least this high if the true rate were really 3\% or less. That test reported \(p\)-value \(=0.018\).
The company uses reference probability \(\alpha=0.05\), meaning managers only treat evidence as strong enough to stop shipments when such extreme sample results would occur less than about 5\% of the time under the benchmark claim.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item State the reported \(p\)-value and reference probability.
    \item Decide whether the \(p\)-value is smaller than, equal to, or greater than \(\alpha\).
    \item State the decision about \(H_0\).
    \item Explain why the decision follows from comparing the two probabilities.
    \item Interpret the decision for shipment policy, and state one thing the result does \emph{not} prove.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{C7}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Reported \(p\)-value: \(0.018\). Reference probability: \(\alpha=0.05\).
    \item Comparison: \(0.018<0.05\).
    \item Decision: reject \(H_0\).
    \item Context meaning of the \(p\)-value: if the true defective rate were really at most 3\%, getting a sample this bad (or worse) would happen about 1.8\% of the time, so the observed sample is relatively unusual under the benchmark.
    \item Context meaning of the reference probability: the factory's 5\% rule is its evidence standard before pausing exports, balancing product-risk and operational cost.
    \item Why the comparison justifies the decision: because 1.8\% is below that 5\% evidence standard, the sample provides enough evidence to move away from the benchmark claim \(p\le 0.03\).
    \item Practical interpretation: the quality team has support for stopping shipment and running full inspection until defect control improves.
    \item Limitation: this does not prove every future shipment is above 3\% defects or identify the exact true defect rate.
\end{itemize}

\section{Logistics: on-time delivery target}
A logistics team checks whether on-time delivery has fallen below 92\%. Let \(p\) be the true on-time proportion.
Use \(H_0: p\ge 0.92\), \(H_1: p<0.92\).
In a monthly audit, analysts sampled completed deliveries, measured how far the observed on-time share was below 92\%, and used a hypothesis test to find how likely a drop at least that large would be if true performance were still 92\% or higher. The test reported \(p\)-value \(=0.041\).
The operations board set \(\alpha=0.05\): they launch corrective action only when the sample evidence would be this extreme less than 5\% of the time under the target-meeting claim.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Identify the \(p\)-value and the reference probability.
    \item Compare the \(p\)-value with \(\alpha\).
    \item State whether to reject or fail to reject \(H_0\).
    \item Explain what this means for launching a performance-improvement plan.
    \item State one thing this result does \emph{not} prove.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{C7}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \(p\)-value \(=0.041\), reference probability \(\alpha=0.05\).
    \item Since \(0.041<0.05\), the \(p\)-value is smaller than the reference probability.
    \item Decision: reject \(H_0\).
    \item Context meaning of the \(p\)-value: assuming true on-time performance were still at least 92\%, a sample this weak (or weaker) would occur only about 4.1\% of the time.
    \item Context meaning of \(\alpha\): the team accepts at most a 5\% rarity cutoff before declaring performance has likely slipped below target.
    \item Why the comparison leads to rejection: 4.1\% is below the 5\% cutoff, so the evidence is strong enough under the team rule to doubt \(H_0\).
    \item Practical interpretation: launching a performance-improvement plan is supported by the data.
    \item Limitation: this does not prove every delivery is late or quantify exactly how far below 92\% the true rate is.
\end{itemize}

Use \(H_0: \mu=40\), \(H_1: \mu>40\).
Analysts sampled processing records across shifts, compared the sample mean to the 40-minute benchmark, and computed how probable a delay this large (or larger) would be if the true mean were really 40 minutes. The test output gave \(p\)-value \(=0.006\).
The port authority uses \(\alpha=0.05\), meaning staffing changes are triggered only when evidence against the 40-minute benchmark is very unlikely (below 5\%) under that benchmark.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Compare the \(p\)-value and \(\alpha\).
    \item State the decision about \(H_0\).
    \item Explain why that decision follows from the comparison.
    \item Interpret the decision for staffing policy and state one limitation.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{C7}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \(p\)-value \(=0.006\), \(\alpha=0.05\).
    \item Comparison: \(0.006<0.05\).
    \item Decision: reject \(H_0\).
    \item Context meaning of the \(p\)-value: if the true average were actually 40 minutes, seeing a sample mean this high (or higher) would happen only about 0.6\% of the time.
    \item Context meaning of \(\alpha\): the authority's 5\% rule is a caution standard before committing resources to extra staffing.

    \item Practical interpretation: managers have strong support for adding staffing or redesigning workflow to reduce delays.
    \item Limitation: this does not prove every shipment takes more than 40 minutes or reveal the exact cause of delays.

\section{Macroeconomics: mean inflation monitoring}
Analysts test whether mean monthly inflation is above 4.0\%. Let \(\mu\) be the true mean monthly inflation rate.
Use \(H_0: \mu\le 4.0\), \(H_1: \mu>4.0\).
Using recent monthly observations, they compared the sample mean with the 4.0\% benchmark and calculated how likely an increase at least this large would be if true mean inflation were 4.0\% or lower. The report gave \(p\)-value \(=0.27\).
Policy staff use \(\alpha=0.05\): tightening recommendations are made only when such a result would be rare (under 5\%) if the benchmark claim were true.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Compare \(0.27\) and \(0.05\).
    \item State the decision about \(H_0\).
    \item Explain why this comparison leads to that decision.
    \item Interpret what policymakers can conclude, and what they cannot conclude.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{C7}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \(p\)-value \(=0.27\), reference probability \(\alpha=0.05\).
    \item Comparison: \(0.27>0.05\).
    \item Decision: fail to reject \(H_0\).
    \item Context meaning of the \(p\)-value: if true mean inflation were at most 4.0\%, a sample result this high (or higher) is not very unusual; it could occur about 27\% of the time.
    \item Context meaning of \(\alpha\): policymakers require much rarer evidence than that (below 5\%) before claiming inflation is above target.
    \item Why the comparison leads to fail-to-reject: 27\% is far above the evidence standard, so the sample does not justify rejecting the benchmark claim.
    \item Practical interpretation: the current data do not provide strong evidence that mean inflation exceeds 4.0\%.
    \item Limitation: this does not prove inflation is permanently under control or exactly equal to 4.0\%.
\end{itemize}

\section{Banking: loan approval fairness check}
A regulator tests whether the approval rate for small-business loans is below 60\%.
Let \(p\) be the true approval proportion. Use \(H_0: p\ge 0.60\), \(H_1: p<0.60\).
From reviewed loan files, analysts computed the sample approval share, compared it with the 60\% benchmark, and used a hypothesis test to find the probability of a shortfall at least this large if true approvals were actually 60\% or higher. The test reported \(p\)-value \(=0.049\).
The regulator uses \(\alpha=0.05\), meaning an official fairness audit starts only when the observed shortfall would be rare enough (below 5\%) under the no-problem benchmark.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Compare the \(p\)-value with the reference probability.
    \item State the correct decision about \(H_0\).
    \item Interpret the decision for whether a fairness audit should start.
    \item Explain why being close to \(\alpha\) does not change the comparison rule.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{C7}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \(p\)-value \(=0.049\), \(\alpha=0.05\).
    \item Comparison: \(0.049<0.05\), even though the gap is small.
    \item Decision: reject \(H_0\).
    \item Context meaning of the \(p\)-value: if the true approval rate were at least 60\%, a sample this low (or lower) would occur about 4.9\% of the time.
    \item Context meaning of \(\alpha\): the regulator's threshold represents the caution level for launching investigations that can be costly and sensitive.
    \item Why comparison implies rejection: 4.9\% is just below the 5\% standard, so the test meets the predefined evidence rule.
    \item Practical interpretation: opening a fairness audit is justified by the data under the regulator's rule.
    \item Limitation: this does not prove intentional discrimination or identify the exact mechanism behind lower approvals.
\end{itemize}

\section{Digital payments: fraud checks in two regions}
A payment platform runs separate fraud-rate tests in Region A and Region B.
For each region, use \(H_0: p\le 0.02\), \(H_1: p>0.02\), where \(p\) is the fraud proportion.
Each regional analytics team used its own transaction sample, compared observed fraud rates with the 2\% benchmark, and calculated how likely results at least that extreme would be if true fraud were 2\% or less.
Region A reported \(p\)-value \(=0.012\) with \(\alpha=0.05\).
Region B reported \(p\)-value \(=0.19\) with \(\alpha=0.05\).
The same 5\% reference probability is used in both regions so decisions are made with the same evidence standard before strict blocking is activated.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Compare each \(p\)-value with its reference probability.
    \item State the decision for Region A and Region B separately.
    \item Interpret each decision for activating strict blocking rules.
    \item Briefly explain why the two regions can have different decisions under the same \(\alpha\).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{C7}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Region A: reported \(p\)-value is \(0.012\), reference probability is \(0.05\), and \(0.012<0.05\) so reject \(H_0\).
    \item Region B: reported \(p\)-value is \(0.19\), reference probability is \(0.05\), and \(0.19>0.05\) so fail to reject \(H_0\).
    \item Meaning of Region A \(p\)-value: if true fraud were at most 2\%, Region A's observed level would be quite unusual (about 1.2\%), which is below the decision standard.
    \item Meaning of Region B \(p\)-value: if true fraud were at most 2\%, Region B's result is not unusual enough (about 19\%) to pass the same standard.
    \item Meaning of common \(\alpha\): both regions require evidence rarer than 5\% before triggering strict blocking.
    \item Practical interpretation: strict blocking is supported for Region A, but not yet for Region B.
    \item Limitation: this does not prove every suspicious transaction in Region A is fraud, and it does not prove Region B has no fraud issue.
    \item Why decisions differ: independent samples can show different fraud patterns, so each test can produce a different \(p\)-value even under one policy threshold.
\end{itemize}

\section{Telecom: churn result under two reference probabilities}
A telecom provider tests whether monthly churn exceeds 12\%. Let \(p\) be true churn proportion.
Use \(H_0: p\le 0.12\), \(H_1: p>0.12\).
Analysts sampled customer exits, compared the observed churn share with 12\%, and computed how likely a result at least this high would be if true churn were actually 12\% or less. The study reported one \(p\)-value: \(0.047\).
Management reviews this same test under two reference probabilities: \(\alpha=0.05\) for routine intervention, and \(\alpha=0.01\) when budgets are tight and only very strong evidence can justify costly discounts.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Compare \(0.047\) with \(0.05\), then with \(0.01\).
    \item State the decision at each reference probability.
    \item Explain why the decision changes or does not change when the threshold changes.
    \item Give the practical interpretation for retention discounts at each threshold.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{C7}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Reported \(p\)-value is \(0.047\).
    \item At \(\alpha=0.05\): \(0.047<0.05\) so reject \(H_0\).
    \item At \(\alpha=0.01\): \(0.047>0.01\) so fail to reject \(H_0\).
    \item Meaning of the \(p\)-value in context: if true churn were at most 12\%, seeing a sample this high (or higher) would happen about 4.7\% of the time.
    \item Meaning of the two reference probabilities: 5\% is a moderate evidence bar for routine action, while 1\% is a much stricter bar requiring rarer evidence before spending heavily.
    \item Why the decision changes: 4.7\% is below 5\% but not below 1\%, so the same evidence passes one decision rule and fails the stricter one.
    \item Practical interpretation: routine retention discounts are supported at 5\%, but under the 1\% policy the evidence is not strong enough for the same action.
    \item Limitation: this does not prove churn will keep rising every month.
\end{itemize}

\section{Public services: waiting-time tests across departments}
A city compares waiting-time targets in three departments using independent tests.
Each department collected its own service records, compared the sample average wait with its benchmark, and computed a \(p\)-value for getting a wait this high (or higher) if the benchmark were true.
Each test uses the reference probability shown below.
\begin{itemize}
    \item Clinic: test if mean wait exceeds 30 minutes; \(p\)-value \(=0.021\), \(\alpha=0.05\).
    \item Licensing office: test if mean wait exceeds 25 minutes; \(p\)-value \(=0.14\), \(\alpha=0.05\).
    \item Call center: test if mean wait exceeds 6 minutes; \(p\)-value \(=0.008\), \(\alpha=0.01\).
\end{itemize}
Here, 5\% is the standard trigger for most units, while 1\% for the call center reflects a stricter requirement before making high-cost scheduling changes.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item For each department, compare the \(p\)-value with its corresponding \(\alpha\).
    \item State reject/fail-to-reject decisions for all three tests.
    \item Interpret each decision in operational terms.
    \item State one caution about what these test results do not prove.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{C7}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Clinic: \(p\)-value \(0.021\), reference probability \(0.05\), and \(0.021<0.05\) so reject \(H_0\). The result is rare enough under the 30-minute benchmark to support action.
    \item Licensing office: \(p\)-value \(0.14\), reference probability \(0.05\), and \(0.14>0.05\) so fail to reject \(H_0\). The observed delay is not rare enough under the benchmark claim.
    \item Call center: \(p\)-value \(0.008\), reference probability \(0.01\), and \(0.008<0.01\) so reject \(H_0\). Even the stricter 1\% standard is met.
    \item Meaning of reference probabilities: each unit compares its evidence to its own policy standard for when intervention is justified.
    \item Practical interpretation: prioritize corrective staffing for clinic and call center; for licensing, current data do not yet justify declaring waits above target.
    \item Limitation: these results do not prove exact future wait times or isolate the operational causes.
\end{itemize}

\section{Labor market policy: stability and change across thresholds}
An agency evaluates two independent claims.
For each claim, analysts used labor-market data to compute how unusual the observed change would be if the corresponding no-change benchmark were true, producing the following \(p\)-values:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Claim A (youth unemployment increased): \(p\)-value \(=0.004\).
    \item Claim B (average training completion improved): \(p\)-value \(=0.038\).
\end{itemize}
Use both \(\alpha=0.05\) and \(\alpha=0.01\) for each claim.
The 5\% threshold supports routine policy updates, while 1\% represents a stricter standard used before major national reallocation.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Compare each \(p\)-value with both thresholds.
    \item State the decision for Claim A at 5\% and 1\%.
    \item State the decision for Claim B at 5\% and 1\%.
    \item Identify which claim is stable across thresholds and which changes.
    \item Interpret these patterns in policy language and include one limitation.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{C7}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Claim A: \(p\)-value \(0.004\); compare with \(0.05\) and \(0.01\): \(0.004<0.05\) and \(0.004<0.01\), so reject \(H_0\) at both thresholds.
    \item Claim B: \(p\)-value \(0.038\); compare with \(0.05\) and \(0.01\): \(0.038<0.05\) but \(0.038>0.01\), so reject at 5\% and fail to reject at 1\%.
    \item Meaning of \(p\)-values: Claim A's observed change is very unlikely under its benchmark, while Claim B's result is moderately unlikely but not extremely rare.
    \item Meaning of thresholds: 1\% demands stronger evidence than 5\%, so only results with very small \(p\)-values remain significant there.
    \item Practical interpretation: concern about youth unemployment increase is robust under both caution levels; support for improved training completion depends on using the less strict 5\% policy standard.
    \item Limitation: this does not prove the size of either effect or identify all economic drivers.
\end{itemize}

\section{Economic monitoring dashboard: mixed evidence across indicators}
A ministry tests four independent indicators and must report decisions at both \(\alpha=0.10\) and \(\alpha=0.05\).
For each indicator, analysts compared recent data with a policy benchmark and computed a \(p\)-value: the probability of seeing a result at least this extreme if the benchmark claim were true.
\begin{itemize}
    \item Inflation persistence above target: \(p\)-value \(=0.072\).
    \item Export growth slowdown: \(p\)-value \(=0.049\).
    \item Public transport delays worsening: \(p\)-value \(=0.003\).
    \item Small-business confidence drop: \(p\)-value \(=0.11\).
\end{itemize}
Here, 10\% is an early-warning threshold for preliminary action, while 5\% is a stricter standard for stronger policy commitments.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item For each indicator, compare the \(p\)-value with 0.10 and with 0.05.
    \item State reject/fail-to-reject decisions at both thresholds for all indicators.
    \item Identify which indicators pass at 10\% but not at 5\%, which pass at both, and which pass at neither.
    \item Write a short policy summary that correctly reflects what the comparisons do and do not justify.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{C7}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Inflation persistence: \(p\)-value \(0.072\). Compare with thresholds: \(0.072<0.10\) but \(0.072>0.05\), so reject at 10\% and fail to reject at 5\%.
    \item Export slowdown: \(p\)-value \(0.049\). Since \(0.049<0.10\) and \(0.049<0.05\), reject at both thresholds.
    \item Transport delays: \(p\)-value \(0.003\). Since \(0.003<0.10\) and \(0.003<0.05\), reject at both thresholds.
    \item Confidence drop: \(p\)-value \(0.11\). Since \(0.11>0.10\) and \(0.11>0.05\), fail to reject at both thresholds.
    \item Meaning of \(p\)-values in context: smaller values indicate sample evidence less compatible with the benchmark claim; larger values indicate results that are not rare under that claim.
    \item Meaning of the two reference probabilities: 10\% allows earlier but less certain alerts, while 5\% requires stronger evidence before firm decisions.
    \item Practical policy interpretation: evidence is strongest and robust for export slowdown and transport delays, moderate for inflation persistence (only early-warning level), and insufficient for a confidence-drop claim.
    \item Limitation: these tests do not prove causality or guarantee future movements of each indicator.
\end{itemize}
