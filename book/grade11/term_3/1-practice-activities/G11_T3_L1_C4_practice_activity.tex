	\section{General Problem}
	A benchmark claim may refer to a population proportion. A hypothesis test is conducted at a given significance level \(\alpha\) to evaluate whether the sample evidence supports or contradicts the benchmark value.
	
	\textbf{Proportion template:} A benchmark percentage (for example, 62\%) is given for a population proportion. A sample of size \(n\) (for example, 200) is collected, with observed successes \(x\) (for example, 112). Using a significance level (for example, 5\%), test whether the true population proportion is lower than, higher than, or different from the benchmark percentage.
	
	\textbf{Questions}
	\begin{enumerate}
	    \item[(a)] Write the hypotheses for the case where the true parameter is lower than the benchmark.
	    \item[(b)] Write the hypotheses for the case where the true parameter is higher than the benchmark.
	    \item[(c)] Write the hypotheses for the case where the true parameter is different from the benchmark.
	\end{enumerate}
	
	\subsection*{C4}
	\begin{itemize}
	    \item \textbf{General setup:} Identify the parameter of interest, the benchmark value, the sample information, and the significance level \(\alpha\).
	    \item \textbf{Proportion test (one-sample \(z\)-test):}
	    \begin{itemize}
	        \item Define \(p\) as the true population proportion and \(p_0\) as the benchmark proportion.
	        \item State hypotheses according to direction:
	        \[
	        H_0\colon p=p_0,\qquad H_a\colon p<p_0\ \text{or}\ p>p_0\ \text{or}\ p\ne p_0.
	        \]
	        \item Check large-sample conditions using \(n\) and \(p_0\): \(np_0\ge 10\) and \(n(1-p_0)\ge 10\).
	        \item Compute the sample proportion: \(\hat p=\dfrac{x}{n}\).
	        \item Compute the test statistic:
	        \[
	        z=\frac{\hat p-p_0}{\sqrt{\frac{p_0(1-p_0)}{n}}}.
	        \]
	        \item Compute the \(p\)-value from the standard normal distribution using the tail type (left-tailed, right-tailed, or two-tailed).
	        \item Decision rule: reject \(H_0\) if \(p\)-value \(\le \alpha\); otherwise fail to reject \(H_0\).
	        \item State the conclusion in words about the population proportion relative to the benchmark.
	    \end{itemize}
	
	\end{itemize}
	
	\item
	\section{Food delivery completion rate among part-time couriers}
	A gig platform reports that 64\% of assigned food delivery orders are completed without cancellation by part-time couriers in their twenties. In a random sample of 220 assigned orders, 129 are completed without cancellation. Using a significance level of 5\%, test whether the true completion proportion is lower than 64\%.
	
	\subsection*{C4}
	\begin{itemize}
	    \item \textbf{Hypotheses:} \(H_0\colon p=0.64\), \(H_a\colon p<0.64\).
	    \item \textbf{Conditions:} \(np_0=220(0.64)=140.8\ge 10\), \(n(1-p_0)=220(0.36)=79.2\ge 10\).
	    \item \textbf{Sample statistic:} \(\hat p=129/220\approx 0.586\).
	    \item \textbf{Test statistic:}
	    \[
	    z=\frac{0.586-0.64}{\sqrt{\frac{0.64(0.36)}{220}}}\approx -1.66.
	    \]
	    \item \textbf{\(p\)-value:} \(P(Z\le -1.66)\approx 0.0485\), or about 4.85\%.
	    \item \textbf{Decision:} Reject \(H_0\) because 4.85\% is less than 5\%.
	    \item \textbf{Conclusion:} There is sufficient evidence that the completion rate is lower than 64\%.
	\end{itemize}

	\section{Average one-way commute time for new office workers}
	A company benchmark says that workers in their twenties spend 42 minutes on average for a one-way commute. A random sample of 49 new employees has an average one-way commute of 44.8 minutes, and the population standard deviation is known to be 8 minutes. Using a significance level of 5\%, test whether the true average commute time is higher than 42 minutes.
	
	\subsection*{C4}
	\begin{itemize}
	    \item \textbf{Hypotheses:} \(H_0\colon \mu=42\), \(H_a\colon \mu>42\).
	    \item \textbf{Sample statistic:} \(\bar x=44.8\).
	    \item \textbf{Test statistic:}
	    \[
	    z=\frac{44.8-42}{8/\sqrt{49}}=\frac{2.8}{1.143}\approx 2.45.
	    \]
	    \item \textbf{\(p\)-value:} \(P(Z\ge 2.45)\approx 0.0071\), or about 0.71\%.
	    \item \textbf{Decision:} Reject \(H_0\) because 0.71\% is less than 5\%.
	    \item \textbf{Conclusion:} There is sufficient evidence that the true average commute time is higher than 42 minutes.
	\end{itemize}

	\section{Weekly freelance income for recent graduates}
	A university career office claims that recent graduates in their twenties earn 310 dollars per week on average from freelance work. A random sample of 64 graduates has an average weekly freelance income of 298 dollars, and the population standard deviation is known to be 40 dollars. Using a significance level of 10\%, test whether the true average weekly freelance income is different from 310 dollars.
	
	\subsection*{C4}
	\begin{itemize}
	    \item \textbf{Hypotheses:} \(H_0\colon \mu=310\), \(H_a\colon \mu\ne 310\).
	    \item \textbf{Sample statistic:} \(\bar x=298\).
	    \item \textbf{Test statistic:}
	    \[
	    z=\frac{298-310}{40/\sqrt{64}}=\frac{-12}{5}=-2.40.
	    \]
	    \item \textbf{\(p\)-value:} \(2P(Z\le -2.40)\approx 0.0164\), or about 1.64\%.
	    \item \textbf{Decision:} Reject \(H_0\) because 1.64\% is less than 10\%.
	    \item \textbf{Conclusion:} There is sufficient evidence that the true average weekly freelance income is different from 310 dollars.
	\end{itemize}

	\section{On-time rent payment among young tenants}
	A housing platform benchmark says that 78\% of tenants in their twenties pay rent on time. In a random sample of 300 tenants, 223 paid on time. Use a significance level of 10\% for both parts.
	
	\textbf{Questions}
	\begin{enumerate}
	    \item[(a)] Test whether the true on-time payment proportion is lower than 78\%.
	    \item[(b)] Test whether the true on-time payment proportion is higher than 78\%.
	\end{enumerate}
	
	\subsection*{C4}
	\textbf{Part (a): Left-tailed test}
	\begin{itemize}
	    \item \textbf{Hypotheses:} \(H_0\colon p=0.78\), \(H_a\colon p<0.78\).
	    \item \textbf{Conditions:} \(np_0=300(0.78)=234\ge 10\), \(n(1-p_0)=300(0.22)=66\ge 10\).
	    \item \textbf{Sample statistic:} \(\hat p=223/300\approx 0.743\).
	    \item \textbf{Test statistic:}
	    \[
	    z=\frac{0.743-0.78}{\sqrt{\frac{0.78(0.22)}{300}}}\approx -1.53.
	    \]
	    \item \textbf{\(p\)-value:} \(P(Z\le -1.53)\approx 0.0630\), or about 6.30\%.
	    \item \textbf{Decision:} Reject \(H_0\) because 6.30\% is less than 10\%.
	    \item \textbf{Conclusion:} There is sufficient evidence that the on-time rent payment proportion is lower than 78\%.
	\end{itemize}
	
	\textbf{Part (b): Right-tailed test}
	\begin{itemize}
	    \item \textbf{Hypotheses:} \(H_0\colon p=0.78\), \(H_a\colon p>0.78\).
	    \item \textbf{Conditions:} \(np_0=234\ge 10\), \(n(1-p_0)=66\ge 10\).
	    \item \textbf{Sample statistic:} \(\hat p\approx 0.743\).
	    \item \textbf{Test statistic:} \(z\approx -1.53\).
	    \item \textbf{\(p\)-value:} \(P(Z\ge -1.53)\approx 0.9370\), or about 93.70\%.
	    \item \textbf{Decision:} Fail to reject \(H_0\) because 93.70\% is greater than 10\%.
	    \item \textbf{Conclusion:} There is not enough evidence to claim the on-time rent payment proportion is higher than 78\%.
	\end{itemize}

	A university support office states that students in their twenties who also work part-time study 18 hours per week on average. A random sample of 36 such students has an average of 16.9 study hours per week, and the population standard deviation is known to be 3.6 hours. Use a significance level of 5\% for both parts.
	
	\textbf{Questions}
	\begin{enumerate}
	    \item[(a)] Test whether the true average weekly study time is lower than 18 hours.
	    \item[(b)] Test whether the true average weekly study time is different from 18 hours.
	\end{enumerate}
	
	\subsection*{C4}

	    \item \textbf{Test statistic:} \(z\approx -1.83\).

	\textbf{Questions}

	    \item[(b)] Test whether the true proportion using written contracts is different from 52\%.
