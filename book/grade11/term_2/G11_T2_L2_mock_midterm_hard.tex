		\SubsectionBox{Criteria assessment}\vspace{-0.25cm}
		In this hard version, criteria C1, C5, and C6 are evaluated across problems 1--4; criteria C2, C3, and C4 are evaluated across problems 5--8; and criterion C7 is evaluated across problems 9--11. A criterion is considered passed when it is correctly activated in at least three problems.

		\vspace{0.25cm}
		\SubsectionBox{1. (C1,C5,C6) Maintenance Cost Comparison Across Two Rail Depots}\vspace{-0.25cm}
		A regional rail authority compares average monthly maintenance cost per trainset (thousand USD) in two depots after introducing predictive maintenance software.
		The grouped data are:

		\textbf{Depot A (27 trainsets):} 72(5), 78(7), 85(6), 92(5), 100(4).

		\textbf{Depot B (28 trainsets):} 68(4), 75(6), 83(8), 90(6), 98(4).

		From long-run accounting records, the population standard deviation is known to be $\sigma = 14$ thousand USD for both depots.
		Construct 90\%, 95\%, and 99\% confidence intervals for each depot mean.
		Then compute required sample sizes for target margins of error $E=3.5$ and $E=2.8$ (each at 90\%, 95\%, and 99\%), and determine additional observations needed.

		\vspace{0.25cm}
		\SubsectionBox{2. (C1,C5,C6) Fraud-Review Time Comparison by Team}\vspace{-0.25cm}
		A digital payments platform tracks average fraud-review handling time (minutes) for two independent teams.
		Times are reported as class intervals.

		\textbf{Team A (38 cases):} 42--46(6), 47--51(9), 52--56(10), 57--61(8), 62--66(5).

		\textbf{Team B (40 cases):} 40--44(5), 45--49(8), 50--54(11), 55--59(9), 60--64(7).

		Assume known population standard deviation $\sigma=6.5$ minutes.
		Construct 90\%, 95\%, 99\% intervals for each team.
		Then plan required sample sizes for $E=1.2$ and $E=0.9$ at each confidence level, including additional cases needed.

		\vspace{0.25cm}
		\SubsectionBox{3. (C1,C5,C6) Dispatch Completion Time in an Expansion Zone}\vspace{-0.25cm}
		An emergency logistics center records warehouse dispatch completion times (hours) for one expansion zone.
		A random sample of 18 dispatches is:
		31, 29, 34, 28, 33, 35, 30, 32, 36, 27, 34, 31, 29, 33, 30, 37, 28, 32.
		Known population standard deviation is $\sigma=5.8$ hours.
		Build 90\%, 95\%, and 99\% confidence intervals for the population mean completion time.
		Then compute required $n$ for target errors $E=2.0$ and $E=1.5$, and additional dispatches needed.

		\SubsectionBox{4. (C1,C5,C6) Audit Value Recovery Comparison Across Field Units}\vspace{-0.25cm}
		A tax administration compares average daily audit value recovered (thousand USD) by two independent field units.

		\textbf{Unit A (15 days):} 112, 108, 115, 121, 117, 109, 114, 118, 116, 113, 120, 111, 119, 107, 122.

		\textbf{Unit B (18 days):} 105, 109, 111, 114, 108, 112, 116, 110, 113, 107, 115, 118, 106, 109, 117, 111, 114, 108.

		Assume known $\sigma=9.2$ thousand USD in both units.
		Construct 90\%, 95\%, 99\% intervals and compute sample-size plans for $E=3.0$ and $E=2.2$.

		\vspace{0.25cm}
		\SubsectionBox{5. (C2,C3,C4) Fintech Repayment Delay Supervision Analysis}\vspace{-0.25cm}
		A central bank supervision office evaluates a fintech lender.
		One team reports from a recent compliance sample: average repayment delay was 4.7 days with sample spread 2.1 days, and a 95\% CI for the population mean delay was [4.1, 5.3].
		A second team cites long-run portfolio parameters from the full national borrower base: true mean delay $\mu=4.9$ and true standard deviation $\sigma=2.4$.
		The policy division also references a strategic threshold: ``acceptable mean delay below 5.0 days.''

		Explicit analysis tasks:
		1) Classify every numerical quantity above as sample statistic, population parameter, or policy benchmark and justify.
		2) Identify point estimate(s) and explain information lost versus interval form.
		3) Compare precision if a second CI [4.3, 5.1] is produced; explain conceptually what design changes could narrow intervals.
		4) State which mean values are ruled out/plausible under [4.1, 5.3] and explain ``95\% confidence'' in repeated-sampling language.

		\vspace{0.25cm}
		\SubsectionBox{6. (C2,C3,C4) Border Port Turnaround Analysis for Policy Decisions}\vspace{-0.25cm}
		A national logistics ministry monitors truck turnaround time at border ports.
		A pilot sample from Port North gives mean 11.8 hours and 90\% CI [10.9, 12.7].
		A separate audit sample from Port South gives mean 12.4 hours and 90\% CI [11.6, 13.2].
		Historic all-port parameters for the old regime were $\mu=12.1$ hours and $\sigma=3.0$ hours.
		A policy KPI requires maintaining average turnaround at or below 12.0 hours.

		Explicit analysis tasks:
		1) Classify sample vs population vs policy quantities.
		2) Identify point estimates and explain why point-only reporting is insufficient for capital-allocation decisions.
		3) Compare precision between North and South intervals and list conceptual levers to narrow both.
		4) For each port, state ruled-out/plausible means and explain operational meaning of 90\% confidence.

		\vspace{0.25cm}
		\SubsectionBox{7. (C2,C3,C4) Social Protection Processing-Time Channel Comparison}\vspace{-0.25cm}
		A social protection agency studies benefit-payment processing time across two digital channels.
		Channel A monitoring sample reports mean 6.3 days with CI [5.8, 6.8] at 95\%.
		Channel B audit sample reports mean 6.0 days with CI [5.2, 6.8] at 95\%.
		Legacy national parameters before automation were $\mu=6.7$ and $\sigma=1.9$ days.
		The ministry also publishes two benchmarks: warning level 6.5 days, excellent level 6.0 days.

		Explicit analysis tasks:
		1) Categorize all values into sample statistics, population parameters, and policy benchmarks.
		2) Identify point estimates and explain what interval endpoints add.
		3) Compare interval precision across channels and propose practical changes to obtain tighter intervals.
		4) Determine plausible/ruled-out means per channel and explain repeated-sampling meaning of 95\% confidence.

		\SubsectionBox{8. (C2,C3,C4) External Fund Manager Return Reporting Analysis}\vspace{-0.25cm}
		A sovereign wealth monitoring office evaluates monthly return volatility reporting from two external fund managers.
		Manager Alpha reports a sample mean monthly return of 1.4\% with 95\% CI [0.8\%, 2.0\%].
		Manager Beta reports a sample mean of 1.1\% with 95\% CI [0.2\%, 2.0\%].
		Historical long-run population parameters from the full investment universe are $\mu=1.3\%$ and $\sigma=3.5\%$.
		The board uses two policy thresholds: ``underperforming'' if mean return $<1.0\%$ and ``stretch target'' if mean return $\ge 1.8\%$.

		Explicit analysis tasks:
		1) Classify all quantities into sample statistics, population parameters, and policy thresholds.
		2) Identify point estimates and explain why interval reporting is superior for strategic allocation.
		3) Compare interval precision between Alpha and Beta and state conceptual ways to reduce uncertainty.
		4) For each manager, state ruled-out and plausible values and explain repeated-sampling meaning of 95\% confidence.

		\SubsectionBox{9. (C7) Supplier On-Time Submission Proportion by Region}\vspace{-0.25cm}
		A public procurement platform reports supplier on-time document submission rates for two regions.
		Region East sample: 420 suppliers with 246 on-time.
		Region West sample: 380 suppliers with 186 on-time.
		Construct 90\%, 95\%, and 99\% confidence intervals for each region's population proportion.
		Report margin of error values.
		Check large-sample conditions.
		Then compute required sample size for targets $E=0.04$ and $E=0.03$ at each confidence level using each region's $\hat{p}$, and additional suppliers needed.

		\SubsectionBox{10. (C7) Digital Tax Filing Completion by Business Subgroup}\vspace{-0.25cm}
		A municipal e-governance program evaluates digital tax filing completion by business size subgroup in one city.
		Small firms: $n=250$, completed $x=142$.
		Medium firms: $n=200$, completed $x=96$.
		Large firms: $n=150$, completed $x=57$.
		Construct 90\% and 95\% confidence intervals for each subgroup proportion, with margins of error and condition checks.
		Then, for Small and Large firms, compute required sample size at 95\% to achieve $E=0.05$ using each subgroup's $\hat{p}$.
