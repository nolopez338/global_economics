	\ExamSection{Evaluated criteria}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Evaluated criteria}
	\begin{ExamCriteria}
		\item C1: Compute the sample mean and sample standard deviation.
		\item C2: Distinguish between sample statistics and population parameters.
		\item C3: Explain why interval estimation is preferred over a single point estimate.
		\item C4: Interpret the meaning of a X\% confidence interval in context.
		\item C5: Construct a X\% confidence interval using known population standard deviation.
		\item C6: Determine the required sample size to achieve a target margin of error.
		\item C7: Construct and interpret a confidence interval for a population proportion.
	\end{ExamCriteria}

		\subsection*{Problem description}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{Problem description}
		A regional rail authority compares average monthly maintenance cost per trainset (thousand USD) in two depots after introducing predictive maintenance software.
		The grouped data are:
		
		\textbf{Depot A (27 trainsets):} 72(5), 78(7), 85(6), 92(5), 100(4).
		
		\textbf{Depot B (28 trainsets):} 68(4), 75(6), 83(8), 90(6), 98(4).
		
		From long-run accounting records, the population standard deviation is known to be $\sigma = 14$ thousand USD for both depots.
		Construct 90\%, 95\%, and 99\% confidence intervals for each depot mean.
		Then compute required sample sizes for target margins of error $E=3.5$ and $E=2.8$ (each at 90\%, 95\%, and 99\%), and determine additional observations needed.
		
		\subsection*{C1}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{C1}
		\textbf{Depot A}
		\[
		\sum fx = 72(5)+78(7)+85(6)+92(5)+100(4)=2276,
		\qquad \bar{x}_A=\frac{2276}{27}=84.30
		\]
		\[
		\sum f(x-\bar{x}_A)^2=2323.48,
		\quad s_A^2=\frac{2323.48}{26}=89.36,
		\quad s_A\approx 9.45
		\]
		\textbf{Depot B}
		\[
		\sum fx = 68(4)+75(6)+83(8)+90(6)+98(4)=2318,
		\qquad \bar{x}_B=\frac{2318}{28}=82.79
		\]
		\[
		\sum f(x-\bar{x}_B)^2=2475.64,
		\quad s_B^2=\frac{2475.64}{27}=91.69,
		\quad s_B\approx 9.58
		\]
		
		\subsection*{C5}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{C5}
		For Depot A, $SE_A=\frac{14}{\sqrt{27}}\approx2.694$.
		\[
		E_{A,90}=1.645(2.694)=4.43,\ \ CI_{90}:\ 84.30\pm4.43=[79.87,88.73]
		\]
		\[
		E_{A,95}=1.96(2.694)=5.28,\ \ CI_{95}:\ 84.30\pm5.28=[79.02,89.58]
		\]
		\[
		E_{A,99}=2.576(2.694)=6.94,\ \ CI_{99}:\ 84.30\pm6.94=[77.36,91.24]
		\]
		For Depot B, $SE_B=\frac{14}{\sqrt{28}}\approx2.646$.
		\[
		E_{B,90}=1.645(2.646)=4.35,\ \ CI_{90}:\ 82.79\pm4.35=[78.43,87.14]
		\]
		\[
		E_{B,95}=1.96(2.646)=5.19,\ \ CI_{95}:\ 82.79\pm5.19=[77.60,87.97]
		\]
		\[
		E_{B,99}=2.576(2.646)=6.82,\ \ CI_{99}:\ 82.79\pm6.82=[75.97,89.60]
		\]
		
		\subsection*{C6}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{C6}
		Use $n=\left(\frac{z^*\sigma}{E}\right)^2$ and round up.
		For $E=3.5$:
		\[
		n_{90}=\left(\frac{1.645\cdot14}{3.5}\right)^2=43.32\Rightarrow44,
		n_{95}=61.47\Rightarrow62,
		n_{99}=106.15\Rightarrow107
		\]
		Additional observations:
		Depot A ($n=27$): $17,35,80$; Depot B ($n=28$): $16,34,79$.
		For $E=2.8$:
		\[
		n_{90}=67.68\Rightarrow68,
		n_{95}=96.05\Rightarrow97,
		n_{99}=165.86\Rightarrow166
		\]
		Additional observations:
		Depot A: $41,70,139$; Depot B: $40,69,138$.
		Higher confidence and tighter error targets require much larger samples.

		\subsection*{Problem description}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{Problem description}
		A central bank supervision office evaluates a fintech lender.
		One team reports from a recent compliance sample: average repayment delay was 4.7 days with sample spread 2.1 days, and a 95\% CI for the population mean delay was [4.1, 5.3].
		A second team cites long-run portfolio parameters from the full national borrower base: true mean delay $\mu=4.9$ and true standard deviation $\sigma=2.4$.
		The policy division also references a strategic threshold: ``acceptable mean delay below 5.0 days.''
		
		Explicit analysis tasks:
		1) Classify every numerical quantity above as sample statistic, population parameter, or policy benchmark and justify.
		2) Identify point estimate(s) and explain information lost versus interval form.
		3) Compare precision if a second CI [4.3, 5.1] is produced; explain conceptually what design changes could narrow intervals.
		4) State which mean values are ruled out/plausible under [4.1, 5.3] and explain ``95\% confidence'' in repeated-sampling language.
		
		\subsection*{C2}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{C2}
		Sample statistics: 4.7 (sample mean), 2.1 (sample SD), and interval endpoints 4.1 and 5.3 because they come from sampled accounts.
		Population parameters: $\mu=4.9$ and $\sigma=2.4$ because they describe the full borrower population in long-run records.
		Policy benchmark: 5.0 days is not a statistical estimate; it is a decision threshold chosen by regulators.
		
		\subsection*{C3}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{C3}
		Point estimate is $\hat{\mu}=4.7$ days.
		Information lost with only 4.7: uncertainty size, precision, and plausible alternatives.
		Interval [4.1,5.3] communicates those directly.
		Comparing [4.1,5.3] (width 1.2) vs [4.3,5.1] (width 0.8), the second is more precise.
		Conceptually, intervals narrow with larger $n$, lower confidence level, or lower variability through cleaner measurement and more homogeneous sampling windows.
		
		\subsection*{C4}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{C4}
		Under [4.1,5.3], values below 4.1 and above 5.3 are ruled out at the 95\% confidence procedure level; values inside remain plausible.
		In repeated sampling, ``95\% confidence'' means that if the same sampling-and-interval method is repeated many times, about 95\% of constructed intervals would contain the true $\mu$.
		It does not mean a 95\% probability that this already-computed fixed interval contains $\mu$.
