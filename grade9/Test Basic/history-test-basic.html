The French Revolution

Grade 9 history practice activity about the causes, events, and consequences of one of the most important political revolutions in modern history.

Historical Introduction

Contextual introduction

The French Revolution was a major period of political and social change in France that began in 1789. Before the revolution, France was ruled by an absolute monarchy, meaning the king held great political power. French society was also divided into three legal groups called estates. The First Estate was the clergy, the Second Estate was the nobility, and the Third Estate included the majority of the population, such as peasants, urban workers, and the middle class.

Key causes and background

Several factors helped cause the revolution. France had serious financial problems after years of war and royal spending. Many people in the Third Estate paid heavy taxes while privileged groups often paid less. Poor harvests led to rising bread prices, which made daily life harder for ordinary people. Enlightenment ideas also encouraged people to question absolute monarchy and demand rights, representation, and equality before the law.

Main events and developments

In 1789, King Louis XVI called the Estates-General to discuss the financial crisis. Representatives of the Third Estate declared themselves the National Assembly and promised to write a constitution. The storming of the Bastille on July 14, 1789, became a symbol of popular resistance. Later, the Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen announced principles such as liberty, equality, and national sovereignty. France eventually abolished the monarchy and became a republic. During the Reign of Terror, the revolutionary government used violence against those considered enemies of the revolution.

Main consequences

The French Revolution weakened the old social order based on inherited privilege. It promoted ideas of citizenship, legal equality, and popular sovereignty. The revolution also led to conflict, political instability, and war in Europe. Its ideals influenced later democratic movements around the world, although the revolution also showed that political change could become violent and unstable.

Historical importance

The French Revolution is important because it changed how many people understood government, rights, and citizenship. It challenged the idea that kings ruled by divine right and helped spread the belief that political authority should come from the people. Its legacy remains visible in modern ideas about constitutional government, civil rights, and equality before the law.

Short summary

The French Revolution began because of economic crisis, social inequality, political exclusion, and Enlightenment ideas. It transformed France from an absolute monarchy into a republic, introduced new ideas about rights and citizenship, and influenced political movements across the modern world.
