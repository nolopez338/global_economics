Cellular Respiration

Grade 9 Biology practice activity about how cells release usable energy from glucose to support the functions of living organisms.

Biology Introduction

Contextual introduction

Cellular respiration is the process cells use to release energy from glucose, a simple sugar. This energy is transferred to a molecule called ATP, or adenosine triphosphate. ATP stores usable chemical energy that cells can use for movement, growth, repair, active transport, and many other life processes. Cellular respiration occurs in plants, animals, fungi, protists, and many microorganisms.

Key biological concepts

The main reactants of aerobic cellular respiration are glucose and oxygen. The main products are carbon dioxide, water, and ATP. Aerobic means that the process requires oxygen. The overall word equation is glucose plus oxygen produces carbon dioxide, water, and ATP.

Main structures involved

Cellular respiration begins in the cytoplasm, the fluid-like region inside the cell. Most ATP production happens in the mitochondria. Mitochondria are organelles that have folded inner membranes. These folds increase surface area, helping the mitochondria produce ATP efficiently. Because of this role, mitochondria are often described as important energy-transforming organelles in eukaryotic cells.

Main processes and mechanisms

Cellular respiration happens in several stages. First, glycolysis breaks glucose into smaller molecules in the cytoplasm and produces a small amount of ATP. If oxygen is available, the next stages occur in the mitochondria. The Krebs cycle releases carbon dioxide and transfers energy to carrier molecules. The electron transport chain uses oxygen and produces most of the ATP. Oxygen acts as the final electron acceptor, allowing the process to continue efficiently.

Importance in living organisms

Cellular respiration is essential because cells need ATP to perform work. Muscle cells use ATP for contraction, nerve cells use ATP to maintain electrical signals, and plant cells use ATP for growth, repair, and transport of substances. During exercise, muscle cells demand more ATP, so the rate of cellular respiration increases. Without enough oxygen, some cells can use fermentation, but this produces much less ATP than aerobic respiration.

Short summary

Cellular respiration is a controlled process that releases energy from glucose and transfers it to ATP. It uses glucose and oxygen and produces carbon dioxide, water, and ATP. Glycolysis begins in the cytoplasm, while most ATP is produced in the mitochondria. This process is essential because ATP provides usable energy for the activities of living cells.
