Grade 9 History Practice: The French Revolution (1789-1799)

Historical Introduction

The French Revolution was a major political and social change in France between 1789 and 1799. It began when many people in France challenged the old system of absolute monarchy and unfair social privileges. The revolution changed not only France, but also ideas about rights and government across Europe and the world.

Key Causes / Background

  • French society was divided into three estates, and the Third Estate paid most taxes.
  • France faced severe financial debt from wars and royal spending.
  • Food shortages and high bread prices increased anger among ordinary people.
  • Enlightenment ideas promoted liberty, equality, and popular sovereignty.

Main Events / Developments

  • 1789: The Estates-General met, and the Third Estate formed the National Assembly.
  • 1789: The storming of the Bastille became a symbol of resistance to royal power.
  • 1789: The Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen announced key rights.
  • 1793-1794: The Reign of Terror used executions to defend the revolution.
  • 1799: Napoleon Bonaparte took power, ending the revolutionary government phase.

Main Consequences / Importance

  • The monarchy was weakened and eventually abolished.
  • Ideas of citizenship and equality before the law spread widely.
  • Old feudal privileges declined.
  • The revolution inspired later political movements in Europe and beyond.

Summary: The French Revolution started because of inequality, economic crisis, and new political ideas. Through dramatic events, it transformed France and reshaped modern ideas about rights, government, and citizenship.
