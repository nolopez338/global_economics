What is correlation? The Pearson linear correlation coefficient r measures the strength and direction of a linear relationship between two variables.

Range of r: from -1 (perfect negative line) to +1 (perfect positive line). Values near 0 indicate weak linear association.

Important: Correlation describes association, not causation.

Zoom controls: These change only the visible graph window. Data values are not changed by zooming.

How are r and b related, and why are they different?

The linear correlation coefficient r answers: How strong and in what direction is the linear association? The regression slope coefficient b answers: How much does predicted \(y\) change when \(x\) increases by 1 unit?

So, r is a measure of association, while b is a measure of change.

r = [nΣxy - (Σx)(Σy)] / √([nΣx² - (Σx)²][nΣy² - (Σy)²])
b = [nΣxy - (Σx)(Σy)] / [nΣx² - (Σx)²]

Both formulas have the same numerator, but the denominators are different. Therefore, r and b are generally not equal.

b = r(sy / sx)

Here, \(s_y\) is the standard deviation of \(y\), and \(s_x\) is the standard deviation of \(x\). This relationship explains why r and b may sometimes look similar but are usually different.

Interpretation: r is always between -1 and 1 and has no units. The slope coefficient b is not restricted to [-1, 1] and has units of y per unit of x.

If r = b in one example, that is a special case where \(s_y / s_x = 1\), meaning the spread of \(x\) and \(y\) is the same. This is not a general rule.

Memory aid:
• r: “How strong is the linear relationship?”
• b: “How much does predicted y change when x increases by 1?”
