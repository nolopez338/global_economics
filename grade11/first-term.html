Continuous Probability Distributions & Risk

Foundations for working with continuous random variables, their distributions, and portfolio risk for 11th grade Global Economics.

Conceptual Referents

  • Area of simple geometric figures.
  • Random variable
  • Continuous probability distribution
  • Mean and variance
  • Normal distribution
  • Exponential distribution
  • t-student distribution
  • Investment portfolios
  • Risk

Assessment Criteria

  1. Describes continuous random variable considering the concept.
  2. Interprets probabilities in continuous variables as the area under the curve.
  3. Explains how a uniform random variable works.
  4. Shows how to work with distributions that have other shapes, like triangular or circular.
  5. Uses the normal distribution and its tables to calculate probabilities in context situations.
  6. Employs the standardization of the normal variable in complex problems.
  7. Solves problems through the concept of inverse normal distribution.
  8. Differentiate the elements and characteristics that an investment portfolio may have.
  9. Establishes the risk of a portfolio based on its probability distribution.
  10. Evaluates finance context situations with normal distribution and other continuous random variables.

Working with Densities

  • Area insight: The area under a probability density function over an interval gives the probability of values in that range.
  • Uniform distribution: Constant density on an interval; probabilities correspond to rectangle areas.
  • Alternative shapes: Triangular or circular distributions model contexts with symmetry or radial outcomes.

Normal Tools

  • Standardize with Z = (X − μ) / σ to use normal tables.
  • Inverse normal helps retrieve percentiles or cutoffs from probabilities.
  • Mean and variance summarize location and spread for comparison across contexts.
Example: For X ~ Normal(60, 16), P(X > 66) = P(Z > (66−60)/4 = 1.5). Table lookup shows a right-tail area of about 0.0668.

Portfolio Perspective

  • Investment portfolios combine assets; each asset has a return distribution with its own risk.
  • Risk is tied to variance and covariance between assets, influencing the portfolio’s overall spread.
  • Probabilities and expected values guide comparisons between safer and riskier mixes.
