Context: A small retail store recorded the checkout times (in seconds) of a random sample of 10 customers to plan staffing and line management.

Recorded times:

\[42,\ 38,\ 51,\ 47,\ 44,\ 39,\ 56,\ 48,\ 41,\ 52.\]

Known: historical system data indicate the population standard deviation is \(\sigma = 6\) seconds.

Goal: estimate the population mean checkout time and quantify uncertainty with a 95% confidence interval.

Let the checkout times be \(x_1,\dots,x_{10}\). The sample mean and sample standard deviation summarize the sample, while \(\mu\) and \(\sigma\) describe the population.

\[ \bar{x} = \frac{42 + 38 + 51 + 47 + 44 + 39 + 56 + 48 + 41 + 52}{10} = \frac{458}{10} = 45.8 \text{ seconds} \]

\[ \begin{aligned} x_i - \bar{x} &= (42-45.8,\ 38-45.8,\ 51-45.8,\ 47-45.8,\ 44-45.8, \\ &\quad 39-45.8,\ 56-45.8,\ 48-45.8,\ 41-45.8,\ 52-45.8) \\ &= (-3.8,\ -7.8,\ 5.2,\ 1.2,\ -1.8,\ -6.8,\ 10.2,\ 2.2,\ -4.8,\ 6.2) \end{aligned} \]

\[ \begin{aligned} \sum (x_i-\bar{x})^2 &= (-3.8)^2 + (-7.8)^2 + 5.2^2 + 1.2^2 + (-1.8)^2 \\ &\quad + (-6.8)^2 + 10.2^2 + 2.2^2 + (-4.8)^2 + 6.2^2 \\ &= 14.44 + 60.84 + 27.04 + 1.44 + 3.24 + 46.24 + 104.04 + 4.84 + 23.04 + 38.44 \\ &= 323.60 \end{aligned} \]

\[ s^2 = \frac{\sum (x_i-\bar{x})^2}{n-1} = \frac{323.60}{10-1} = \frac{323.60}{9} = 35.96 \text{ seconds}^2 \]

\[ s = \sqrt{35.96} \approx 6.00 \text{ seconds}, \qquad \sigma = 6 \text{ seconds (known)} \]

↑

The population mean \(\mu\) is the parameter we want, while the sample mean \(\bar{x} = 45.8\) is the statistic used to estimate it.

Similarly, \(\sigma = 6\) is a population parameter from historical data, while \(s \approx 6.00\) is the sample-based estimate of spread.

Comparing variances: the population variance is \(\sigma^2 = 6^2 = 36\), while the sample variance is \(s^2 = 35.96\), so the sample variability is nearly the same as the historical population variability.

↑

The point estimate of the mean checkout time is \(\bar{x} = 45.8\) seconds. An interval estimate adds a margin of error to show plausible values for \(\mu\).

Because the sample variance is \(s^2 = 35.96\) seconds\(^2\), checkout times fluctuate enough that a single point estimate is risky, so the interval estimation is needed to capture that variability.

↑

Compute the confidence interval for the mean at the 95% confidence level using the known population variance.

\[ E = z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} = 1.96 \cdot \frac{6}{\sqrt{10}} \approx 3.72 \]

\[ \mu \in 45.8 \pm 3.72 = [42.08,\ 49.52] \text{ seconds} \]

↑

The store can be 95% confident the true mean checkout time lies in the interval. With \(s^2 = 35.96\) seconds\(^2\) (about \(s \approx 6\) seconds), managers can add a 1-standard-deviation buffer to staffing plans, such as scheduling an extra cashier when forecasted checkout averages exceed 45.8 seconds to keep wait times within budgeted targets.

Context: A café tracks the number of daily online orders for a random sample of 50 days to plan staffing and inventory levels.

Grouped data:

Order Count Frequency
510
915
1218
207

Known: historical data show the population standard deviation is \(\sigma = 4.5\) orders.

Goal: estimate the population mean number of daily orders and build a 95% confidence interval.

For grouped data, use \(\bar{x} = \frac{\sum f x}{n}\) and compute the sample spread \(s\). The population spread is \(\sigma = 4.5\).

\[ \sum f x = 10(5) + 15(9) + 18(12) + 7(20) = 50 + 135 + 216 + 140 = 541 \]

\[ \bar{x} = \frac{\sum f x}{n} = \frac{541}{50} = 10.82 \]

\[ \begin{aligned} \sum f(x-\bar{x})^2 &= 10(5-10.82)^2 + 15(9-10.82)^2 + 18(12-10.82)^2 + 7(20-10.82)^2 \\ &= 10(-5.82)^2 + 15(-1.82)^2 + 18(1.18)^2 + 7(9.18)^2 \\ &= 10(33.8724) + 15(3.3124) + 18(1.3924) + 7(84.2724) \\ &= 338.724 + 49.686 + 25.0632 + 589.9068 \\ &= 1003.38 \end{aligned} \]

\[ s^2 = \frac{\sum f(x-\bar{x})^2}{n-1} = \frac{1003.38}{50-1} = \frac{1003.38}{49} = 20.48 \text{ orders}^2 \]

\[ s = \sqrt{20.48} \approx 4.53 \text{ orders}, \qquad \sigma = 4.5 \text{ orders (known)} \]

↑

The parameter of interest is the population mean \(\mu\) for daily orders. The statistic \(\bar{x} = 10.82\) estimates it from the sample.

We also distinguish the known population spread \(\sigma = 4.5\) from the sample spread \(s \approx 4.53\).

Comparing variances: \(\sigma^2 = 4.5^2 = 20.25\) orders\(^2\) versus \(s^2 = 20.48\) orders\(^2\), so the sample variance is slightly higher than the historical population variance.

↑

The point estimate of daily orders is \(\bar{x} = 10.82\). An interval estimate adds a margin of error to show a range of plausible values for \(\mu\).

Since the sample variance is \(s^2 = 20.48\) orders\(^2\), daily demand swings enough that the café needs interval estimation to reflect that variability rather than relying on one point value.

↑

Compute the confidence interval for the mean at the 95% confidence level using the known population variance.

\[ E = z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} = 1.96 \cdot \frac{4.5}{\sqrt{50}} \approx 1.25 \]

\[ \mu \in 10.82 \pm 1.25 = [9.57,\ 12.07] \text{ orders} \]

↑

The café can be 95% confident the true mean daily order volume lies in the interval. With \(s^2 = 20.48\) orders\(^2\) (about \(s \approx 4.53\) orders), managers can hold an extra 4–5 order inventory buffer and schedule a flex staff member on days forecasted near the upper bound to reduce stockouts and overtime costs.

Context: An online boutique records weekly revenue (in thousand USD) from a grouped random sample of 40 weeks to forecast typical income.

Grouped data (described): The weekly revenue fell into these classes:

  • 45 thousand USD occurred in 8 weeks.
  • 50 thousand USD occurred in 12 weeks.
  • 55 thousand USD occurred in 14 weeks.
  • 80 thousand USD occurred in 6 weeks.

Known: financial records show the population standard deviation is \(\sigma = 10.5\) thousand USD.

Goal: estimate the population mean weekly revenue and build a 95% confidence interval.

Revenue (k USD) Frequency
458
5012
5514
806

Use the grouped mean formula and note both the sample and population variability measures.

\[ \sum f x = 8(45) + 12(50) + 14(55) + 6(80) = 360 + 600 + 770 + 480 = 2210 \]

\[ \bar{x} = \frac{\sum f x}{n} = \frac{2210}{40} = 55.25 \]

\[ \begin{aligned} \sum f(x-\bar{x})^2 &= 8(45-55.25)^2 + 12(50-55.25)^2 + 14(55-55.25)^2 + 6(80-55.25)^2 \\ &= 8(-10.25)^2 + 12(-5.25)^2 + 14(-0.25)^2 + 6(24.75)^2 \\ &= 8(105.0625) + 12(27.5625) + 14(0.0625) + 6(612.5625) \\ &= 840.50 + 330.75 + 0.875 + 3675.375 \\ &= 4847.50 \end{aligned} \]

\[ s^2 = \frac{\sum f(x-\bar{x})^2}{n-1} = \frac{4847.50}{40-1} = \frac{4847.50}{39} = 124.29 \text{ (k USD)}^2 \]

\[ s = \sqrt{124.29} \approx 11.15 \text{ k USD}, \qquad \sigma = 10.5 \text{ k USD (known)} \]

↑

The population mean \(\mu\) is the parameter of interest, estimated by the statistic \(\bar{x} = 55.25\).

Because the population variability \(\sigma = 10.5\) is known, it replaces the sample spread \(s \approx 11.15\) in the interval calculation.

Comparing variances: \(\sigma^2 = 10.5^2 = 110.25\) (k USD)\(^2\) while \(s^2 = 124.29\) (k USD)\(^2\), so the sample variance is higher than the long-run population variance.

↑

The point estimate is \(\bar{x} = 55.25\) thousand USD. The interval estimate wraps this point with a margin of error to reflect uncertainty about \(\mu\).

Given the sample variance \(s^2 = 124.29\) (k USD)\(^2\), weekly revenue fluctuates materially, so estimating a range is necessary to capture that variance-driven uncertainty.

↑

Compute the confidence interval for the mean at the 95% confidence level using the known population variance.

\[ E = z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} = 1.96 \cdot \frac{10.5}{\sqrt{40}} \approx 3.26 \]

\[ \mu \in 55.25 \pm 3.26 = [52.00,\ 58.50] \text{ thousand USD} \]

↑

The boutique can be 95% confident the true mean weekly revenue lies in the interval. With \(s^2 = 124.29\) (k USD)\(^2\) (about \(s \approx 11.15\) k USD), the owner can set a cash-reserve buffer of roughly one standard deviation and delay inventory expansion when projected revenue is near the lower bound.

Context: A logistics startup studies processing costs (USD) using a grouped random sample of 60 shipments. The data show low, mid, and extreme high-cost categories.

Grouped data (described): The processing costs fall into these classes:

  • 70 USD occurred in 18 shipments.
  • 95 USD occurred in 20 shipments.
  • 120 USD occurred in 12 shipments.
  • 250 USD occurred in 10 shipments.

Known: long-run audits show the population standard deviation is \(\sigma = 60\) USD.

Goal: estimate the population mean processing cost and build a 95% confidence interval for planning in a high-volatility environment.

Cost (USD) Frequency
7018
9520
12012
25010

\[ \sum f x = 18(70) + 20(95) + 12(120) + 10(250) = 1260 + 1900 + 1440 + 2500 = 7100 \]

\[ \bar{x} = \frac{\sum f x}{n} = \frac{7100}{60} = 118.33 \]

\[ \begin{aligned} \sum f(x-\bar{x})^2 &= 18(70-118.33)^2 + 20(95-118.33)^2 + 12(120-118.33)^2 + 10(250-118.33)^2 \\ &= 18(-48.33)^2 + 20(-23.33)^2 + 12(1.67)^2 + 10(131.67)^2 \\ &= 18(2336.11) + 20(544.44) + 12(2.79) + 10(17336.11) \\ &= 42050.00 + 10888.89 + 33.33 + 173361.11 \\ &= 226333.33 \end{aligned} \]

\[ s^2 = \frac{\sum f(x-\bar{x})^2}{n-1} = \frac{226333.33}{60-1} = \frac{226333.33}{59} = 3836.16 \text{ USD}^2 \]

\[ s = \sqrt{3836.16} \approx 61.94 \text{ USD}, \qquad \sigma = 60 \text{ USD (known)} \]

↑

The population mean \(\mu\) is the cost parameter we want, estimated by \(\bar{x} = 118.33\).

The known population spread \(\sigma = 60\) replaces the sample spread \(s \approx 61.94\) when building the interval.

Comparing variances: \(\sigma^2 = 60^2 = 3600\) USD\(^2\) while \(s^2 = 3836.16\) USD\(^2\), so the sample shows slightly higher cost volatility than the long-run benchmark.

↑

The point estimate is \(\bar{x} = 118.33\) USD. The interval estimate adds a margin of error to show plausible values for \(\mu\) given cost volatility.

Because the sample variance is \(s^2 = 3836.16\) USD\(^2\), the volatility is substantial, so interval estimation is needed to capture the uncertainty that variance introduces.

↑

Compute the confidence interval for the mean at the 95% confidence level using the known population variance.

\[ E = z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} = 1.96 \cdot \frac{60}{\sqrt{60}} \approx 15.19 \]

\[ \mu \in 118.33 \pm 15.19 = [103.14,\ 133.52] \text{ USD} \]

↑

The 95% interval indicates the average processing cost for budgeting should fall in the stated range. With \(s^2 = 3836.16\) USD\(^2\) (about \(s \approx 61.94\) USD), planners can add a one-standard-deviation contingency line in the budget and set higher reserve funds or contract surcharges when expected costs move toward the upper bound.
