Context: A small retail store recorded the checkout times (in seconds) of a random sample of 10 customers to plan staffing and line management.

Recorded times:

\[42,\ 38,\ 51,\ 47,\ 44,\ 39,\ 56,\ 48,\ 41,\ 52.\]

Known: historical system data indicate the population standard deviation is \(\sigma = 6\) seconds (population variance \(\sigma^2 = 36\) seconds\(^2\)).

Goal: estimate the population mean checkout time and quantify uncertainty with a 95% confidence interval.

↑

Let the checkout times be \(x_1,\dots,x_{10}\). The sample mean and sample standard deviation summarize the sample, while \(\mu\) and \(\sigma\) describe the population.

\[ \bar{x} = \frac{42 + 38 + 51 + 47 + 44 + 39 + 56 + 48 + 41 + 52}{10} = \frac{458}{10} = 45.8 \text{ seconds} \]

\[ \begin{aligned} x_i - \bar{x} &= (42-45.8,\ 38-45.8,\ 51-45.8,\ 47-45.8,\ 44-45.8, \\ &\quad 39-45.8,\ 56-45.8,\ 48-45.8,\ 41-45.8,\ 52-45.8) \\ &= (-3.8,\ -7.8,\ 5.2,\ 1.2,\ -1.8,\ -6.8,\ 10.2,\ 2.2,\ -4.8,\ 6.2) \end{aligned} \]

\[ \begin{aligned} \sum (x_i-\bar{x})^2 &= (-3.8)^2 + (-7.8)^2 + 5.2^2 + 1.2^2 + (-1.8)^2 \\ &\quad + (-6.8)^2 + 10.2^2 + 2.2^2 + (-4.8)^2 + 6.2^2 \\ &= 14.44 + 60.84 + 27.04 + 1.44 + 3.24 + 46.24 + 104.04 + 4.84 + 23.04 + 38.44 \\ &= 323.60 \end{aligned} \]

\[ s^2 = \frac{\sum (x_i-\bar{x})^2}{n-1} = \frac{323.60}{10-1} = \frac{323.60}{9} = 35.96 \text{ seconds}^2 \]

\[ s = \sqrt{35.96} \approx 6.00 \text{ seconds}, \qquad \sigma = 6 \text{ seconds (known)} \]

↑

The population mean \(\mu\) is the parameter we want, while the sample mean \(\bar{x} = 45.8\) is the statistic used to estimate it.

Similarly, \(\sigma = 6\) is a population parameter from historical data, while \(s \approx 6.00\) is the sample-based estimate of spread.

Comparing variances: the population variance is \(\sigma^2 = 6^2 = 36\), while the sample variance is \(s^2 = 35.96\), so the sample variability is nearly the same as the historical population variability.

↑
  • Definition (Point estimate): A point estimate is a single-number estimate of a population parameter. In practice, the sample mean \(\bar{x}\) is used as one best estimate of the population mean \(\mu\).
  • Definition (Confidence interval): A confidence interval is a range of plausible values for a parameter, reported with a confidence level (such as 95%). It combines the point estimate with a margin of error.
  • Why interval estimation is preferred: Interval estimation is preferred because it quantifies uncertainty from sampling variability. The margin of error shows precision (narrower interval = more precise estimate) and makes results easier to interpret for planning and decision-making.
  • Mini example in context (1–2 sentences, aligned to page context): For checkout time, a point estimate is \(\bar{x} = 45.8\) seconds, but a confidence interval gives a realistic band for the true mean checkout time \(\mu\) during planning.
↑

Compute the confidence interval for the mean at the 95% confidence level using the known population variance.

\[ E = z^* \cdot \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} = 1.96 \cdot \frac{6}{\sqrt{10}} \approx 3.72 \]

\[ \mu \in 45.8 \pm 3.72 = [42.08,\ 49.52] \text{ seconds} \]

↑

The store can be 95% confident the true mean checkout time lies in the interval. With \(s^2 = 35.96\) seconds\(^2\) (about \(s \approx 6\) seconds), managers can add a 1-standard-deviation buffer to staffing plans, such as scheduling an extra cashier when forecasted checkout averages exceed 45.8 seconds to keep wait times within budgeted targets.

Context: A food court tracks lunch delivery times (in minutes) for a random sample of 12 orders during the weekday rush.

Recorded times:

\[28,\ 30,\ 31,\ 29,\ 27,\ 32,\ 33,\ 30,\ 28,\ 31,\ 29,\ 34.\]

Known: operational benchmarks give the population standard deviation as \(\sigma = 2.1\) minutes (population variance \(\sigma^2 = 4.41\) minutes\(^2\)).

Goal: construct 90%, 95%, and 98% confidence intervals for the population mean delivery time using the known population variance.

↑

Let the delivery times be \(x_1,\dots,x_{12}\). Compute the sample mean and sample standard deviation.

\[ \bar{x} = \frac{28 + 30 + 31 + 29 + 27 + 32 + 33 + 30 + 28 + 31 + 29 + 34}{12} = \frac{362}{12} = 30.17 \text{ minutes} \]

\[ \sum (x_i-\bar{x})^2 = 49.67 \]

\[ s^2 = \frac{\sum (x_i-\bar{x})^2}{n-1} = \frac{49.67}{11} = 4.52 \text{ minutes}^2 \]

\[ s = \sqrt{4.52} \approx 2.12 \text{ minutes} \]

↑

The parameter of interest is the population mean delivery time \(\mu\). The statistic \(\bar{x} = 30.17\) estimates it from the sample.

The population spread is known as \(\sigma = 2.1\) minutes, while the sample standard deviation \(s \approx 2.12\) summarizes the sample.

↑
  • Definition (Point estimate): A point estimate is a single-number estimate of a population parameter. In practice, the sample mean \(\bar{x}\) is used as one best estimate of the population mean \(\mu\).
  • Definition (Confidence interval): A confidence interval is a range of plausible values for a parameter, reported with a confidence level (such as 95%). It combines the point estimate with a margin of error.
  • Why interval estimation is preferred: Interval estimation is preferred because it quantifies uncertainty from sampling variability. The margin of error shows precision (narrower interval = more precise estimate) and makes results easier to interpret for planning and decision-making.
  • Mini example in context (1–2 sentences, aligned to page context): For lunch delivery time, \(\bar{x} = 30.17\) minutes is one best estimate, while a confidence interval gives a practical range for the true mean delivery time \(\mu\).
↑

Use a \(z\)-distribution because the population variance is known.

\[ SE = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} = \frac{2.1}{\sqrt{12}} \approx 0.61 \]

\[ \text{90% CI: } E = 1.645(0.61) \approx 1.00, \quad \mu \in 30.17 \pm 1.00 = [29.17,\ 31.17] \]

\[ \text{95% CI: } E = 1.96(0.61) \approx 1.19, \quad \mu \in 30.17 \pm 1.19 = [28.98,\ 31.36] \]

\[ \text{98% CI: } E = 2.326(0.61) \approx 1.41, \quad \mu \in 30.17 \pm 1.41 = [28.76,\ 31.58] \]

↑

At 90% confidence, managers can be 90% confident the true mean delivery time is between 29.17 and 31.17 minutes; the 95% and 98% intervals widen to reflect higher confidence. Higher confidence means a wider interval, so the 98% interval provides the most conservative planning buffer for staffing and dispatch.

Context: A grocery chain draws two independent random samples of restock quantities (in boxes) from the same product category to compare typical order sizes.

Sample A (10 stores):

\[15,\ 18,\ 17,\ 19,\ 16,\ 20,\ 18,\ 17,\ 16,\ 19.\]

Sample B (12 stores):

\[14,\ 15,\ 16,\ 17,\ 15,\ 18,\ 16,\ 17,\ 14,\ 16,\ 18,\ 15.\]

Known: corporate inventory records report the population standard deviation as \(\sigma = 1.5\) boxes (population variance \(\sigma^2 = 2.25\) boxes\(^2\)).

Goal: construct 95% confidence intervals for the mean restock quantity in each sample using the known population variance and compare the intervals.

↑

Sample A:

\[ \bar{x}_A = \frac{15 + 18 + 17 + 19 + 16 + 20 + 18 + 17 + 16 + 19}{10} = \frac{175}{10} = 17.5 \]

\[ \sum (x_i-\bar{x}_A)^2 = 22.5 \]

\[ s_A^2 = \frac{22.5}{9} = 2.50, \qquad s_A = \sqrt{2.50} \approx 1.58 \]

Sample B:

\[ \bar{x}_B = \frac{14 + 15 + 16 + 17 + 15 + 18 + 16 + 17 + 14 + 16 + 18 + 15}{12} = \frac{191}{12} = 15.92 \]

\[ \sum (x_i-\bar{x}_B)^2 = 20.92 \]

\[ s_B^2 = \frac{20.92}{11} = 1.90, \qquad s_B = \sqrt{1.90} \approx 1.38 \]

↑

Both samples estimate the same population mean restock quantity \(\mu\), using \(\bar{x}_A = 17.5\) and \(\bar{x}_B = 15.92\).

The known population spread is \(\sigma = 1.5\) boxes, while the samples show \(s_A \approx 1.58\) and \(s_B \approx 1.38\).

↑
  • Definition (Point estimate): A point estimate is a single-number estimate of a population parameter. In practice, the sample mean \(\bar{x}\) is used as one best estimate of the population mean \(\mu\).
  • Definition (Confidence interval): A confidence interval is a range of plausible values for a parameter, reported with a confidence level (such as 95%). It combines the point estimate with a margin of error.
  • Why interval estimation is preferred: Interval estimation is preferred because it quantifies uncertainty from sampling variability. The margin of error shows precision (narrower interval = more precise estimate) and makes results easier to interpret for planning and decision-making.
  • Mini example in context (1–2 sentences, aligned to page context): For restock quantities, point estimates \(\bar{x}_A = 17.5\) and \(\bar{x}_B = 15.92\) are useful, but intervals are better for comparing likely population means because both samples vary.
↑

Use a \(z\)-distribution because the population variance is known.

\[ SE_A = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} = \frac{1.5}{\sqrt{10}} \approx 0.47, \quad E_A = 1.96(0.47) \approx 0.93 \]

\[ \mu \in 17.5 \pm 0.93 = [16.57,\ 18.43] \text{ boxes} \]

\[ SE_B = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} = \frac{1.5}{\sqrt{12}} \approx 0.43, \quad E_B = 1.96(0.43) \approx 0.85 \]

\[ \mu \in 15.92 \pm 0.85 = [15.07,\ 16.77] \text{ boxes} \]

↑

The intervals overlap slightly (about 16.57 to 16.77), suggesting the average restock quantity could be similar across the two samples, but Sample A’s interval sits higher overall. Managers might treat Sample A stores as higher-demand locations while monitoring overlap before changing ordering policy.

Context: A café tracks the number of daily online orders for a random sample of 50 days to plan staffing and inventory levels.

Grouped data (described): The order counts were recorded as follows:

  • 5 orders occurred on 10 days.
  • 9 orders occurred on 15 days.
  • 12 orders occurred on 18 days.
  • 20 orders occurred on 7 days.

Known: historical data show the population standard deviation is \(\sigma = 4.5\) orders.

Goal: estimate the population mean number of daily orders and build a 95% confidence interval.

↑

For grouped data, use \(\bar{x} = \frac{\sum f x}{n}\) and compute the sample spread \(s\). The population spread is \(\sigma = 4.5\).

\[ \sum f x = 10(5) + 15(9) + 18(12) + 7(20) = 50 + 135 + 216 + 140 = 541 \]

\[ \bar{x} = \frac{\sum f x}{n} = \frac{541}{50} = 10.82 \]

\[ \begin{aligned} \sum f(x-\bar{x})^2 &= 10(5-10.82)^2 + 15(9-10.82)^2 + 18(12-10.82)^2 + 7(20-10.82)^2 \\ &= 10(-5.82)^2 + 15(-1.82)^2 + 18(1.18)^2 + 7(9.18)^2 \\ &= 10(33.8724) + 15(3.3124) + 18(1.3924) + 7(84.2724) \\ &= 338.724 + 49.686 + 25.0632 + 589.9068 \\ &= 1003.38 \end{aligned} \]

\[ s^2 = \frac{\sum f(x-\bar{x})^2}{n-1} = \frac{1003.38}{50-1} = \frac{1003.38}{49} = 20.48 \text{ orders}^2 \]

\[ s = \sqrt{20.48} \approx 4.53 \text{ orders}, \qquad \sigma = 4.5 \text{ orders (known)} \]

↑

The parameter of interest is the population mean \(\mu\) for daily orders. The statistic \(\bar{x} = 10.82\) estimates it from the sample.

We also distinguish the known population spread \(\sigma = 4.5\) from the sample spread \(s \approx 4.53\).

Comparing variances: \(\sigma^2 = 4.5^2 = 20.25\) orders\(^2\) versus \(s^2 = 20.48\) orders\(^2\), so the sample variance is slightly higher than the historical population variance.

↑
  • Definition (Point estimate): A point estimate is a single-number estimate of a population parameter. In practice, the sample mean \(\bar{x}\) is used as one best estimate of the population mean \(\mu\).
  • Definition (Confidence interval): A confidence interval is a range of plausible values for a parameter, reported with a confidence level (such as 95%). It combines the point estimate with a margin of error.
  • Why interval estimation is preferred: Interval estimation is preferred because it quantifies uncertainty from sampling variability. The margin of error shows precision (narrower interval = more precise estimate) and makes results easier to interpret for planning and decision-making.
  • Mini example in context (1–2 sentences, aligned to page context): For daily café orders, the point estimate \(\bar{x} = 10.82\) is a starting value, while a confidence interval shows the likely range for the true mean number of orders \(\mu\).
↑

Compute the confidence interval for the mean at the 95% confidence level using the known population variance.

\[ E = z^* \cdot \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} = 1.96 \cdot \frac{4.5}{\sqrt{50}} \approx 1.25 \]

\[ \mu \in 10.82 \pm 1.25 = [9.57,\ 12.07] \text{ orders} \]

↑

The café can be 95% confident the true mean daily order volume lies in the interval. With \(s^2 = 20.48\) orders\(^2\) (about \(s \approx 4.53\) orders), managers can hold an extra 4–5 order inventory buffer and schedule a flex staff member on days forecasted near the upper bound to reduce stockouts and overtime costs.

Grouped data (described): The weekly revenue fell into these classes:

  • 45 thousand USD occurred in 8 weeks.
  • 50 thousand USD occurred in 12 weeks.
  • 55 thousand USD occurred in 14 weeks.
  • 80 thousand USD occurred in 6 weeks.

Known: financial records show the population standard deviation is \(\sigma = 10.5\) thousand USD.

Goal: estimate the population mean weekly revenue and construct 90%, 95%, and 98% confidence intervals.

↑
Revenue (k USD) Frequency
458
5012

806

Use the grouped mean formula and note both the sample and population variability measures.

\[ \sum f x = 8(45) + 12(50) + 14(55) + 6(80) = 360 + 600 + 770 + 480 = 2210 \]

\[ \bar{x} = \frac{\sum f x}{n} = \frac{2210}{40} = 55.25 \]

\[ \begin{aligned} \sum f(x-\bar{x})^2 &= 8(45-55.25)^2 + 12(50-55.25)^2 + 14(55-55.25)^2 + 6(80-55.25)^2 \\ &= 8(-10.25)^2 + 12(-5.25)^2 + 14(-0.25)^2 + 6(24.75)^2 \\ &= 8(105.0625) + 12(27.5625) + 14(0.0625) + 6(612.5625) \\ &= 840.50 + 330.75 + 0.875 + 3675.375 \\ &= 4847.50 \end{aligned} \]

\[ s^2 = \frac{\sum f(x-\bar{x})^2}{n-1} = \frac{4847.50}{40-1} = \frac{4847.50}{39} = 124.29 \text{ (k USD)}^2 \]

\[ s = \sqrt{124.29} \approx 11.15 \text{ k USD}, \qquad \sigma = 10.5 \text{ k USD (known)} \]

↑

The population mean \(\mu\) is the parameter of interest, estimated by the statistic \(\bar{x} = 55.25\).

Because the population variability \(\sigma = 10.5\) is known, it replaces the sample spread \(s \approx 11.15\) in the interval calculation.

Comparing variances: \(\sigma^2 = 10.5^2 = 110.25\) (k USD)\(^2\) while \(s^2 = 124.29\) (k USD)\(^2\), so the sample variance is higher than the long-run population variance.

↑
  • Definition (Point estimate): A point estimate is a single-number estimate of a population parameter. In practice, the sample mean \(\bar{x}\) is used as one best estimate of the population mean \(\mu\).
  • Definition (Confidence interval): A confidence interval is a range of plausible values for a parameter, reported with a confidence level (such as 95%). It combines the point estimate with a margin of error.
  • Why interval estimation is preferred: Interval estimation is preferred because it quantifies uncertainty from sampling variability. The margin of error shows precision (narrower interval = more precise estimate) and makes results easier to interpret for planning and decision-making.
  • Mini example in context (1–2 sentences, aligned to page context): For weekly revenue, \(\bar{x} = 55.25\) thousand USD is the point estimate, and a confidence interval gives a more decision-ready range for the true mean revenue \(\mu\).
↑

Compute the confidence interval for the mean at each confidence level using the known population variance.

\[ SE = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} = \frac{10.5}{\sqrt{40}} \approx 1.66 \]

\[ \text{90% CI: } E = 1.645(1.66) \approx 2.73, \quad \mu \in 55.25 \pm 2.73 = [52.52,\ 57.98] \]

\[ \text{95% CI: } E = 1.96(1.66) \approx 3.25, \quad \mu \in 55.25 \pm 3.25 = [52.00,\ 58.50] \]

\[ \text{98% CI: } E = 2.326(1.66) \approx 3.86, \quad \mu \in 55.25 \pm 3.86 = [51.39,\ 59.11] \]

↑

The 90% interval is the narrowest and the 98% interval is widest, reflecting the tradeoff between confidence and precision. For cash-flow planning, the boutique can choose a confidence level that matches its risk tolerance, using the 98% interval for conservative budgeting or the 90% interval for tighter short-term planning.
