A university career center uses a mobile app to support students and recent graduates with internship alerts, interview reminders, and short productivity check-ins. The center is reviewing whether users are staying engaged enough for the app to justify next semester's funding, especially because many users are balancing classes, part-time work, and job applications. The current benchmark says that 62% of active users complete the daily check-in. A class project reviews 200 active users and finds that 112 completed the daily check-in.

Questions.

  1. Using a significance level of 5%, write the null and alternative hypotheses for the case where the true daily check-in proportion is lower than 62%.
  2. Using a significance level of 5%, write the null and alternative hypotheses for the case where the true daily check-in proportion is higher than 62%.
  3. Using a significance level of 5%, write the null and alternative hypotheses for the case where the true daily check-in proportion is different from 62%.
↑

Let \(p\) be the true population proportion of active users who check in daily. Let \(p_0\) be the benchmark proportion from the app report, so \(p_0=62\%\). Let \(n\) be the sample size, with \(n=200\). Let \(x\) be the number of daily check-ins observed in the sample, with \(x=112\). Let \(\alpha\) be the significance level, with \(\alpha=5\%\). Parts (a)–(c) use the same parameter \(p\) and the same benchmark \(p_0\), but each part uses a different form of the alternative claim.

↑
  • (a) \(H_0\colon p=0.62,\; H_a\colon p<0.62\), \(\alpha=0.05\).
  • (b) \(H_0\colon p=0.62,\; H_a\colon p>0.62\), \(\alpha=0.05\).
  • (c) \(H_0\colon p=0.62,\; H_a\colon p\ne 0.62\), \(\alpha=0.05\).
↑
  • (a) Left-tailed test because the alternative claims the parameter is lower than the benchmark.
  • (b) Right-tailed test because the alternative claims the parameter is higher than the benchmark.
  • (c) Two-tailed test because the alternative claims the parameter is different from the benchmark.

A university academic support office is evaluating whether students are spending enough time studying each evening during a demanding midterm period. Many of them are combining coursework with internships, long commutes, or part-time jobs, so the office wants to know whether the reported average still reflects current study habits. The report claims that students study an average of 75 minutes per evening. A sample of 36 students has an average of 79 minutes per evening. Assume the population standard deviation is known to be 12 minutes.

Questions.

  1. Using a significance level of 1%, write the null and alternative hypotheses for the case where the true mean study time is lower than 75 minutes.
  2. Using a significance level of 1%, write the null and alternative hypotheses for the case where the true mean study time is higher than 75 minutes.
  3. Using a significance level of 1%, write the null and alternative hypotheses for the case where the true mean study time is different from 75 minutes.
↑

Let \(\mu\) be the true population mean study time per evening for students. Let \(\mu_0\) be the benchmark mean from the report, so \(\mu_0=75\). Let \(n\) be the sample size, with \(n=36\). Let \(\bar{x}\) be the sample mean study time, with \(\bar{x}=79\). Let \(\sigma\) be the known population standard deviation, with \(\sigma=12\). Let \(\alpha\) be the significance level, with \(\alpha=1\%\). Parts (a)–(c) use the same parameter \(\mu\) and the same benchmark \(\mu_0\), but each part uses a different form of the alternative claim.

↑
  • (a) \(H_0\colon \mu=75,\; H_a\colon \mu<75\), \(\alpha=0.01\).
  • (b) \(H_0\colon \mu=75,\; H_a\colon \mu>75\), \(\alpha=0.01\).
  • (c) \(H_0\colon \mu=75,\; H_a\colon \mu\ne 75\), \(\alpha=0.01\).
↑
  • (a) Left-tailed test because the alternative claims the parameter is lower than the benchmark.
  • (b) Right-tailed test because the alternative claims the parameter is higher than the benchmark.
  • (c) Two-tailed test because the alternative claims the parameter is different from the benchmark.
