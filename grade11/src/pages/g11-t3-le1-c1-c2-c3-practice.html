A benchmark claim may refer either to a population proportion or to a population mean.

For a proportion setting, the claim states that specific percentage (for example, 62%) of the population has the characteristic of interest. A certain number of samples (for example, 200) observations gives number of observed successes (for example, 112). Use a significance level (for example, 5%).

For a mean setting, the claim states that the population mean is a benchmark mean value (for example, 75). A certain number of samples (for example, 36) observations gives a sample mean value (for example, 79). If needed, the population standard deviation is given as a known value (for example, 12). Use a significance level (for example, 1%).

Questions/Tasks.

  1. (C1) Identify whether the setting is a proportion claim or a mean claim, and map the benchmark value, sample information, and significance level to the correct symbols.
  2. (C2) Write the null and alternative hypotheses for the three directions of the claim.
    • (a) Write the null hypothesis as "parameter equals the benchmark" and the alternative as "parameter is lower than the benchmark".
    • (b) Write the null hypothesis as "parameter equals the benchmark" and the alternative as "parameter is higher than the benchmark".
    • (c) Write the null hypothesis as "parameter equals the benchmark" and the alternative as "parameter is different from the benchmark".
  3. (C3) Classify the tail type for each direction of the claim and justify it in words.
    • (a) Classify the test for the claim "lower".
    • (b) Classify the test for the claim "higher".
    • (c) Classify the test for the claim "different".
↑
  • Identify whether the problem is about a population proportion or a population mean.
  • For a proportion problem:
    • state that \(p\) is the true population proportion,
    • state that \(p_0\) is the benchmark value (for example, 62%),
    • define \(n\) as the sample size (for example, 200),
    • define \(x\) as the observed count (for example, 112),
    • define \(\alpha\) as the significance level (for example, 5%).
  • For a mean problem:
    • state that \(\mu\) is the true population mean,
    • state that \(\mu_0\) is the benchmark value (for example, 75),
    • define \(n\) as the sample size (for example, 36),
    • define \(\bar{x}\) as the sample mean (for example, 79),
    • define \(\sigma\) as the known population standard deviation when it is provided (for example, 12),
    • define \(\alpha\) as the significance level (for example, 1%).
  • Match each symbol correctly to the numerical values given in the statement.
  • Parts "lower", "higher", and "different" use the same parameter and benchmark, but different alternative claims.
↑
  • Write the hypotheses correctly for each part (a), (b), and (c), and include \(\alpha\) in each case.
  • For a proportion problem, use \(p\) and \(p_0\):
    • (a) \(H_0\colon p=p_0\), \(H_a\colon p<p_0\), with the stated significance level.
    • (b) \(H_0\colon p=p_0\), \(H_a\colon p>p_0\), with the stated significance level.
    • (c) \(H_0\colon p=p_0\), \(H_a\colon p \neq p_0\), with the stated significance level.
  • For a mean problem, use \(\mu\) and \(\mu_0\):
    • (a) \(H_0\colon \mu=\mu_0\), \(H_a\colon \mu<\mu_0\), with the stated significance level.
    • (b) \(H_0\colon \mu=\mu_0\), \(H_a\colon \mu>\mu_0\), with the stated significance level.
    • (c) \(H_0\colon \mu=\mu_0\), \(H_a\colon \mu \neq \mu_0\), with the stated significance level.
  • In every case, keep the null hypothesis as "parameter equals the benchmark" and match the inequality direction to lower, higher, or different.
↑
  • Classify and justify each part directly from the wording of the alternative claim:
    • (a) left-tailed test, because the claim says "lower".
    • (b) right-tailed test, because the claim says "higher".
    • (c) two-tailed test, because the claim says "different".
  • This mapping is the same for both proportion and mean tests.
  • Do not include calculations.

A university career center uses a mobile app to support students and recent graduates with internship alerts, interview reminders, and short productivity check-ins. The center is reviewing whether users are staying engaged enough for the app to justify next semester's funding, especially because many users are balancing classes, part-time work, and job applications. The current benchmark says that 62% of active users complete the daily check-in. A class project reviews 200 active users and finds that 112 completed the daily check-in. They will use a 5% significance level for this check.

Questions/Tasks.

  1. (C1) Identify whether the claim is about a population proportion or a population mean, and match the parameter, the benchmark value, the sample size, and the observed sample result for the daily check-in proportion to the symbols used in the solution.
  2. (C2) Write hypotheses for each case using the stated significance level.
    • (a) Using a significance level of 5%, write the null and alternative hypotheses for the case where the true daily check-in proportion is lower than 62%.
    • (b) Using a significance level of 5%, write the null and alternative hypotheses for the case where the true daily check-in proportion is higher than 62%.
    • (c) Using a significance level of 5%, write the null and alternative hypotheses for the case where the true daily check-in proportion is different from 62%.
  3. (C3) For each case, classify the test as left-tailed, right-tailed, or two-tailed and justify using the wording of the alternative claim.
↑
  • This is a population proportion setting.
  • Let \(p\) be the true population proportion of active users who check in daily.
  • Let \(p_0\) be the benchmark proportion, with \(p_0=62\%\).
  • Let \(n\) be the sample size, with \(n=200\).
  • Let \(x\) be the observed number of daily check-ins, with \(x=112\).
  • Let \(\alpha\) be the significance level, with \(\alpha=5\%\).
  • Parts “lower”, “higher”, and “different” use the same parameter \(p\) and the same benchmark \(p_0\), but different alternative claims.
↑
  • (a) \(H_0\colon p=0.62,\; H_a\colon p<0.62\), \(\alpha=0.05\).
  • (b) \(H_0\colon p=0.62,\; H_a\colon p>0.62\), \(\alpha=0.05\).
  • (c) \(H_0\colon p=0.62,\; H_a\colon p\ne 0.62\), \(\alpha=0.05\).
↑
  • (a) Left-tailed test because the alternative claims the parameter is lower than the benchmark.
  • (b) Right-tailed test because the alternative claims the parameter is higher than the benchmark.
  • (c) Two-tailed test because the alternative claims the parameter is different from the benchmark.

A university academic support office is evaluating whether students are spending enough time studying each evening during a demanding midterm period. Many of them are combining coursework with internships, long commutes, or part-time jobs, so the office wants to know whether the reported average still reflects current study habits. The report claims that students study an average of 75 minutes per evening. A sample of 36 students has an average of 79 minutes per evening. Assume the population standard deviation is known to be 12 minutes. They will use a 10% significance level for this check.

Questions/Tasks.

  1. (C1) Identify whether the claim is about a population mean and map symbols.
  2. (C2) Write hypotheses for lower, higher, and different from 75 at 10%.
  3. (C3) Classify each as left-, right-, or two-tailed with justification.
↑
  • This is a population mean setting.
  • Let \(\mu\) be the true population mean study time per evening for students.
  • Let \(\mu_0\) be the benchmark mean, with \(\mu_0=75\).
  • Let \(n\) be the sample size, with \(n=36\).
  • Let \(\bar{x}\) be the sample mean study time, with \(\bar{x}=79\).
  • Let \(\sigma\) be the known population standard deviation, with \(\sigma=12\).
  • Let \(\alpha\) be the significance level, with \(\alpha=10\%\).
  • Parts "lower", "higher", and "different" use the same parameter \(\mu\) and the same benchmark \(\mu_0\), but different alternative claims.
↑
  • (a) \(H_0\colon \mu=75,\; H_a\colon \mu<75\), \(\alpha=0.10\).
  • (b) \(H_0\colon \mu=75,\; H_a\colon \mu>75\), \(\alpha=0.10\).
  • (c) \(H_0\colon \mu=75,\; H_a\colon \mu\ne 75\), \(\alpha=0.10\).

A department is reviewing a career-focused course taken by students preparing for internships, graduate applications, or entry-level jobs. Because quiz performance is used to decide whether tutoring hours and review sessions should be expanded, the department wants to know whether the typical score is matching its target during a busy part of the term. The target is an average quiz score of 68 points. A sample of 25 students has an average score of 71 points. Assume the population standard deviation is known to be 9 points. Because tutoring hours and review sessions may be expanded under stricter or more flexible policies, the department will consider three significance levels for this check: 1%, 2%, and 5%.

Questions/Tasks.

  1. (C1) Identify whether the claim is about a population proportion or a population mean, and match the parameter, the benchmark value of 68 points, the sample size, the observed sample result, and the three stated significance levels to the symbols used in the solution.
  2. (C2) For each stated significance level, write the null and alternative hypotheses for the case where the true mean quiz score is different from 68 points.
  3. (C3) Classify the test and justify it in words for the case where the true mean quiz score is different from 68 points, and state whether the tail type changes across the stated significance levels.
↑
  • This is a population mean setting.
  • Let \(\mu\) be the true population mean quiz score for all students in the department.
  • Let \(\mu_0\) be the benchmark target mean, with \(\mu_0=68\).
  • Let \(n\) be the sample size, with \(n=25\).
  • Let \(\bar{x}\) be the sample mean quiz score, with \(\bar{x}=71\).
  • Let \(\sigma\) be the known population standard deviation, with \(\sigma=9\).
  • Let \(\alpha\) represent the significance level, and here \(\alpha\) will be considered at 1%, 2%, and 5%.
  • The alternative claim in this problem is that the mean is different from the benchmark.
↑

At \(\alpha=0.01\): \(H_0\colon \mu=68,\; H_a\colon \mu\ne 68\).

At \(\alpha=0.02\): \(H_0\colon \mu=68,\; H_a\colon \mu\ne 68\).

At \(\alpha=0.05\): \(H_0\colon \mu=68,\; H_a\colon \mu\ne 68\).

↑

Two-tailed test because the alternative claims the parameter is different from the benchmark. The tail type does not change across the three significance levels.
