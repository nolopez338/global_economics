Context: A small retail store recorded the checkout times (in seconds) of a random sample of 10 customers to plan staffing and line management.

Recorded times:

\[42,\ 38,\ 51,\ 47,\ 44,\ 39,\ 56,\ 48,\ 41,\ 52.\]

Goal: estimate the population mean checkout time and quantify uncertainty with a 95% confidence interval.

↑

Let the checkout times be \(x_1,\dots,x_{10}\). The sample mean is the point estimate of the population mean.

\[ \bar{x} = \frac{42 + 38 + 51 + 47 + 44 + 39 + 56 + 48 + 41 + 52}{10} = \frac{458}{10} = 45.8 \text{ seconds} \]

↑

Compute the sample variance and standard deviation to quantify variability:

\[ s^2 = \frac{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2}{n - 1}, \qquad s \approx 6.23 \text{ seconds} \]

This spread will drive the standard error in the interval calculation.

↑

Because \(n = 10\) and the population variance is unknown, use a t distribution with \(df = 9\).

\[ SE = \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}} = \frac{6.23}{\sqrt{10}} \approx 1.97 \]

\[ \mu \in \bar{x} \pm t_{0.025,9} \cdot SE = 45.8 \pm 2.262(1.97) \]

\[ \mu \in [41.34,\ 50.26] \text{ seconds} \]

↑

The store can be 95% confident that the true average checkout time falls between about 41 and 50 seconds. This turns the sample evidence into a realistic planning range for customer flow.

↑

Use the upper bound (about 50 seconds) to stress-test staffing schedules during peak hours. If the queue targets depend on a faster service time, the team should plan for at least one extra cashier to keep waits within acceptable limits.

Context: A café tracks the number of daily online orders for a random sample of 50 days to plan staffing and inventory levels.

Grouped data:

Order Count Frequency Total Contribution
51010(5)
91515(9)
121818(12)
2077(20)

Goal: estimate the population mean number of daily orders and build a 95% confidence interval.

↑

Use \(\bar{x} = \frac{\sum f x}{n}\) for grouped data.

\[ \sum f x = 10(5) + 15(9) + 18(12) + 7(20) = 541 \]

\[ \bar{x} = \frac{541}{50} = 10.82 \]

↑

Compute grouped variance with \(s^2 = \frac{1}{n-1}\sum f(x-\bar{x})^2\):

\[ \sum f(x-\bar{x})^2 = 10(5 - 10.82)^2 + 15(9 - 10.82)^2 + 18(12 - 10.82)^2 + 7(20 - 10.82)^2 \]

\[ \sum f(x-\bar{x})^2 \approx 1017.4,\quad s^2 = \frac{1017.4}{49} = 20.77,\quad s = 4.56 \]

↑

With \(n = 50\) and unknown population variance, use the t distribution with \(df = 49\).

\[ SE = \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}} = \frac{4.56}{\sqrt{50}} \approx 0.65 \]

\[ \mu \in \bar{x} \pm t_{0.025,49}\cdot SE = 10.82 \pm 2.009(0.65) \]

\[ \mu \in [9.51,\ 12.13] \]

↑

The café can be 95% confident that the true average daily online order volume is between about 9.5 and 12.1 orders. This range captures typical demand rather than unusually quiet or busy days.

↑

Plan staffing and ingredient purchases around the upper bound (about 12 orders) to reduce stockouts. If costs are sensitive, use the midpoint (around 11 orders) as the baseline and keep a small safety buffer.

Context: An online boutique records weekly revenue (in thousand USD) from a grouped random sample of 40 weeks to forecast typical income.

Grouped data:

Revenue (k USD) Frequency Contribution
458360
5012600
5514770
806480

Goal: estimate the population mean weekly revenue and build a 95% confidence interval.

↑

Use the grouped mean formula:

\[ \bar{x} = \frac{\sum f x}{n} = \frac{360 + 600 + 770 + 480}{40} = 55.25 \]

↑

Compute grouped variance:

\[ \sum f(x-\bar{x})^2 = 4852.3,\quad s^2 = \frac{4852.3}{39} = 124.43,\quad s = 11.15 \]

↑

With unknown population variance, use a t distribution with \(df = 39\).

\[ SE = \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}} = \frac{11.15}{\sqrt{40}} = 1.76 \]

\[ \mu \in 55.25 \pm t_{0.025,39}(1.76) = 55.25 \pm 2.023(1.76) \]

\[ \mu \in [51.69,\ 58.81] \text{ (thousand USD)} \]

↑

The boutique can be 95% confident the typical weekly revenue lies between about 51.7k and 58.8k USD. This range frames expected cash inflows for budgeting.

↑

Use the lower bound (about 51.7k) for conservative cash-flow planning and the upper bound (about 58.8k) when setting stretch targets. This prevents overcommitting to expenses when weekly sales are volatile.

Context: A logistics startup studies processing costs (USD) using a grouped random sample of 60 shipments. The data show low, mid, and extreme high-cost categories.

Cost (USD) Frequency Contribution
70181260
95201900
120121440
250102500

Goal: estimate the population mean processing cost and build a 95% confidence interval for planning in a high-volatility environment.

↑

\[ \bar{x} = \frac{1260 + 1900 + 1440 + 2500}{60} = \frac{7100}{60} = 118.33 \]

↑

Compute grouped variance:

\[ \sum f(x-\bar{x})^2 = 226461.2,\quad s^2 = \frac{226461.2}{59} = 3830.69,\quad s = 61.90 \]

↑

Even with \(n = 60\), the heavy-tailed cost pattern warrants a t distribution with \(df = 59\).

\[ SE = \frac{61.90}{\sqrt{60}} = 7.98 \]

\[ \mu \in 118.33 \pm t_{0.025,59}(7.98) = 118.33 \pm 2.000(7.98) \]

\[ \mu \in [102.37,\ 134.29] \]

↑

The 95% interval suggests the typical processing cost is between about 102 and 134 USD. The wide range reflects the impact of extreme high-cost shipments on the overall average.

↑

Budget using the upper bound (about 134 USD) as a contingency rate for high-volatility lanes. For routine shipments, use the midpoint while keeping reserve funds to absorb spikes in processing costs.
