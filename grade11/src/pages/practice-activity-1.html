Context: A small retail store recorded the checkout times (in seconds) of a random sample of 10 customers to plan staffing and line management.

Recorded times:

\[42,\ 38,\ 51,\ 47,\ 44,\ 39,\ 56,\ 48,\ 41,\ 52.\]

Known: historical system data indicate the population standard deviation is \(\sigma = 6\) seconds.

Goal: estimate the population mean checkout time and quantify uncertainty with a 95% confidence interval.

Let the checkout times be \(x_1,\dots,x_{10}\). The sample mean and sample standard deviation summarize the sample, while \(\mu\) and \(\sigma\) describe the population.

\[ \bar{x} = \frac{42 + 38 + 51 + 47 + 44 + 39 + 56 + 48 + 41 + 52}{10} = \frac{458}{10} = 45.8 \text{ seconds} \]

\[ s \approx 6.23 \text{ seconds}, \qquad \sigma = 6 \text{ seconds (known)} \]

↑

The population mean \(\mu\) is the parameter we want, while the sample mean \(\bar{x} = 45.8\) is the statistic used to estimate it.

Similarly, \(\sigma = 6\) is a population parameter from historical data, while \(s \approx 6.23\) is the sample-based estimate of spread.

↑

The point estimate of the mean checkout time is \(\bar{x} = 45.8\) seconds. An interval estimate adds a margin of error to show plausible values for \(\mu\).

↑

With known \(\sigma\), use the z-interval:

\[ SE = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} = \frac{6}{\sqrt{10}} \approx 1.90 \]

\[ \mu \in \bar{x} \pm z_{\alpha/2} \cdot SE = 45.8 \pm 1.96(1.90) \]

\[ \mu \in [42.08,\ 49.52] \text{ seconds} \]

↑

The store can be 95% confident the true mean checkout time lies in the interval. That range translates into expected service speed for staffing budgets and customer-wait targets.

Context: A café tracks the number of daily online orders for a random sample of 50 days to plan staffing and inventory levels.

Grouped data:

Order Count Frequency Total Contribution
51010(5)
91515(9)
121818(12)
2077(20)

Known: historical data show the population standard deviation is \(\sigma = 4.5\) orders.

Goal: estimate the population mean number of daily orders and build a 95% confidence interval.

For grouped data, use \(\bar{x} = \frac{\sum f x}{n}\) and compute the sample spread \(s\). The population spread is \(\sigma = 4.5\).

\[ \sum f x = 10(5) + 15(9) + 18(12) + 7(20) = 541 \]

\[ \bar{x} = \frac{541}{50} = 10.82 \]

\[ s \approx 4.56 \text{ orders}, \qquad \sigma = 4.5 \text{ orders (known)} \]

↑

The parameter of interest is the population mean \(\mu\) for daily orders. The statistic \(\bar{x} = 10.82\) estimates it from the sample.

We also distinguish the known population spread \(\sigma = 4.5\) from the sample spread \(s \approx 4.56\).

↑

The point estimate of daily orders is \(\bar{x} = 10.82\). An interval estimate adds a margin of error to show a range of plausible values for \(\mu\).

↑

With known \(\sigma\), use the z-interval:

\[ SE = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} = \frac{4.5}{\sqrt{50}} \approx 0.64 \]

\[ \mu \in \bar{x} \pm z_{\alpha/2} \cdot SE = 10.82 \pm 1.96(0.64) \]

\[ \mu \in [9.57,\ 12.07] \text{ orders} \]

↑

The café can be 95% confident the true mean daily order volume lies in the interval. This range informs staffing costs and inventory budgets for typical demand.

Context: An online boutique records weekly revenue (in thousand USD) from a grouped random sample of 40 weeks to forecast typical income.

Grouped data:

Revenue (k USD) Frequency Contribution
458360
5012600
5514770
806480

Known: financial records show the population standard deviation is \(\sigma = 10.5\) thousand USD.

Goal: estimate the population mean weekly revenue and build a 95% confidence interval.

Use the grouped mean formula and note both the sample and population variability measures.

\[ \bar{x} = \frac{\sum f x}{n} = \frac{360 + 600 + 770 + 480}{40} = 55.25 \]

\[ s \approx 11.15 \text{ k USD}, \qquad \sigma = 10.5 \text{ k USD (known)} \]

↑

The population mean \(\mu\) is the parameter of interest, estimated by the statistic \(\bar{x} = 55.25\).

Because the population variability \(\sigma = 10.5\) is known, it replaces the sample spread \(s \approx 11.15\) in the interval calculation.

↑

The point estimate is \(\bar{x} = 55.25\) thousand USD. The interval estimate wraps this point with a margin of error to reflect uncertainty about \(\mu\).

↑

With known \(\sigma\), use the z-interval:

\[ SE = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} = \frac{10.5}{\sqrt{40}} \approx 1.66 \]

\[ \mu \in \bar{x} \pm z_{\alpha/2} \cdot SE = 55.25 \pm 1.96(1.66) \]

\[ \mu \in [52.00,\ 58.50] \text{ thousand USD} \]

↑

The boutique can be 95% confident the true mean weekly revenue lies in the interval. That range guides budgeting, loan repayment schedules, and inventory investment targets.

Context: A logistics startup studies processing costs (USD) using a grouped random sample of 60 shipments. The data show low, mid, and extreme high-cost categories.

Cost (USD) Frequency Contribution
70181260
95201900
120121440
250102500

Known: long-run audits show the population standard deviation is \(\sigma = 60\) USD.

Goal: estimate the population mean processing cost and build a 95% confidence interval for planning in a high-volatility environment.

\[ \bar{x} = \frac{1260 + 1900 + 1440 + 2500}{60} = \frac{7100}{60} = 118.33 \]

\[ s \approx 61.90 \text{ USD}, \qquad \sigma = 60 \text{ USD (known)} \]

↑

The population mean \(\mu\) is the cost parameter we want, estimated by \(\bar{x} = 118.33\).

The known population spread \(\sigma = 60\) replaces the sample spread \(s \approx 61.90\) when building the interval.

↑

The point estimate is \(\bar{x} = 118.33\) USD. The interval estimate adds a margin of error to show plausible values for \(\mu\) given cost volatility.

↑

With known \(\sigma\), use the z-interval:

\[ SE = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} = \frac{60}{\sqrt{60}} \approx 7.75 \]

\[ \mu \in \bar{x} \pm z_{\alpha/2} \cdot SE = 118.33 \pm 1.96(7.75) \]

\[ \mu \in [103.14,\ 133.52] \text{ USD} \]

↑

The 95% interval indicates the average processing cost for budgeting should fall in the stated range, supporting pricing, cash reserves, and risk premiums in logistics contracts.
