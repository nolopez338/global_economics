Context: A global retail chain sources products from multiple countries, and weekend demand shifts when shipping costs or exchange rates change. The operations manager wants to staff stores based on typical weekend traffic, but tracking every weekend hour in every country is impossible. Each store, city, and time zone would require constant counting. Instead, the manager samples 36 weekend hours across a few locations and uses the sample summary to estimate the overall average traffic rate for planning.

Given sample summaries: \(\bar{x} = 128\) customers/hour, \(s^2 = 256\) \((\text{customers/hour})^2\).

Reference only (not observable in real contexts): \(\mu = 130\) customers/hour and \(\sigma^2 = 225\) \((\text{customers/hour})^2\) from long-run audits.

Goal: build a 95% confidence interval for the mean weekend traffic.

↑
  • Sample size: \(n = 36\).
  • Sample mean: \(\bar{x} = 128\) customers/hour.
  • Sample variance: \(s^2 = 256\) \((\text{customers/hour})^2\).
  • Confidence level: 95%.
  • Reference-only parameters: \(\mu = 130\) customers/hour, \(\sigma^2 = 225\) \((\text{customers/hour})^2\).
↑

The population mean \(\mu = 130\) is a parameter, while \(\bar{x} = 128\) is the sample statistic used to estimate it.

For variability, the reference population variance is \(\sigma^2 = 225\) and the sample variance is \(s^2 = 256\), so the sample shows slightly more spread than the long-run benchmark.

These population values are not observable in practice; they are listed only to compare how close the sample summaries are to the true parameters.

↑

The point estimate for mean traffic is \(\bar{x} = 128\) customers/hour. An interval estimate adds a margin of error to reflect uncertainty because we do not observe every weekend hour in the population.

Interval estimation is preferred here because traffic fluctuates, so relying only on the point estimate can misstate staffing needs.

↑

Compute the confidence interval for the mean at the 95% confidence level using the known population variance.

\[ E = z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} = 1.96 \cdot \frac{15}{\sqrt{36}} = 4.90 \]

\[ \mu \in 128 \pm 4.90 = [123.10,\ 132.90] \text{ customers/hour} \]

↑

The 95% interval means planners can expect average weekend traffic to fall between about 123 and 133 customers per hour. Staffing and inventory budgets can be set near the upper bound when the cost of stockouts is high, while the lower bound helps identify minimum staffing required to avoid wasted labor.

Context: A small bakery buys imported flour, so its electricity use depends on global wheat prices and shipping delays. When costs rise, the bakery may run extra night shifts to meet orders, which changes energy bills. The owner wants a budget for the next year, but it is impossible to measure every future month because exchange rates and demand will change. The owner uses a sample of 25 months and summarizes the data to estimate the typical monthly electricity cost for planning.

Given sample summaries: \(\bar{x} = 82\) USD, \(s^2 = 100\) \(\text{USD}^2\).

Reference only (not observable in real contexts): the true population variance is \(\sigma^2 = 121\) \(\text{USD}^2\).

Goal: build a 90% confidence interval for the mean monthly electricity cost.

↑
  • Sample size: \(n = 25\).
  • Sample mean: \(\bar{x} = 82\) USD.
  • Sample variance: \(s^2 = 100\) \(\text{USD}^2\).
  • Confidence level: 90%.
  • Reference-only parameter: \(\sigma^2 = 121\) \(\text{USD}^2\).
↑

The population mean \(\mu\) is unknown, so the bakery must rely on \(\bar{x} = 82\) as the sample-based estimate.

The population variance \(\sigma^2 = 121\) is a reference-only value, and the sample variance \(s^2 = 100\) is slightly smaller, implying the sample months were a bit less volatile than the long-run pattern.

Because \(\mu\) is not observable, the sample statistic is the practical summary for budgeting decisions.

↑

The point estimate for monthly cost is \(\bar{x} = 82\) USD. The interval estimate shows a range of plausible values for \(\mu\), accounting for uncertainty from sampling only 25 months.

Since the bakery cannot access every month in the population, the interval estimate communicates risk more clearly than a single point.

↑

Compute the confidence interval for the mean at the 90% confidence level using the known population variance.

\[ E = z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} = 1.645 \cdot \frac{11}{\sqrt{25}} = 3.62 \]

\[ \mu \in 82 \pm 3.62 = [78.38,\ 85.62] \text{ USD} \]

↑

The 90% interval indicates the bakery should budget for average monthly electricity costs between about 78 and 86 USD. This range supports setting a reserve for utilities, and management can plan for the upper bound during peak baking seasons to avoid cash flow stress.

Context: A bank finances exporters who sell goods overseas, so loan demand rises when foreign buyers place big orders. The lending team tracks approval time because delays can slow shipments and cash flow. It is impossible to review every application in the global portfolio, especially across different time zones and branches. Instead, the operations lead audits 64 loan applications and uses the sample statistics to estimate the average turnaround time.

Given sample summaries: \(\bar{x} = 5.4\) days, \(s^2 = 2.25\) \(\text{days}^2\).

Reference only (not observable in real contexts): \(\mu = 5.0\) days and \(\sigma^2 = 1.96\) \(\text{days}^2\) from the bank's long-run quality dashboard.

Goal: build a 95% confidence interval for the mean approval time.

↑
  • Sample size: \(n = 64\).
  • Sample mean: \(\bar{x} = 5.4\) days.
  • Sample variance: \(s^2 = 2.25\) \(\text{days}^2\).
  • Confidence level: 95%.
  • Reference-only parameters: \(\mu = 5.0\) days, \(\sigma^2 = 1.96\) \(\text{days}^2\).
↑

The population mean \(\mu = 5.0\) is the parameter of interest, while \(\bar{x} = 5.4\) is the sample statistic estimating it.

The population variance \(\sigma^2 = 1.96\) is slightly smaller than the sample variance \(s^2 = 2.25\), suggesting the sampled approvals were a bit more variable than the long-run benchmark.

These population values are provided only for comparison, since they are not observable in real-time operations.

↑

The point estimate is \(\bar{x} = 5.4\) days. The interval estimate adds a margin of error to communicate uncertainty, since only a subset of all applications was audited.

Interval estimation is essential for service-level planning because turnaround times can fluctuate with staffing, policy, and demand.

↑

Compute the confidence interval for the mean at the 95% confidence level using the known population variance.

\[ E = z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} = 1.96 \cdot \frac{1.4}{\sqrt{64}} = 0.343 \]

\[ \mu \in 5.4 \pm 0.343 = [5.06,\ 5.74] \text{ days} \]

↑

The interval suggests the bank can be 95% confident that average approval time is about 5.1 to 5.7 days. This supports setting customer communication targets and staffing buffers; if service-level agreements require approvals under six days, the interval shows current performance is close but still requires monitoring.

Context: A delivery firm moves electronics across borders, so fuel spending changes with global oil prices, port delays, and longer routes when shipping lanes are crowded. The finance manager needs a daily fuel budget, but measuring every delivery day in every region is unrealistic. Weather, exchange rates, and demand from overseas customers make the population too large to track fully. The manager samples 49 delivery days and uses the sample statistics to estimate the average daily fuel expense.

Given sample summaries: \(\bar{x} = 62\) USD, \(s^2 = 81\) \(\text{USD}^2\).

Reference only (not observable in real contexts): the true population variance is \(\sigma^2 = 100\) \(\text{USD}^2\).

Goal: build a 95% confidence interval for the mean daily fuel expense.

↑
  • Sample size: \(n = 49\).
  • Sample mean: \(\bar{x} = 62\) USD.
  • Sample variance: \(s^2 = 81\) \(\text{USD}^2\).
  • Confidence level: 95%.
  • Reference-only parameter: \(\sigma^2 = 100\) \(\text{USD}^2\).
↑

The population mean \(\mu\) is unknown, so \(\bar{x} = 62\) is the statistic used to estimate it.

The population variance \(\sigma^2 = 100\) is a reference-only benchmark. The sample variance \(s^2 = 81\) is slightly lower, suggesting the sample days were less volatile than the long-run fuel-cost variability.

Because the true mean is not directly observable, the sample statistic is the practical basis for budgeting.

↑

The point estimate for mean fuel expense is \(\bar{x} = 62\) USD. The interval estimate acknowledges uncertainty because only 49 days were observed, not the full population of delivery days.

Interval estimation is essential for cost planning since fuel prices can change quickly.

↑

Compute the confidence interval for the mean at the 95% confidence level using the known population variance.

\[ E = z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} = 1.96 \cdot \frac{10}{\sqrt{49}} \approx 2.80 \]

\[ \mu \in 62 \pm 2.80 = [59.20,\ 64.80] \text{ USD} \]

↑

The 95% interval indicates average daily fuel expense is likely between about 59 and 65 USD. This range helps finance teams set per-route fuel allowances and decide when to lock in bulk fuel contracts to protect against higher daily costs near the upper bound.
