Term 3 - Practice Activity 1 (C5)

Practice writing hypothesis tests in context and solving one-sample mean tests with known population standard deviation.

C5 Formulates the null and alternative hypotheses in contextual situations.

  • General problem
  • Average monthly spending on phone plans
  • Average weekly grocery budget
  • Average monthly commuting cost
  • Average daily screen-free time after work
  • Average monthly gym visits
  • Average focus hours in deep-work sessions
  • Average monthly streaming subscriptions cost
  • Average monthly commuting distance
  • Average number of sick days used per year

A benchmark claim may refer to a population mean. A hypothesis test is conducted at a given significance level \(\alpha\) to evaluate whether the sample evidence supports or contradicts the benchmark value.

Mean template: A benchmark mean value (for example, 75) is given for a population mean. A sample of size \(n\) (for example, 36) is collected with sample mean \(\bar{x}\) (for example, 79). If provided, the population standard deviation \(\sigma\) is known (for example, 12). Using a significance level (for example, 1%), test whether the true population mean is lower than, higher than, or different from the benchmark mean.

Questions

  1. Write the hypotheses for the case where the true parameter is lower than the benchmark.
  2. Write the hypotheses for the case where the true parameter is higher than the benchmark.
  3. Write the hypotheses for the case where the true parameter is different from the benchmark.
  • General setup: Identify the parameter of interest, the benchmark value, the sample information, and the significance level \(\alpha\).
  • Mean test with known \(\sigma\) (one-sample \(z\)-test):
    • Define \(\mu\) as the true population mean and \(\mu_0\) as the benchmark mean.
    • State hypotheses according to direction: \(H_0\colon \mu=\mu_0\), \(H_a\colon \mu<\mu_0\) or \(H_a\colon \mu>\mu_0\) or \(H_a\colon \mu\ne\mu_0\).
    • Compute \(z=\frac{\bar{x}-\mu_0}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}\).
    • Compute the \(p\)-value using the matching tail type.
    • Decision rule: reject \(H_0\) if \(p\)-value \(\le \alpha\); otherwise fail to reject \(H_0\).
    • State a contextual conclusion about the population mean relative to the benchmark.

A mobile provider says customers spend an average of 45 dollars per month on phone plans. A random sample of 64 customers has an average of 46.2 dollars per month. The known long-run standard deviation is 8 dollars. Use a 5% significance level to test whether the true mean spending is higher than 45 dollars.

  • Let \(\mu\) be the true mean monthly spending on phone plans.
  • Hypotheses: \(H_0\colon \mu=45\), \(H_1\colon \mu>45\).
  • Test statistic: \(z=\frac{46.2-45}{8/\sqrt{64}}=\frac{1.2}{1}=1.20\).
  • \(p\)-value: \(P(Z\ge 1.20)\approx 0.1151\).
  • Decision: Fail to reject \(H_0\) since \(0.1151\ge 0.05\).
  • Conclusion: There is insufficient evidence that the true average monthly phone-plan spending is above 45 dollars.

A budgeting app reports that users spend 180 dollars per week on groceries on average. A random sample of 100 users shows an average of 171 dollars per week. The known long-run standard deviation is 18 dollars. Test whether the true mean is lower than 180 dollars using two significance levels: 5% and 1%.

  • Let \(\mu\) be the true mean weekly grocery spending.
  • Hypotheses: \(H_0\colon \mu=180\), \(H_1\colon \mu<180\).
  • Test statistic: \(z=\frac{171-180}{18/\sqrt{100}}=\frac{-9}{1.8}=-5.00\).
  • \(p\)-value: \(P(Z\le -5.00)\approx 0.0000\).
  • Decision at 5%: Reject \(H_0\) since \(0.0000<0.05\).
  • Decision at 1%: Reject \(H_0\) since \(0.0000<0.01\).
  • Conclusion: At both significance levels, there is strong evidence that the true average weekly grocery spending is below 180 dollars.

A workplace wellness program says employees spend 220 dollars per month on commuting on average. A random sample of 64 employees has an average of 224 dollars per month. The known long-run standard deviation is 15 dollars. Test whether the true mean is different from 220 dollars using two significance levels: 5% and 1%.

  • Let \(\mu\) be the true mean monthly commuting cost.
  • Hypotheses: \(H_0\colon \mu=220\), \(H_1\colon \mu\ne 220\).
  • Test statistic: \(z=\frac{224-220}{15/\sqrt{64}}=\frac{4}{1.875}\approx 2.13\).
  • \(p\)-value: \(2P(Z\ge 2.13)\approx 0.0332\).
  • Decision at 5%: Reject \(H_0\) since \(0.0332<0.05\).
  • Decision at 1%: Fail to reject \(H_0\) since \(0.0332\ge 0.01\).
  • Conclusion: The data suggest a difference from 220 dollars at 5%, but not at 1%.

A productivity coach says people average 90 minutes of screen-free time after work each day. The known long-run standard deviation is 20 minutes. Use a 5% significance level.

  1. Sample A: A random sample of 64 people has an average of 95 minutes. Test whether the true mean is higher than 90 minutes.
  2. Sample B: A different random sample of 100 people has an average of 84 minutes. Test whether the true mean is lower than 90 minutes.
  • Let \(\mu\) be the true mean daily screen-free time after work.
  • (a) Higher-than test
    • Hypotheses: \(H_0\colon \mu=90\), \(H_1\colon \mu>90\).
    • Test statistic: \(z=\frac{95-90}{20/\sqrt{64}}=\frac{5}{2.5}=2.00\).
    • \(p\)-value: \(P(Z\ge 2.00)\approx 0.0228\).
    • Decision: Reject \(H_0\) since \(0.0228<0.05\).
    • Conclusion: There is evidence that the mean screen-free time is higher than 90 minutes.
  • (b) Lower-than test
    • Hypotheses: \(H_0\colon \mu=90\), \(H_1\colon \mu<90\).
    • Test statistic: \(z=\frac{84-90}{20/\sqrt{100}}=\frac{-6}{2}=-3.00\).
    • \(p\)-value: \(P(Z\le -3.00)\approx 0.0013\).
    • Decision: Reject \(H_0\) since \(0.0013<0.05\).
    • Conclusion: There is evidence that the mean screen-free time is lower than 90 minutes in Sample B.
