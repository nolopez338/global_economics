Term 3 - Practice Activity 1 (C4)

Practice the complete hypothesis-testing procedure in contextual situations with fixed probability: define hypotheses, verify conditions, compute statistics, find p-values, make decisions, and interpret conclusions.

Evaluated Criterion

C4 Applies the general procedure for hypothesis testing with fixed probability in contextual situations.

  • General Problem
  • Food delivery completion rate
  • Average one-way commute time for office staff
  • Weekly side-income amount from freelance tasks
  • Monthly public transport spending benchmark
  • Use of automatic savings transfers
  • Average daily sleep before workdays
  • Average monthly spending on mobile data plans
  • Use of monthly budgeting reminders
  • Budgeting reminders: near-boundary lower-tail result
  • Budgeting reminders: lower-tail and two-tailed rejection
  • Budgeting reminders: higher-tail and two-tailed rejection

A benchmark claim may refer to a population proportion. A hypothesis test is conducted at a significance level \(\alpha\) to evaluate whether the sample evidence supports or contradicts the benchmark value.

Proportion template: A benchmark percentage (for example, 62%) is given for a population proportion. A sample of size \(n\) (for example, 200) is collected, with observed successes \(x\) (for example, 112). Using a significance level (for example, 5%), test whether the true population proportion is lower than, higher than, or different from the benchmark percentage.

Questions

  1. Write the hypotheses for the case where the true parameter is lower than the benchmark.
  2. Write the hypotheses for the case where the true parameter is higher than the benchmark.
  3. Write the hypotheses for the case where the true parameter is different from the benchmark.
  • General setup: Identify parameter, benchmark value, sample information, and significance level \(\alpha\).
  • Proportion test (one-sample \(z\)-test):
    • Define \(p\) as the true population proportion and \(p_0\) as benchmark proportion.
    • State hypotheses: \(H_0\colon p=p_0\), \(H_a\colon p<p_0\) or \(p>p_0\) or \(p\ne p_0\).
    • Check conditions: \(np_0\ge 10\), \(n(1-p_0)\ge 10\).
    • Compute \(\hat p=\dfrac{x}{n}\).
    • Compute \(z=\dfrac{\hat p-p_0}{\sqrt{\frac{p_0(1-p_0)}{n}}}\).
    • Find p-value using the correct tail type.
    • Decision: reject \(H_0\) if p-value \(\le\alpha\); otherwise fail to reject \(H_0\).
    • Conclude in context relative to the benchmark.

A local delivery app states that 64% of assigned orders are completed without cancellation. In a random sample of 220 assigned orders, 129 are completed without cancellation. Using a significance level of 5%, test whether the true completion proportion is lower than 64%.

  • Hypotheses: \(H_0\colon p=0.64\), \(H_a\colon p<0.64\).
  • Conditions: \(np_0=220(0.64)=140.8\ge 10\), \(n(1-p_0)=220(0.36)=79.2\ge 10\).
  • Sample statistic: \(\hat p=129/220\approx 0.586\).
  • Test statistic: \(z=\dfrac{0.586-0.64}{\sqrt{\frac{0.64(0.36)}{220}}}\approx -1.66\).
  • p-value: \(P(Z\le -1.66)\approx 0.0485\) (4.85%).
  • Decision: Reject \(H_0\).
  • Conclusion: Sufficient evidence that completion rate is lower than 64%.

A company dashboard sets 42 minutes as the benchmark average one-way commute time for staff. A random sample of 49 employees has an average one-way commute of 43.0 minutes, and the population standard deviation is known to be 8 minutes. Using a significance level of 5%, test whether the true average commute time is higher than 42 minutes.

  • Hypotheses: \(H_0\colon \mu=42\), \(H_a\colon \mu>42\).
  • Sample statistic: \(\bar x=43.0\).
  • Test statistic: \(z=\dfrac{43.0-42}{8/\sqrt{49}}=\dfrac{1.0}{1.143}\approx 0.88\).
  • p-value: \(P(Z\ge 0.88)\approx 0.1894\) (18.94%).
  • Decision: Fail to reject \(H_0\).
  • Conclusion: Not enough evidence that average commute is higher than 42 minutes.

A financial planning blog uses 310 dollars per week as a benchmark for average side-income from freelance tasks. A random sample of 64 workers has an average weekly side-income of 296 dollars, and the population standard deviation is known to be 40 dollars. Test whether the true average weekly side-income is different from 310 dollars at significance levels of 5% and 1%.

  • Hypotheses: \(H_0\colon \mu=310\), \(H_a\colon \mu\ne 310\).
  • Sample statistic: \(\bar x=296\).
  • Test statistic: \(z=\dfrac{296-310}{40/\sqrt{64}}=\dfrac{-14}{5}=-2.80\).
  • p-value: \(2P(Z\le -2.80)\approx 0.0051\) (0.51%).
  • Decision at 5%: Reject \(H_0\).
  • Decision at 1%: Reject \(H_0\).
  • Conclusion: At both levels, sufficient evidence that the mean is different from 310 dollars.

A mobility report sets 185 dollars as the benchmark average monthly public transport spending. A random sample of 50 commuters has an average monthly spending of 191.2 dollars, and the population standard deviation is known to be 20 dollars. Test whether the true average monthly spending is different from 185 dollars at significance levels of 5% and 1%.

  • Hypotheses: \(H_0\colon \mu=185\), \(H_a\colon \mu\ne 185\).
  • Sample statistic: \(\bar x=191.2\).
  • Test statistic: \(z=\dfrac{191.2-185}{20/\sqrt{50}}\approx 2.19\).
  • p-value: \(2P(Z\ge 2.19)\approx 0.0286\) (2.86%).
  • Decision at 5%: Reject \(H_0\).
  • Decision at 1%: Fail to reject \(H_0\).
  • Conclusion: Evidence of a difference at 5%, but not at 1%.

A budgeting app reports that 52% of users keep an automatic weekly savings transfer active. In a random sample of 250 users, 133 have active automatic transfers. Use a significance level of 5% for both parts.

  1. Test whether the true proportion with active automatic transfers is lower than 52%.
  2. Test whether the true proportion with active automatic transfers is higher than 52%.

Part (a): \(H_0\colon p=0.52\), \(H_a\colon p<0.52\); \(\hat p=0.532\), \(z\approx 0.38\), p-value \(\approx 0.6480\). Fail to reject \(H_0\): not enough evidence the proportion is lower.

Part (b): \(H_0\colon p=0.52\), \(H_a\colon p>0.52\); same \(\hat p\), \(z\approx 0.38\), p-value \(\approx 0.3520\). Fail to reject \(H_0\): not enough evidence the proportion is higher.

Conditions for both parts: \(np_0=130\ge 10\), \(n(1-p_0)=120\ge 10\).

A wellness report sets 7.5 hours as the benchmark average sleep duration before workdays. A random sample of 36 people has an average of 6.4 hours, and the population standard deviation is known to be 3.6 hours. Use a significance level of 5% for both parts.

  1. Test whether the true average sleep duration is higher than 7.5 hours.
  2. Test whether the true average sleep duration is lower than 7.5 hours.

Part (a): \(H_0\colon \mu=7.5\), \(H_a\colon \mu>7.5\), \(z\approx -1.83\), p-value \(\approx 0.9666\). Fail to reject \(H_0\).

Part (b): \(H_0\colon \mu=7.5\), \(H_a\colon \mu<7.5\), \(z\approx -1.83\), p-value \(\approx 0.0334\). Reject \(H_0\).

Conclusion: sufficient evidence only for the lower-than claim.
