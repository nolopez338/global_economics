Evaluated criteria

  • C1: Describe the concept of a statistical hypothesis in context.
  • C2: Identify the null and alternative hypotheses using correct parameter notation.
  • C3: Determine whether a test is one tailed or two tailed based on the claim.

Introduction

This activity is organized as a progression. In Block 1, you identify the correct test direction from context and complete the test decision. In Block 2, the hypotheses and tail direction remain fixed while the significance level changes, so you can see how conclusions can change when standards of evidence change. In Block 3, you compare one-sided and two-sided alternatives on the same evidence.

Use statistically precise language in your conclusions: reject \(H_0\) or fail to reject \(H_0\).

Math-support program and average algebra score (lower-tailed direction)

A school wants to check whether a new support schedule has reduced average algebra quiz scores below the benchmark of 72 points. A sample of 36 students has mean score \(\bar{x}=69.5\). Assume population standard deviation \(\sigma=9\) points and use \(\alpha=0.05\).

Questions/Tasks. (C1) Identify parameter type and symbols. (C2) Write the hypothesis pair that fits the context. (C3) Classify the tail and justify it. Then compute the test statistic, estimate the p-value, compare with \(\alpha\), and state the conclusion in context.

C1

Mean setting. Let \(\mu\) be the true mean algebra score; \(\mu_0=72\), \(n=36\), \(\bar{x}=69.5\), \(\sigma=9\), \(\alpha=0.05\).

C2

\(H_0:\mu=72\), \(H_a:\mu<72\).

C3

Left-tailed test because the claim is ``reduced'' (lower than benchmark).

\[ z=\frac{\bar{x}-\mu_0}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}=\frac{69.5-72}{9/6}=\frac{-2.5}{1.5}=-1.67. \]

For a left tail, \(p\approx P(Z\le -1.67)\approx 0.048\). Since \(0.048<0.05\), reject \(H_0\).

Conclusion: At the 5\% level, there is sufficient evidence that the true mean algebra score is below 72.

Delivery app on-time rate after route training (higher-tailed direction)

A local delivery company claims that new route training increased the on-time delivery proportion above the historical 80\% level. In a sample of \(n=250\) deliveries, \(x=214\) were on time. Use \(\alpha=0.05\).

Questions/Tasks. (C1) Identify parameter type and symbols. (C2) Write the context-appropriate hypotheses. (C3) Classify tail and justify. Then compute test evidence and conclude.

C1

Proportion setting. Let \(p\) be true on-time proportion; \(p_0=0.80\), \(n=250\), \(x=214\), \(\hat{p}=214/250=0.856\), \(\alpha=0.05\).

C2

\(H_0:p=0.80\), \(H_a:p>0.80\).

C3

Right-tailed test because claim is ``increased above 80\%.''

\[ z=\frac{\hat p-p_0}{\sqrt{p_0(1-p_0)/n}}=\frac{0.856-0.80}{\sqrt{0.8(0.2)/250}}=\frac{0.056}{0.0253}\approx 2.21. \]

Right-tail p-value: \(p\approx P(Z\ge 2.21)\approx 0.014\). Since \(0.014<0.05\), reject \(H_0\).

Conclusion: At the 5\% level, evidence supports that the true on-time proportion is above 80\%.

Clinic waiting time consistency check (two-tailed direction)

A school clinic monitors whether average waiting time is different from its 18-minute benchmark after a staffing change. A sample of \(n=49\) visits gives \(\bar{x}=18.9\) minutes. Assume \(\sigma=4.2\) minutes and use \(\alpha=0.05\).

Questions/Tasks. (C1) Identify parameter type and symbols. (C2) Write hypotheses for ``different.'' (C3) Classify tail and justify. Then test and conclude.

C1

Mean setting. Let \(\mu\) be true mean waiting time; \(\mu_0=18\), \(n=49\), \(\bar{x}=18.9\), \(\sigma=4.2\), \(\alpha=0.05\).

C2

\(H_0:\mu=18\), \(H_a:\mu\ne 18\).

C3

Two-tailed test because ``different'' allows either direction.

\[ z=\frac{18.9-18}{4.2/\sqrt{49}}=\frac{0.9}{0.6}=1.50. \]

Two-tailed p-value: \(p\approx 2P(Z\ge 1.50)\approx 0.134\). Since \(0.134>0.05\), fail to reject \(H_0\).

Conclusion: At the 5\% level, evidence is insufficient to conclude the true mean waiting time is different from 18 minutes.

Fuel efficiency review under different policy strictness (lower tail, changing \(\alpha\))

A transport office checks whether a new maintenance cycle lowered average bus fuel efficiency below 14.0 km/L. A sample of \(n=64\) buses gives \(\bar{x}=13.70\) km/L, with known \(\sigma=1.2\) km/L.

For routine monitoring they use \(\alpha=0.10\), and for annual accountability reporting they use \(\alpha=0.05\).

Questions/Tasks. (C1--C3) Identify symbols, hypotheses, and tail once, then keep them fixed. Compute one test statistic and p-value, and make decisions at both significance levels.

C1

Mean setting. \(\mu\): true mean fuel efficiency; \(\mu_0=14.0\), \(n=64\), \(\bar{x}=13.70\), \(\sigma=1.2\).

C2 (fixed across both levels). \(H_0:\mu=14.0\), \(H_a:\mu<14.0\).

C3 (fixed across both levels). Left-tailed test.

\[ z=\frac{13.70-14.0}{1.2/8}=\frac{-0.30}{0.15}=-2.00, \quad p\approx P(Z\le -2.00)=0.0228. \]
  • At \(\alpha=0.10\): \(0.0228<0.10\) so reject \(H_0\).
  • At \(\alpha=0.05\): \(0.0228<0.05\) so reject \(H_0\).

To show sensitivity, compare with a stricter audit level \(\alpha=0.01\): \(0.0228>0.01\), so fail to reject \(H_0\).

Conclusion: Hypotheses and tail type remain fixed; the decision changes only when the evidence standard becomes much stricter.

Scholarship completion-rate target (higher tail, changing \(\alpha\))

A foundation tests whether a mentoring program increased scholarship completion proportion above 60\%. From \(n=200\) students, \(x=131\) completed the program. For internal planning they use \(\alpha=0.10\), but for public reporting they require \(\alpha=0.05\).

Questions/Tasks. Identify C1--C3 once, keep them fixed, compute test evidence once, and decide at both \(\alpha\) levels.

C1

Proportion setting. \(p\): true completion proportion; \(p_0=0.60\), \(n=200\), \(x=131\), \(\hat p=0.655\).

C2 (fixed). \(H_0:p=0.60\), \(H_a:p>0.60\).

C3 (fixed). Right-tailed test.

\[ z=\frac{0.655-0.60}{\sqrt{0.6(0.4)/200}}=\frac{0.055}{0.0346}\approx 1.59, \quad p\approx P(Z\ge 1.59)\approx 0.056. \]
  • At \(\alpha=0.10\): \(0.056<0.10\) so reject \(H_0\).
  • At \(\alpha=0.05\): \(0.056>0.05\) so fail to reject \(H_0\).

Conclusion: Same hypotheses and right-tail direction, but the final decision changes because \(\alpha\) changes.

Factory packaging-weight stability check (two tail, changing \(\alpha\))

A factory checks whether mean package weight is different from the 500 g specification. A sample of \(n=100\) packages has \(\bar{x}=501.2\) g, with known \(\sigma=6\) g. Routine checks use \(\alpha=0.10\); regulatory reports use \(\alpha=0.05\).

Questions/Tasks. Identify C1--C3 once, keep them fixed, then show decisions for both significance levels.

C1

Mean setting. \(\mu\): true mean package weight; \(\mu_0=500\), \(n=100\), \(\bar{x}=501.2\), \(\sigma=6\).

C2 (fixed). \(H_0:\mu=500\), \(H_a:\mu\ne 500\).

C3 (fixed). Two-tailed test.

\[ z=\frac{501.2-500}{6/10}=\frac{1.2}{0.6}=2.00, \quad p\approx 2P(Z\ge 2.00)=0.0456. \]
  • At \(\alpha=0.10\): \(0.0456<0.10\) so reject \(H_0\).
  • At \(\alpha=0.05\): \(0.0456<0.05\) so reject \(H_0\).

With stricter quality escalation \(\alpha=0.01\), \(0.0456>0.01\), so fail to reject \(H_0\).

Conclusion: The alternative remains ``different'' throughout; only the significance level changes the decision threshold.

App response time: lower vs different on the same data

A software team studies whether average response time is below the 250 ms benchmark after optimization. Some stakeholders also ask whether response time is simply different from 250 ms. The same sample gives \(n=49\), \(\bar{x}=246.8\) ms, known \(\sigma=8\) ms, and \(\alpha=0.05\).

Questions/Tasks. Using the same data and significance level:

  • Formulation A: test \(H_a:\mu<250\).
  • Formulation B: test \(H_a:\mu\ne 250\).

Compare hypotheses, tail type, p-values, and conclusions.

C1

Mean setting with \(\mu_0=250\), \(n=49\), \(\bar{x}=246.8\), \(\sigma=8\), \(\alpha=0.05\).

C2

Formulation A (lower).
\(H_0:\mu=250\), \(H_a:\mu<250\), left-tailed.

Formulation B (different).
\(H_0:\mu=250\), \(H_a:\mu\ne 250\), two-tailed.

C3

\[ z=\frac{246.8-250}{8/7}=\frac{-3.2}{1.1429}\approx -2.80. \]

\(p_A\approx P(Z\le -2.80)=0.0026\). Since \(0.0026<0.05\), reject \(H_0\).

\(p_B\approx 2P(Z\le -2.80)=0.0052\). Since \(0.0052<0.05\), reject \(H_0\).

Comparison conclusion: Both tests reject \(H_0\) at 5\%, but the two-tailed p-value is larger because evidence is split across two directions.

Daily study-time claim: higher vs different on the same data

An academic coach claims students now study more than 2.0 hours per day after a planning workshop. A second reviewer asks whether study time is merely different from 2.0 hours. The same sample gives \(n=36\), \(\bar{x}=2.32\) hours, known \(\sigma=1.2\) hours, and \(\alpha=0.05\).

Questions/Tasks. Using the same data and significance level:

  • Formulation A: test \(H_a:\mu>2.0\).
  • Formulation B: test \(H_a:\mu\ne 2.0\).

Compare hypotheses, tail type, p-values, and conclusions.

C1

Mean setting with \(\mu_0=2.0\), \(n=36\), \(\bar{x}=2.32\), \(\sigma=1.2\), \(\alpha=0.05\).

C2

Formulation A (higher).
\(H_0:\mu=2.0\), \(H_a:\mu>2.0\), right-tailed.

Formulation B (different).
\(H_0:\mu=2.0\), \(H_a:\mu\ne 2.0\), two-tailed.

C3

\[ z=\frac{2.32-2.0}{1.2/6}=\frac{0.32}{0.20}=1.60. \]

\(p_A\approx P(Z\ge 1.60)=0.0548\). Since \(0.0548>0.05\), fail to reject \(H_0\).

\(p_B\approx 2P(Z\ge 1.60)=0.1096\). Since \(0.1096>0.05\), fail to reject \(H_0\).

Comparison conclusion: Both formulations fail to reject \(H_0\) at 5\%. The two-tailed version is less significant because it tests departures in both directions.
