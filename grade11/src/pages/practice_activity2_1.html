A regional logistics agency wants to estimate the mean time (in minutes) for cargo to clear port inspections.

Historical monitoring shows the population standard deviation is \(\sigma = 12\) minutes.

The agency wants a 95% confidence estimate with a maximum error of \(E = 3\) minutes.

Determine the minimum sample size needed.

↑

This solution establishes the sample size so the error will not exceed a specified amount by identifying \(E\), selecting \(z^*\), substituting into the formula, and rounding up to the minimum integer sample size.

Step 1: Identify the target error and confidence level. The maximum error is \(E = 3\) minutes and the confidence level is 95%, so \(z^* = 1.96\).

Step 2: Apply the sample size formula.

\[ n = \left(\frac{z^*\,\sigma}{E}\right)^2 = \left(\frac{1.96 \cdot 12}{3}\right)^2 = \left(7.84\right)^2 = 61.47 \]

Step 3: Round up and verify Criterion 6. Because the sample size must ensure the error does not exceed \(E\), we round up: \(n = 62\). With \(n=62\), the margin of error is at most 3 minutes, satisfying Criterion 6.

A development agency wants to estimate the mean monthly food spending for households in a region.

A pilot survey suggests the population standard deviation is about \(\sigma \approx 45\) currency units.

The agency wants a 90% confidence estimate with a target interval width of \(W = 16\) currency units.

The width is defined as \(W = \text{Upper} - \text{Lower} = 2E\).

Determine the minimum sample size needed.

↑

This solution establishes the sample size so the interval width will not exceed a specified amount by converting \(W\) to \(E\), selecting the correct \(z^*\), substituting into the formula, and rounding up to the minimum safe integer.

Step 1: Identify the target width and confidence level. The target width is \(W = 16\), so \(E = W/2 = 8\). The confidence level is 90%, so \(z^* = 1.645\).

Step 2: Apply the sample size formula.

\[ n = \left(\frac{z^*\,\sigma}{E}\right)^2 = \left(\frac{1.645 \cdot 45}{8}\right)^2 = \left(9.253\right)^2 \approx 85.62 \]

Step 3: Round up and verify Criterion 6. We must round up to keep the width within \(W\), so \(n = 86\). This minimum sample size guarantees the interval width is no more than 16 currency units.

A shipping cooperative wants to estimate the mean fuel cost per container on a trade route.

Company records indicate the population standard deviation is \(\sigma = 18\) dollars.

A sample of \(n = 36\) containers has a mean fuel cost of \(\bar{x} = 240\) dollars.

Construct 90%, 95%, and 98% confidence intervals for the mean fuel cost.

↑

This solution calculates the confidence interval for the mean with known variance by computing the standard error, then using the appropriate \(z^*\) values for each confidence level.

Step 1: Compute the standard error.

\[ SE = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} = \frac{18}{\sqrt{36}} = 3 \]

Step 2: Compute each confidence interval.

\[ \text{90\% CI: } \bar{x} \pm z^* SE = 240 \pm 1.645(3) = 240 \pm 4.94 = (235.06,\ 244.94) \]

\[ \text{95\% CI: } \bar{x} \pm z^* SE = 240 \pm 1.96(3) = 240 \pm 5.88 = (234.12,\ 245.88) \]

\[ \text{98\% CI: } \bar{x} \pm z^* SE = 240 \pm 2.326(3) = 240 \pm 6.98 = (233.02,\ 246.98) \]

Step 3: Interpret the results. The intervals widen as confidence increases. We are 90%, 95%, and 98% confident, respectively, that the true mean fuel cost lies in the corresponding interval.

A trade corridor study tracks the mean daily transit time (in minutes) for cross-border trucks.

The population standard deviation is known to be \(\sigma = 12\) minutes.

A sample of \(n = 64\) trucks yields a mean transit time of \(\bar{x} = 72\) minutes.

Construct 90%, 95%, and 98% confidence intervals for the mean transit time, and report the width \(W = \text{Upper} - \text{Lower} = 2E\) for each.

↑

This solution calculates multiple confidence intervals with known variance by computing the standard error, then using the appropriate \(z^*\) values for 90%, 95%, and 98% confidence.

Step 1: Compute the standard error.

\[ SE = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} = \frac{12}{\sqrt{64}} = 1.5 \]

Step 2: Compute each confidence interval and its width.

\[ \text{90\% CI: } \bar{x} \pm z^* SE = 72 \pm 1.645(1.5) = 72 \pm 2.47 = (69.53,\ 74.47),\ W = 4.94 \]

\[ \text{95\% CI: } \bar{x} \pm z^* SE = 72 \pm 1.96(1.5) = 72 \pm 2.94 = (69.06,\ 74.94),\ W = 5.88 \]

\[ \text{98\% CI: } \bar{x} \pm z^* SE = 72 \pm 2.326(1.5) = 72 \pm 3.49 = (68.51,\ 75.49),\ W = 6.98 \]

Step 3: Interpret the results. Higher confidence levels require wider intervals (larger \(W\)). Each interval gives a plausible range for the mean transit time at its confidence level.

An inflation monitoring team wants to estimate the mean price of a staple food basket (in dollars) across markets.

They know the population standard deviation is \(\sigma = 0.60\) dollars and the current sample mean is \(\bar{x} = 2.85\) dollars.

The team has collected \(n_{\text{current}} = 36\) market prices and wants a 90% confidence interval.

(a) Construct the confidence interval for the mean price.

(b) How many additional observations are required to reduce the margin of error to \(E = 0.12\) dollars?

↑

This solution calculates the confidence interval for the mean with known variance and then determines how many additional observations are needed to reach the target margin of error.

Step 1: Compute the standard error.

\[ SE = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n_{\text{current}}}} = \frac{0.60}{\sqrt{36}} = 0.10 \]

Step 2: Construct the confidence interval. For 90% confidence, \(z^* = 1.645\).

\[ \bar{x} \pm z^* SE = 2.85 \pm 1.645(0.10) = 2.85 \pm 0.16 = (2.69,\ 3.01) \]

Step 3: Compute the required sample size for the target margin of error.

\[ n_{\text{required}} = \left(\frac{z^*\,\sigma}{E}\right)^2 = \left(\frac{1.645 \cdot 0.60}{0.12}\right)^2 = \left(8.225\right)^2 \approx 67.65 \]

Step 4: Determine additional observations. Round up to \(n_{\text{required}} = 68\). The additional observations needed are \(68 - 36 = 32\).

Conclusion. The 90% confidence interval is \((2.69,\ 3.01)\), and the team needs 32 more observations to achieve a margin of error of 0.12 dollars.

A distribution center tracks the mean time (in minutes) required to handle incoming shipments.

The population standard deviation is known to be \(\sigma = 12\) minutes.

The current sample mean is \(\bar{x} = 52\) minutes with \(n_{\text{current}} = 40\) observations and a 95% confidence level.

(a) Construct the confidence interval for the mean handling time.

(b) How many additional observations are needed to reach a target interval width of \(W = 6\) minutes? (Recall \(W = \text{Upper} - \text{Lower} = 2E\).)

↑

This solution calculates a confidence interval with known variance and then determines how many additional observations are needed to meet a target width.

Step 1: Compute the standard error.

\[ SE = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n_{\text{current}}}} = \frac{12}{\sqrt{40}} \approx 1.90 \]

Step 2: Construct the confidence interval. For 95% confidence, \(z^* = 1.96\).

\[ \bar{x} \pm z^* SE = 52 \pm 1.96(1.90) = 52 \pm 3.72 = (48.28,\ 55.72) \]

Step 3: Compute the required sample size for the target width. The target width is \(W = 6\), so \(E = W/2 = 3\).

\[ n_{\text{required}} = \left(\frac{z^*\,\sigma}{E}\right)^2 = \left(\frac{1.96 \cdot 12}{3}\right)^2 = \left(7.84\right)^2 \approx 61.47 \]

Step 4: Determine additional observations. Round up to \(n_{\text{required}} = 62\). The additional observations needed are \(62 - 40 = 22\).

Conclusion. The 95% confidence interval is \((48.28,\ 55.72)\), and 22 more observations are needed to reach a width of 6 minutes.

A microcredit program tracks the mean processing fee (in dollars) charged per loan.

The population standard deviation is known to be \(\sigma = 16\) dollars.

The current sample mean is \(\bar{x} = 88\) dollars with \(n_{\text{current}} = 64\) observations at 90% confidence.

(a) Construct the confidence interval for the mean fee.

(b) How many more observations are needed if the margin of error increases by 1.0 dollar? (Use the new target \(E = E_{\text{current}} + 1.0\).)

↑

This solution computes the confidence interval and then checks whether additional observations are needed for a larger margin of error.

Step 1: Compute the standard error.

\[ SE = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n_{\text{current}}}} = \frac{16}{\sqrt{64}} = 2 \]

Step 2: Construct the confidence interval. For 90% confidence, \(z^* = 1.645\).

\[ \bar{x} \pm z^* SE = 88 \pm 1.645(2) = 88 \pm 3.29 = (84.71,\ 91.29) \]

Step 3: Compute the required sample size for the larger margin of error. The target margin is \(E = 3.29 + 1.0 = 4.29\).

\[ n_{\text{required}} = \left(\frac{z^*\,\sigma}{E}\right)^2 = \left(\frac{1.645 \cdot 16}{4.29}\right)^2 = \left(6.14\right)^2 \approx 37.70 \]

Step 4: Determine additional observations. Round up to \(n_{\text{required}} = 38\). The additional observations needed are \(\max(0, 38 - 64) = 0\).

Conclusion. The 90% confidence interval is \((84.71,\ 91.29)\), and no additional observations are required because the larger margin of error is already met.

A rural water agency measures the mean daily delivery volume (in liters) for tanker trucks.

The population standard deviation is \(\sigma = 18\) liters.

The current sample mean is \(\bar{x} = 205\) liters with \(n_{\text{current}} = 81\) observations at 95% confidence.

(a) Construct the confidence interval for the mean delivery volume.

(b) How many more observations are needed if the interval width increases by 2 liters? (Use the new target \(W = W_{\text{current}} + 2\).)

↑

This solution computes the confidence interval and then checks whether additional observations are needed for a larger interval width.

Step 1: Compute the standard error.

\[ SE = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n_{\text{current}}}} = \frac{18}{\sqrt{81}} = 2 \]

Step 2: Construct the confidence interval. For 95% confidence, \(z^* = 1.96\).

\[ \bar{x} \pm z^* SE = 205 \pm 1.96(2) = 205 \pm 3.92 = (201.08,\ 208.92) \]

Step 3: Compute the required sample size for the larger width. The current width is \(W_{\text{current}} = 2E = 2(3.92) = 7.84\), so the target is \(W = 9.84\), giving \(E = W/2 = 4.92\).

\[ n_{\text{required}} = \left(\frac{z^*\,\sigma}{E}\right)^2 = \left(\frac{1.96 \cdot 18}{4.92}\right)^2 = \left(7.17\right)^2 \approx 51.41 \]

Step 4: Determine additional observations. Round up to \(n_{\text{required}} = 52\). The additional observations needed are \(\max(0, 52 - 81) = 0\).

Conclusion. The 95% confidence interval is \((201.08,\ 208.92)\), and no additional observations are required because the larger width is already achieved.

An export office tracks the mean documentation cost (in dollars) per shipment.

The population standard deviation is \(\sigma = 21\) dollars.

The current sample mean is \(\bar{x} = 310\) dollars with \(n_{\text{current}} = 49\) observations at 98% confidence.

(a) Construct the confidence interval for the mean documentation cost.

(b) How many more observations are needed if the margin of error increases by 1.5 dollars? (Use the new target \(E = E_{\text{current}} + 1.5\).)

↑

This solution computes the confidence interval and then checks whether additional observations are needed for a larger margin of error.

Step 1: Compute the standard error.

\[ SE = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n_{\text{current}}}} = \frac{21}{\sqrt{49}} = 3 \]

Step 2: Construct the confidence interval. For 98% confidence, \(z^* = 2.326\).

\[ \bar{x} \pm z^* SE = 310 \pm 2.326(3) = 310 \pm 6.98 = (303.02,\ 316.98) \]

Step 3: Compute the required sample size for the larger margin of error. The target margin is \(E = 6.98 + 1.5 = 8.48\).

\[ n_{\text{required}} = \left(\frac{z^*\,\sigma}{E}\right)^2 = \left(\frac{2.326 \cdot 21}{8.48}\right)^2 = \left(5.76\right)^2 \approx 33.18 \]

Step 4: Determine additional observations. Round up to \(n_{\text{required}} = 34\). The additional observations needed are \(\max(0, 34 - 49) = 0\).

Conclusion. The 98% confidence interval is \((303.02,\ 316.98)\), and no additional observations are required because the larger margin of error is already satisfied.
