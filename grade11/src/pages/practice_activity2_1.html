A regional logistics agency wants to estimate the mean time (in minutes) for cargo to clear port inspections.

Historical monitoring shows the population standard deviation is \(\sigma = 12\) minutes.

A recent pilot sample reported a mean of \(\bar{x} = 74\) minutes and a sample standard deviation of \(s = 11\) minutes.

The agency wants a 95% confidence estimate with a maximum error of \(E = 3\) minutes.

Determine the minimum sample size needed.

↑

This solution establishes the sample size so the error will not exceed a specified amount by identifying \(E\), selecting \(z^*\), substituting into the formula, and rounding up to the minimum integer sample size.

Step 1: Identify the target error and confidence level. The maximum error is \(E = 3\) minutes and the confidence level is 95%, so \(z^* = 1.96\).

Step 2: Apply the sample size formula.

\[ n = \left(\frac{z^*\,\sigma}{E}\right)^2 = \left(\frac{1.96 \cdot 12}{3}\right)^2 = \left(7.84\right)^2 = 61.47 \]

Step 3: Round up and verify Criterion 6. Because the sample size must ensure the error does not exceed \(E\), we round up: \(n = 62\). With \(n=62\), the margin of error is at most 3 minutes, satisfying Criterion 6.

A development agency wants to estimate the mean monthly food spending for households in a region.

A pilot survey suggests the population standard deviation is about \(\sigma \approx 45\) currency units.

The pilot sample produced \(\bar{x} = 510\) currency units and a sample standard deviation of \(s = 42\) currency units.

The agency wants a 90% confidence estimate with a target interval width of \(W = 16\) currency units.

The width is defined as \(W = \text{Upper} - \text{Lower} = 2E\).

Determine the minimum sample size needed.

↑

This solution establishes the sample size so the interval width will not exceed a specified amount by converting \(W\) to \(E\), selecting the correct \(z^*\), substituting into the formula, and rounding up to the minimum safe integer.

Step 1: Identify the target width and confidence level. The target width is \(W = 16\), so \(E = W/2 = 8\). The confidence level is 90%, so \(z^* = 1.645\).

Step 2: Apply the sample size formula.

\[ n = \left(\frac{z^*\,\sigma}{E}\right)^2 = \left(\frac{1.645 \cdot 45}{8}\right)^2 = \left(9.253\right)^2 \approx 85.62 \]

Step 3: Round up and verify Criterion 6. We must round up to keep the width within \(W\), so \(n = 86\). This minimum sample size guarantees the interval width is no more than 16 currency units.

A shipping cooperative wants to estimate the mean fuel cost per container on a trade route.

Company records indicate the population standard deviation is \(\sigma = 18\) dollars.

A sample of \(n = 64\) containers has a mean fuel cost of \(\bar{x} = 250\) dollars.

Construct exactly two confidence intervals for the mean fuel cost: one at 90% confidence and one at 95% confidence.

↑

This solution builds two confidence intervals for the mean fuel cost using the known population standard deviation.

Step 1: Compute the standard error.

\[ SE = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} = \frac{18}{\sqrt{64}} = 2.25 \]

Step 2: Construct the 90% confidence interval. Use \(z^* = 1.645\).

\[ \bar{x} \pm z^* SE = 250 \pm 1.645(2.25) = 250 \pm 3.70 = (246.30,\ 253.70) \]

Interpretation (90%). We are 90% confident the mean fuel cost per container lies between 246.30 and 253.70 dollars.

Step 3: Construct the 95% confidence interval. Use \(z^* = 1.96\).

\[ \bar{x} \pm z^* SE = 250 \pm 1.96(2.25) = 250 \pm 4.41 = (245.59,\ 254.41) \]

Interpretation (95%). We are 95% confident the mean fuel cost per container lies between 245.59 and 254.41 dollars.

A trade corridor study tracks the mean daily transit time (in minutes) for cross-border trucks.

The population standard deviation is known to be \(\sigma = 12\) minutes.

A random sample of \(n = 100\) trucks has a mean transit time of \(\bar{x} = 68\) minutes.

Construct exactly three confidence intervals for the mean transit time at 90%, 95%, and 98% confidence.

↑

This solution constructs three confidence intervals for the mean transit time using known variance.

Step 1: Compute the standard error.

\[ SE = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} = \frac{12}{\sqrt{100}} = 1.2 \]

Step 2: Construct the 90% confidence interval. Use \(z^* = 1.645\).

\[ \bar{x} \pm z^* SE = 68 \pm 1.645(1.2) = 68 \pm 1.97 = (66.03,\ 69.97) \]

Interpretation (90%). We are 90% confident the mean transit time lies between 66.03 and 69.97 minutes.

Step 3: Construct the 95% confidence interval. Use \(z^* = 1.96\).

\[ \bar{x} \pm z^* SE = 68 \pm 1.96(1.2) = 68 \pm 2.35 = (65.65,\ 70.35) \]

Interpretation (95%). We are 95% confident the mean transit time lies between 65.65 and 70.35 minutes.

Step 4: Construct the 98% confidence interval. Use \(z^* = 2.326\).

\[ \bar{x} \pm z^* SE = 68 \pm 2.326(1.2) = 68 \pm 2.79 = (65.21,\ 70.79) \]

Interpretation (98%). We are 98% confident the mean transit time lies between 65.21 and 70.79 minutes.

Confidence interval comparisons: same vs. distinct populations

Same population vs. distinct populations. Two samples from the same population are meant to estimate one common mean. Two distinct but related populations (such as two branches of the same agency) have different means, so we estimate each mean separately.

Why compare intervals? Comparing confidence intervals helps us see whether the plausible ranges for the means are far apart or close together. Overlap is not a formal hypothesis test, but it gives a quick, visual sense of similarity or difference.

What changes the width? Larger sample sizes make \(SE = \sigma/\sqrt{n}\) smaller, so intervals get narrower. Higher confidence levels use larger \(z^*\) values, which make intervals wider.

Known \(\sigma\) means z-based intervals. Because the population standard deviation is known, we use \(z^*\) and the standard error formula above to build each confidence interval.

Two ports in the same corridor run matched inspection procedures but serve different cargo mixes. The samples are related by context but come from two distinct populations.

Port East has \(\bar{x}_E = 42\) minutes, \(\sigma_E = 5\) minutes, and \(n_E = 49\).

Port West has \(\bar{x}_W = 39\) minutes, \(\sigma_W = 6\) minutes, and \(n_W = 64\).

Construct a 95% confidence interval for each port’s mean inspection time and write a comparison conclusion.

↑

Port East.

\[ SE_E = \frac{\sigma_E}{\sqrt{n_E}} = \frac{5}{\sqrt{49}} = 0.71 \]

\[ \bar{x}_E \pm z^* SE_E = 42 \pm 1.96(0.71) = 42 \pm 1.40 = (40.60,\ 43.40) \]

Port West.

\[ SE_W = \frac{\sigma_W}{\sqrt{n_W}} = \frac{6}{\sqrt{64}} = 0.75 \]

\[ \bar{x}_W \pm z^* SE_W = 39 \pm 1.96(0.75) = 39 \pm 1.47 = (37.53,\ 40.47) \]

Conclusion. The 95% confidence intervals are \((40.60,\ 43.40)\) minutes for Port East and \((37.53,\ 40.47)\) minutes for Port West. The intervals do not overlap, suggesting Port East’s mean inspection time is higher, while noting that interval comparison is not a formal test.

A logistics agency takes two independent samples from the same population of warehouse handling times.

Sample A has \(\bar{x}_A = 58\) minutes, \(n_A = 36\), and known \(\sigma = 10\) minutes.

Sample B has \(\bar{x}_B = 56\) minutes, \(n_B = 64\), and the same known \(\sigma = 10\) minutes.

Construct a 95% confidence interval for each sample mean and compare the precision and consistency of the estimates.

↑

Sample A.

\[ SE_A = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n_A}} = \frac{10}{\sqrt{36}} = 1.67 \]

\[ \bar{x}_A \pm z^* SE_A = 58 \pm 1.96(1.67) = 58 \pm 3.27 = (54.73,\ 61.27) \]

Sample B.

\[ SE_B = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n_B}} = \frac{10}{\sqrt{64}} = 1.25 \]

\[ \bar{x}_B \pm z^* SE_B = 56 \pm 1.96(1.25) = 56 \pm 2.45 = (53.55,\ 58.45) \]

Conclusion. Sample B’s interval is narrower because it has the larger sample size, so it is more precise. The intervals overlap, which is consistent with both samples coming from the same population mean.

Two matched job-training programs serve similar applicants but operate in different regions, so they are related yet distinct populations.

Program A has \(\bar{x}_A = 72\) points, \(\sigma_A = 8\) points, and \(n_A = 50\).

Program B has \(\bar{x}_B = 68\) points, \(\sigma_B = 7\) points, and \(n_B = 50\).

Construct confidence intervals for each program at exactly two confidence levels: 90% and 95%. Compare how the interval widths and conclusions change.

↑

Compute standard errors.

\[ SE_A = \frac{\sigma_A}{\sqrt{n_A}} = \frac{8}{\sqrt{50}} \approx 1.13 \]

\[ SE_B = \frac{\sigma_B}{\sqrt{n_B}} = \frac{7}{\sqrt{50}} \approx 0.99 \]

90% confidence intervals. Use \(z^* = 1.645\).

\[ \bar{x}_A \pm z^* SE_A = 72 \pm 1.645(1.13) = 72 \pm 1.86 = (70.14,\ 73.86) \]

\[ \bar{x}_B \pm z^* SE_B = 68 \pm 1.645(0.99) = 68 \pm 1.63 = (66.37,\ 69.63) \]

95% confidence intervals. Use \(z^* = 1.96\).

\[ \bar{x}_A \pm z^* SE_A = 72 \pm 1.96(1.13) = 72 \pm 2.22 = (69.78,\ 74.22) \]

\[ \bar{x}_B \pm z^* SE_B = 68 \pm 1.96(0.99) = 68 \pm 1.94 = (66.06,\ 69.94) \]

Conclusion. The 95% intervals are wider than the 90% intervals because the confidence level is higher. Both confidence levels show Program A’s mean is higher than Program B’s, with more cautious (wider) ranges at 95% confidence.
