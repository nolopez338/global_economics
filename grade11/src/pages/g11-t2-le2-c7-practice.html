A policy team surveys 200 households across a region to estimate the share that approve a proposed renewable energy subsidy. In the sample, 128 households say they approve.

Construct and interpret a 90% confidence interval for the population proportion of households that approve the subsidy.

↑

To construct the confidence interval for a proportion, we identify \(x\) and \(n\) and compute \(\hat{p}\), check normal approximation conditions \(n\hat{p} \ge 10\) and \(n(1-\hat{p}) \ge 10\), choose the correct \(z^*\) for the confidence level, compute \(SE\) and the margin of error, construct and report the confidence interval, and interpret the interval in context.

Step 1: Sample proportion.

\[ \hat{p} = \frac{x}{n} = \frac{128}{200} = 0.64 \]

Step 2: Large-sample conditions.

\[ n\hat{p} = 200(0.64) = 128 \ge 10, \qquad n(1-\hat{p}) = 200(0.36) = 72 \ge 10 \]

Step 3: Standard error.

\[ SE = \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}} = \sqrt{\frac{0.64(0.36)}{200}} \approx 0.0339 \]

Step 4: Margin of error (90% confidence, \(z^*=1.645\)).

\[ E = z^* \cdot SE = 1.645(0.0339) \approx 0.0558 \]

Step 5: Confidence interval.

\[ \hat{p} \pm E = 0.64 \pm 0.0558 \Rightarrow [0.584,\ 0.696] \]

Interpretation: We are 90% confident that between about 58.4% and 69.6% of all households in the region approve the renewable energy subsidy.

An export council reviews 150 shipments and finds that 39 were delayed at least one day. The council wants a proportion estimate for the delay rate in the current trade season.

Construct and interpret a 95% confidence interval for the population proportion of delayed shipments.

↑

Step 1: Sample proportion.

\[ \hat{p} = \frac{x}{n} = \frac{39}{150} = 0.26 \]

Step 2: Large-sample conditions.

\[ n\hat{p} = 150(0.26) = 39 \ge 10, \qquad n(1-\hat{p}) = 150(0.74) = 111 \ge 10 \]

Step 3: Standard error.

\[ SE = \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}} = \sqrt{\frac{0.26(0.74)}{150}} \approx 0.0358 \]

Step 4: Margin of error (95% confidence, \(z^*=1.96\)).

\[ E = z^* \cdot SE = 1.96(0.0358) \approx 0.0702 \]

Step 5: Confidence interval.

\[ \hat{p} \pm E = 0.26 \pm 0.0702 \Rightarrow [0.190,\ 0.330] \]

Interpretation: We are 95% confident that between about 19.0% and 33.0% of all export shipments are delayed at least one day this season.

A metropolitan transit authority wants to estimate the proportion of regular commuters who use a mobile ticketing app. It randomly surveys 260 commuters, and 143 commuters report using the app.

Construct and interpret a 95% confidence interval for the population proportion of commuters who use mobile ticketing.

↑

Step 1: Compute the sample proportion.

\[ \hat{p} = \frac{x}{n} = \frac{143}{260} = 0.55 \]

Step 2: Compute the standard error.

\[ SE = \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}} = \sqrt{\frac{0.55(0.45)}{260}} \approx 0.0308 \]

Step 3: Compute the margin of error (95% confidence, \(z^*=1.96\)).

\[ E = z^* \cdot SE = 1.96(0.0308) \approx 0.0604 \]

Step 4: Construct the confidence interval.

\[ \hat{p} \pm E = 0.55 \pm 0.0604 \Rightarrow [0.490,\ 0.610] \]

Step 5: Interpretation in context. We are 95% confident that between about 49.0% and 61.0% of all regular commuters use the mobile ticketing app.

↑
  1. Interpret the confidence interval in policy context: what does the interval suggest about citywide mobile ticket adoption?
  2. How would the interval change if the authority reported a 99% confidence level instead of 95%, and why?
  3. If planners need a margin of error no larger than 0.04, discuss whether this survey is precise enough and what could be changed.
  4. Suppose the authority expected at least 65% adoption. Does this interval support that expectation? Explain.

An industry chamber wants to estimate the proportion of registered small firms that completed a cybersecurity training module this year. It samples 180 firms, and 66 firms completed the module.

Construct and interpret a 90% confidence interval for the population proportion of firms that completed the training.

↑

Step 1: Compute the sample proportion.

\[ \hat{p} = \frac{x}{n} = \frac{66}{180} \approx 0.3667 \]

Step 2: Compute the standard error.

\[ SE = \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}} = \sqrt{\frac{0.3667(0.6333)}{180}} \approx 0.0359 \]

Step 3: Compute the margin of error (90% confidence, \(z^*=1.645\)).

\[ E = z^* \cdot SE = 1.645(0.0359) \approx 0.0591 \]

Step 4: Construct the confidence interval.

\[ \hat{p} \pm E = 0.3667 \pm 0.0591 \Rightarrow [0.308,\ 0.426] \]

Step 5: Interpretation in context. We are 90% confident that between about 30.8% and 42.6% of all registered small firms completed the cybersecurity module.

↑
  1. Explain in context what it means to be 90% confident in this interval estimate.
  2. How would using a 95% confidence level affect the interval width and the policy interpretation?
  3. Discuss one way to reduce the margin of error without lowering the confidence level.
  4. The chamber claims that at least half of firms completed the module. Does this interval support that claim? Why or why not?

A rural development office studies enrollment in a crop-insurance program. In a random sample of 420 farmers, 170 farmers are currently enrolled. The office has stated that enrollment is no more than 35%.

Construct and interpret a 98% confidence interval for the population proportion of farmers enrolled, and use the interval to evaluate the office's statement.

↑

Step 1: Compute the sample proportion.

\[ \hat{p} = \frac{x}{n} = \frac{170}{420} \approx 0.4048 \]

Step 2: Compute the standard error.

\[ SE = \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}} = \sqrt{\frac{0.4048(0.5952)}{420}} \approx 0.0240 \]

Step 3: Compute the margin of error (98% confidence, \(z^*=2.326\)).

\[ E = z^* \cdot SE = 2.326(0.0240) \approx 0.0558 \]

Step 4: Construct the confidence interval.

\[ \hat{p} \pm E = 0.4048 \pm 0.0558 \Rightarrow [0.349,\ 0.461] \]

Step 5: Interpretation in context. We are 98% confident that between about 34.9% and 46.1% of all farmers in the region are enrolled in crop insurance. Because 0.35 lies inside the interval, the office statement of no more than 35% is still plausible at the 98% confidence level.

↑
  1. Interpret what the interval says about likely enrollment rates across the full farmer population.
  2. Why does a 98% confidence level produce a wider interval than a 90% interval from the same sample?
  3. If policymakers need stronger evidence against the 35% claim, what sample-design changes could improve precision?
  4. Does the confidence interval give clear evidence that enrollment exceeds 35%? Justify your conclusion using interval logic.

A maritime regulator audits 500 port operations records and finds 92 records with full on-time incident reporting. Last year, a separate audit found 80 compliant records out of 480.

Construct and interpret a 99% confidence interval for this year's population proportion of on-time reporting compliance, and compare the interval with last year's observed proportion.

↑

Step 1: Compute the sample proportion.

\[ \hat{p} = \frac{x}{n} = \frac{92}{500} = 0.184 \]

Step 2: Compute the standard error.

\[ SE = \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}} = \sqrt{\frac{0.184(0.816)}{500}} \approx 0.0173 \]

Step 3: Compute the margin of error (99% confidence, \(z^*=2.576\)).

\[ E = z^* \cdot SE = 2.576(0.0173) \approx 0.0446 \]

Step 4: Construct the confidence interval.

\[ \hat{p} \pm E = 0.184 \pm 0.0446 \Rightarrow [0.139,\ 0.229] \]

Step 5: Interpretation in context. We are 99% confident that between about 13.9% and 22.9% of all port operations records are fully compliant with on-time incident reporting this year. Last year's observed rate was \(\hat{p}_{\text{last}}=\frac{80}{480}=0.167\), which lies inside this interval, so there is no clear evidence of a change at the 99% confidence level.

↑
  1. Interpret this 99% confidence interval in terms of likely compliance across all operations.
  2. How does selecting 99% confidence affect certainty and margin of error compared with 95% confidence?
  3. If the regulator wants a narrower interval next year while keeping 99% confidence, what should it change?
  4. Use the interval to compare this year with last year's observed compliance proportion and state a justified conclusion.
